### Differential Effects on Motor Proteins

Single-molecule studies show that dynein tends to reverse direction whereas kinesin tends to detach at patches of bound tau, with kinesin being inhibited at approximately 10-fold lower tau concentration than dynein. This differential sensitivity is crucial for directional regulation.

Tau reduces the processivity of kinesin and dynein by acting as an obstacle on the microtubule, with single-molecule assays indicating that kinesin-1 is more strongly inhibited than kinesin-2 or dynein, suggesting tau might act to spatially modulate the activity of specific motors.

### Creating Directional Bias

Studies using isolated endogenous cargoes with a complement of kinesin-1, kinesin-2, and dynein motors found that tau directs bidirectional cargoes towards the microtubule minus end by tuning the balance of forces exerted by kinesin and dynein teams.

Isolated phagosomes containing motor complexes of kinesin-1, kinesin-2, and dynein are regulated by tau, biasing cargo movement preferentially toward the minus ends of microtubules.

### Spatial Regulation Through Tau Gradients

In a healthy neuron, tau can be distributed in a proximal-to-distal gradient, where lower tau concentration at the cell body allows kinesin to efficiently bind to microtubules and initiate anterograde transport of cargo, whereas higher tau concentration at the synapse facilitates cargo release, while dynein-driven retrograde transport from the distal axon is not impeded due to dynein's lower sensitivity to tau.

### Cargo-Specific Regulation

Different cargoes respond differently to tau based on their motor complement:

Early endosomes are more sensitive to tau than lysosomes and are inhibited by both wild-type and phosphomimetic Y18E tau, while lysosome motility is strongly inhibited by tau, with Y18E tau preferentially inhibiting lysosomes in the cell periphery while centrally located lysosomes are less affected.

### Effects on Transport Parameters

Elevated levels of tau inhibit intracellular transport in neurons, particularly the plus-end-directed transport by kinesin motors from the center of the cell body to the neuronal processes. Tau overexpression alters the run length, but not the velocity, of cargos in both directions, preferentially inhibiting anterograde transport resulting in a shift in net movement of cargo toward the cell body.

## Mechanism of Action

Inhibition of microtubule motor activity occurs by direct competition of tau proteins with dynein and kinesin for microtubule binding, with the microtubule-binding domain of tau being sufficient to inhibit motor activity.

## Pathological Implications

In Alzheimer's disease, tau accumulates in the somatodendritic compartment, and according to this model, kinesin-driven anterograde transport of new material to the axon terminal would be severely compromised, leading to neurodegeneration.

## Summary

Rather than being a motor protein itself, tau acts as a **spatial and selective regulator of bidirectional transport** by:

- Creating physical obstacles that differentially affect kinesin (strongly inhibited) versus dynein (less affected)
- Establishing directional bias through its gradient distribution in axons
- Fine-tuning the balance between anterograde and retrograde transport
- Regulating cargo-specific transport based on motor protein composition

This makes tau a critical regulatory node in axonal transport, with its mislocalization or hyperphosphorylation in disease states causing catastrophic disruption of directional cargo delivery.
