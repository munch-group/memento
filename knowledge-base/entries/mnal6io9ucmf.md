# **Architectural Proteins, Chromatin Looping, and Hybrid Incompatibility**

**Introduction**

Chromatin looping is a fundamental aspect of genome organization that enables regulatory DNA elements (like enhancers, silencers, and insulators) to interact with their target genes over long distances. This 3D genome architecture is orchestrated by architectural proteins such as CTCF and the cohesin complex (whose core subunits include RAD21 and SMC3). These factors shape chromosomes into loops and domains, insulating certain regions while bringing others into contact. Changes in this looping architecture – for example, due to chromosomal rearrangements (like inversions) or divergence in regulatory sequences between populations – can disrupt normal gene regulation. Such disruptions are increasingly recognized as potential causes of hybrid incompatibility (the sterility or inviability of offspring from divergent populations). In this report, we first outline the general mechanisms of chromatin loop formation by CTCF and cohesin. We then explain how alterations in looping architecture, especially in the germline, could lead to reproductive isolation. Finally, we discuss known examples where changes in chromatin architecture or the loop-extrusion machinery have been linked to hybrid sterility or inviability, with detailed mechanistic insights supported by peer-reviewed literature.

**Chromatin Looping and Architectural Proteins (CTCF, RAD21, SMC3)**

CTCF and Loop Anchors: CTCF is a DNA-binding insulator protein that defines the boundaries of topologically associating domains (TADs) and anchors chromatin loops. Genome-wide mapping shows CTCF, often together with cohesin, enriched at TAD borders and loop anchor points . CTCF binding sites are directional, and loops preferentially form between CTCF sites in a convergent orientation. According to the widely supported loop extrusion model, the ring-shaped cohesin complex extrudes loops of DNA until it encounters a CTCF site oriented as a barrier, thereby demarcating the loop’s boundary . In this way, CTCF acts as a loop-stopping barrier (and a stabilizing anchor) that restricts interactions between DNA elements in different loops or domains . If CTCF is removed, TAD insulation is lost and domain boundaries collapse, underscoring CTCF’s key role in loop organization .

Cohesin Complex (RAD21/SMC3) and Loop Extrusion: The cohesin complex is the primary mediator of loop extrusion in interphase chromatin. Cohesin is a ring-shaped heteromer composed of SMC1 and SMC3 (structural maintenance of chromosomes proteins) forming a V-shaped ATPase “clamp,” with RAD21 (a kleisin protein) locking the ring closed . Using energy from ATP, cohesin can grab two sections of DNA and progressively pull or extrude them through the ring in opposite directions, enlarging a loop of chromatin. This dynamic extrusion continues until cohesin is stalled by boundary factors like CTCF or is actively unloaded from DNA. Rapid depletion experiments illustrate these roles: acute removal of RAD21 or other cohesin subunits causes pre-formed loops to “spring open” and disappear, proving that cohesin’s presence is required to maintain loop domains . Likewise, without CTCF, cohesin is no longer constrained to defined domain borders, leading to a loss of insulation between adjacent domains . In summary: SMC3 (paired with SMC1) provides the motor function for loop extrusion, while RAD21 secures the cohesin ring and interacts with loading factors, ensuring cohesin is properly placed on chromatin. These architectural proteins work in concert to shape the genome into loops and TADs, thereby controlling which genes and regulatory elements can communicate in the nucleus.

Mechanistic highlights:

- Loop Extrusion: Cohesin (SMC1/SMC3+RAD21) extrudes DNA into loops until a barrier is reached . This creates self-interacting domains (TADs) with enriched contacts inside and insulated edges.
- CTCF Barriers: Convergent CTCF sites at loop ends serve as stoppers and anchors, often pausing cohesin and stabilizing the loop . Cohesin can reside at CTCF-bound sites, tethering loop bases.
- Dynamic Regulation: Cohesin loading and unloading is regulated by factors like NIPBL (loader) and WAPL (unloader). For example, removing WAPL causes cohesin to remain on DNA longer and extrude larger loops (“vermicelli” chromatin) . Post-translational modifications (e.g. SMC3 acetylation) also modulate cohesin’s loop-extrusion activity . Thus, cells can tune loop length and stability, which is crucial during development and in different cell types.

Overall, CTCF, RAD21, and SMC3 are central to organizing the 3D genome. They ensure that genes have the proper regulatory contacts in any given cell. Next, we explore what happens when this architecture is altered by genomic changes or evolutionary divergence, and how such alterations can lead to incompatibilities in hybrids.

**Changes in Chromatin Loop Architecture and Hybrid Incompatibility**

Genomic architecture is generally conserved, but even modest changes can have outsized effects on gene regulation and phenotype. Structural variants like chromosomal inversions or lineage-specific mutations in insulators/enhancers can rewire chromatin loops. These changes may be benign within a pure species’ genomic context (which has co-evolved compensatory features), but in a hybrid genome (carrying one chromosome from each lineage) the mismatched architectures can create dysfunction. Several scenarios illustrate how divergence in looping architecture can contribute to reproductive isolation:

- Misaligned Loop Domains: If one species has an inversion (flipping a segment of chromosome), the orientation of CTCF sites within that segment is reversed. Loop anchors that were convergent may no longer align properly. In a hybrid cell containing both arrangements, the two parental chromosomes will fold differently. Enhancers and promoters might be in contact on one chromosome (where loops are intact) but separated on the homolog (where the inversion disrupts the loop). This allele-specific misfolding can produce misexpression: essential genes may be silenced on one allele or aberrantly activated on the other. Developmental genes are especially sensitive to such dosage and timing discrepancies, potentially leading to inviability or sterility in hybrids.
- Ectopic Enhancer–Promoter Contacts: Divergence in regulatory DNA (such as the gain or loss of a boundary element) can alter loop insulation. For instance, deletion of a CTCF site or other boundary in one lineage may cause a formerly insulated enhancer to begin contacting a wrong target gene. Studies have shown that inserting or deleting TAD boundaries can create ectopic enhancer–promoter interactions, resulting in abnormal gene expression and developmental defects . In a hybrid, one allele may permit an inappropriate regulatory crosstalk while the other allele does not, leading to dominant-negative effects or haploinsufficiency. If the genes affected are crucial for viability or fertility, this regulatory incompatibility can manifest as hybrid inviability or sterility.
- Chromosomal Pairing and Segregation Issues: Large-scale architectural differences impede proper chromosome pairing in meiosis. Chromosomal inversions are a well-known driver of hybrid sterility because an inversion heterozygote must form a looped structure to align homologous regions during meiosis I. If a crossover occurs within the inverted segment, it can generate duplications and deletions in gametes. These unbalanced gametes either die or produce inviable offspring, reducing hybrid fertility . The more inversions (or other rearrangements) that differentiate two genomes, the more meiotic mishaps occur, lowering hybrid fitness. Such structural differences are often observed between closely related species and are thought to contribute directly to reproductive isolation . Notably, chromosome fusions or number mismatches (as in horse–donkey hybrids, where different karyotypes yield sterile mules) similarly exemplify how higher-order architecture differences cause meiotic arrest or aneuploidy.
- Altered Nuclear Organization: Beyond individual loops, species can differ in broad nuclear architecture (e.g. distribution of heterochromatin vs. euchromatin domains). If one lineage evolves changes in where or when certain regions attach to the nuclear lamina or how chromosomes are compartmentalized, a hybrid nucleus could experience spatial disorganization. Key processes, such as X-chromosome inactivation or meiotic sex chromosome condensation, might fail if the parental chromosomes “choose” different 3D arrangements. For example, divergence in the positioning of centromeres or telomere clustering could disrupt meiotic chromosome dynamics in hybrids, leading to asynapsis (failure to pair) or improper spindle attachments. Such defects typically manifest as hybrid sterility (due to failed gametogenesis) or inviability (embryonic death from chromosome segregation errors).

In summary, modifications to chromatin looping architecture – whether through structural genome rearrangements or sequence-level changes in regulatory sites – can create a mismatch between two genomes that were not “designed” to work together. While each population’s genome is internally coherent, the hybrid combination may suffer regulatory incompatibilities (misexpression of genes) and cytological failures (problems in chromosome pairing and segregation). Over evolutionary time, these incompatibilities act as postzygotic barriers, reinforcing reproductive isolation between diverging lineages.

**Divergence in Germline Chromatin Structure and Reproductive Isolation**

The effects discussed above are especially pronounced in the germline, where the stakes for reproductive success are highest. Germline cells (spermatocytes and oocytes) undergo unique chromatin reorganization events – such as meiosis, extensive chromatin condensation, and gamete-specific gene expression programs. Divergence in germline chromatin architecture can therefore be a potent source of hybrid sterility. Key considerations include:

- Meiotic Chromosome Synapsis: Meiosis requires homologous chromosomes to recognize one another and synapse (pair) along their length. This homology recognition can be influenced by 3D organization – chromosomes typically align starting at special regions (e.g. pairing centers or telomeres clustering) and by sharing similar chromatin marks. If two species differ in these pairing cues or in large-scale organization (e.g. one has an extra heterochromatic block or an inversion that alters pairing geometry), hybrids often exhibit partial synapsis. Unsynapsed chromosomes trigger checkpoints that cause meiotic arrest or eliminate the germ cells. A concrete example is the Prdm9 gene in mice (discussed further below): Prdm9 controls hotspots of meiotic recombination; when Prdm9 alleles from different subspecies are combined, homologous chromosomes fail to synapse properly at prophase I, resulting in widespread asynapsis and sterility of hybrid males .
- Cohesin Dynamics in Meiosis: Cohesin (including SMC3 and RAD21, along with meiosis-specific variants) is crucial for holding sister chromatids and organizing meiotic chromosome axes. It also affects how homologs attach to each other (through the synaptonemal complex) and how they separate during anaphase. Divergence in factors that regulate cohesin’s placement or removal can cause segregation errors. The protections on cohesin differ between oogenesis and spermatogenesis; for instance, a protein called SGO2 (Shugoshin-2) normally localizes to centromeres in oocytes to protect cohesin there from premature removal. If species differ in SGO2 localization or regulation, their F1 hybrid oocytes might mis-localize this protection. In fact, a recent study on two mouse species found that hybrid F1 females were sterile due to cohesin misregulation: SGO2 spread abnormally along chromosome arms in hybrid oocytes, “over-protecting” cohesin and preventing homologs from separating at meiosis I, leading to aneuploid eggs . Such cohesin protection errors activate checkpoints or produce inviable gametes, directly causing hybrid infertility. Notably, these issues were specific to meiosis (somatic cells of the hybrid were viable), highlighting how germline chromatin regulation can be uniquely divergent without affecting mitotic processes .
- Transposable Element (TE) Activation: The germline must tightly silence transposons to maintain genome integrity. This silencing is often achieved via heterochromatin formation and piRNA pathways, which can be species-specific. If two lineages have different complements of TEs or divergent epigenetic repression mechanisms, the hybrid germline might fail to recognize and silence some repetitive elements. The result is transposon reactivation, DNA damage, and apoptotic death of gametocytes. For example, in certain Drosophila hybrids, divergent piRNA profiles cause rampant transposon mobilization (known as hybrid dysgenesis), leading to sterility. More generally, differences in chromatin architecture at pericentromeric or telomeric regions (where many TEs reside) can cause mis-regulation of these repeats in a hybrid. We will see an example in Drosophila where rapidly evolving heterochromatin-associated proteins fail to properly handle TEs in hybrids, causing lethality.
- Imprinted and Epigenetic Barriers: Germline chromatin differences can also involve parent-of-origin effects (imprints) or other epigenetic marks that need to be reset each generation. If species vary in their epigenetic reprogramming, hybrids might inherit incompatible epigenetic states. Although this is less about looping per se, it intersects with chromatin structure – for instance, improper retention or removal of DNA methylation or histone modifications in the hybrid zygote can cause misexpression of key developmental genes and inviability. Some known cases in plants (e.g. endosperm failures in interspecific crosses) and mammals (uniparental imprinting disorders in hybrids) reflect this principle.

In short, the germline is where subtle differences in chromatin architecture hit the hardest: any misregulation can result in failure to produce viable, balanced gametes. Evolutionary divergence in factors like cohesins, insulator proteins, recombination hotspot mapping, and heterochromatin content can thus create hybrid sterility even if each species on its own is perfectly fertile. These germline-specific incompatibilities are particularly insidious because they often do not manifest until an F1 hybrid attempts gametogenesis, thereby strongly isolating the gene pools.

**Case Studies: Chromatin Architecture Changes Causing Hybrid Sterility or Inviability**

Several empirical examples have now linked changes in 3D genome organization or looping proteins to hybrid incompatibilities:

**Cohesin Misregulation in Mouse Hybrid Oocytes**

A striking case was documented in crosses between two mouse species (Mus musculus domesticus and Mus spicilegus). Hybrid F1 females from this cross are sterile due to errors in meiosis I. Cytological analysis showed that homologous chromosomes in the hybrid oocytes failed to segregate properly, resulting in all eggs being aneuploid . The defect was traced to mis-localization of the cohesin protector SGO2. In normal mice, SGO2 is confined to centromeres in oocytes, protecting centromeric cohesin from separase during anaphase I. In the hybrid oocytes, however, SGO2 abnormally coated the chromosome arms (not just centromeres), leading to excess cohesin retention along chromosome arms . Consequently, when the cell attempted anaphase I, the chiasmata holding homologs could not be released (because cohesin along the arms was never cleaved), and homologous chromosomes lagged or failed to separate . Upstream, the mis-localization of SGO2 was attributed to higher activity of BUB1 kinase in the hybrid, revealing a biochemical incompatibility between species in the pathway that positions SGO2 . This example shows how divergence in cohesin-regulatory mechanisms can cause hybrid sterility. Each species evolved slight differences in how they protect cohesin in oocyte meiosis; their hybrid combines these in a way that “over-protects” cohesin, blocking proper chromosome separation. The result – sterility of the female hybrid – is directly due to a chromatin structural incompatibility in the germline (in this case, an erroneous preservation of chromatin cohesion). Importantly, this finding also suggests that factors contributing to human age-related infertility (cohesion defects in oocytes are a known cause of aneuploidy in older mothers) might arise naturally as species diverge , potentially promoting speciation by the same mechanism.

**PRDM9 Divergence and Hybrid Male Sterility in Mice**

PRDM9 is a DNA-binding histone methyltransferase that determines the locations of recombination hotspots during meiosis. It is famous as the first identified vertebrate hybrid sterility gene. Different mouse populations carry different alleles of Prdm9, which recognize distinct DNA motifs across the genome. In an F1 hybrid between certain house mouse subspecies (e.g. M. m. musculus and M. m. domesticus), the two PRDM9 versions bind to largely non-overlapping sets of hotspots, and many double-strand breaks fail to find a properly aligned partner site on the homologous chromosome. This leads to extensive regions of asynapsis (unsynapsed chromosomes) in hybrid spermatocytes . The asynaptic regions trigger a meiotic arrest (via the pachytene checkpoint), resulting in hybrid male sterility . Mechanistically, the Prdm9 incompatibility is a chromatin mapping problem: each species has evolved a unique “hotspot map” for recombination, meaning the looped chromatin structures that form during homolog pairing (facilitating crossover interactions) are arranged differently. The hybrid’s mixed hotspot landscape confuses the homolog pairing process. Elegant experiments have shown that introducing large stretches of homologous sequence or manipulating PRDM9’s binding preferences can restore synapsis and fertility to these hybrids , confirming that the issue is a misalignment of recombination-mediated chromatin loops. Thus, Prdm9-driven divergence in meiotic chromatin programming directly causes postzygotic isolation. Prdm9 evolves rapidly under positive selection (presumably due to arms-race dynamics with TEs or other factors), and this rapid evolution means that even closely related mice can have wholly incompatible recombination landscapes. This example underscores how changes in a single chromatin regulator can have a cascading effect on genome folding during meiosis, ultimately preventing gene flow between populations.

**Chromosomal Inversions and Misexpression in Hybrids**

Structural genome differences often accompany speciation, and inversions are a prime example. Inversions contribute to reproductive barriers in two main ways. First, as mentioned, they cause mechanical segregation problems and partial sterility in heterozygotes (which would include F1 hybrids) due to unbalanced gametes . Second, they lock in specific combinations of alleles by suppressing crossovers, allowing co-adapted gene complexes to diverge between species. Beyond these classical roles, recent work implicates inversions in altering local chromatin architecture. An inversion breakpoint can disrupt a TAD boundary or reposition a gene into a different nuclear neighborhood. Consider a scenario: in species A (ancestral state) an enhancer and gene are in separate loops, insulated by a CTCF-bound boundary; in species B, a small inversion removes that boundary, placing the enhancer and gene in the same loop. Both species may be fine on their own (species B might have evolved compensatory changes, like a weaker enhancer or repressive chromatin, to mitigate the new contact). However, a hybrid carrying one chromosome of each will experience a unique situation: on the species B-derived chromosome the gene is now under the influence of the enhancer (and potentially misexpressed), while on the species A-derived chromosome it remains properly insulated. This allelic imbalance in expression can produce a deleterious phenotype in the hybrid (for instance, if the gene dosage is critical, or if ectopic expression triggers a dominant negative effect). Such gene misexpression syndromes in hybrids are analogous to position-effect variegation – essentially a position-effect incompatibility. While it can be challenging to pinpoint these in non-model organisms, researchers have noted instances where hybrid misexpression correlates with regions of structural divergence. For example, in stickleback fish, a fixed inversion is associated with divergent adaptive traits and shows extensive misexpression in hybrids, suggestive of underlying 3D architectural changes (though the exact looping differences remain under investigation). Inversions can also contribute to behavioral isolation indirectly by capturing alleles that affect mating preferences, but that is beyond our scope – here we emphasize their direct effect on chromosomal architecture and hybrid gametogenesis viability.

One concrete case comes from the monkeyflower (Mimulus) genus. Lowry and Willis (2010) demonstrated that a chromosomal inversion underlies a major life-history difference between annual and perennial ecotypes of Mimulus guttatus and contributes to reproductive isolation. While much of the isolation was ecological, the inversion also caused intrinsic problems in hybrids. The inverted region showed suppressed recombination and likely contained misregulated genes in hybrids. The general principle from such studies is that closely related species often differ by multiple inversions, and these differences are sufficient to cause hybrid problems: partly by creating unbalanced meioses and partly by altering gene regulation networks . Indeed, a survey of many taxa finds that karyotypic differences (inversions, translocations, fusions) correlate with hybrid sterility more often than would be expected by chance, highlighting the role of genome architecture evolution in speciation.

**Divergence in Insulator Proteins and Heterochromatin (Drosophila)**

Hybrid incompatibilities sometimes stem from rapidly evolving chromatin-binding proteins that govern heterochromatin or insulator function. A well-documented example is the hybrid lethal interaction between the Hmr (Hybrid male rescue) and Lhr (Lethal hybrid rescue) genes in Drosophila. Drosophila melanogaster and its sister species D. simulans can produce F1 hybrid offspring that die as larvae (males, in particular, are affected) due to incompatibilities involving HMR and LHR. Molecular studies revealed that HMR and LHR are chromatin-associated proteins that localize to centromeric heterochromatin and to certain insulator sites in the genome . HMR, for instance, was found to bind many genomic insulator regions – including those flanking heterochromatin – and appears to partner with typical insulator-binding factors (like CTCF or other Drosophila insulator proteins) . In D. melanogaster, loss of HMR causes de-repression of transposable elements and mis-expression of heterochromatic genes , indicating that it normally helps maintain the proper chromatin state of repetitive regions. Importantly, HMR and LHR have evolved under positive selection and their expression levels differ between species . In hybrids, the combined dosage of HMR and LHR from two species is abnormally high (since each species’ version doesn’t perfectly replace the other’s function, the cell ends up with excess protein) . This leads to mislocalization of HMR: instead of confining to its normal targets, the HMR protein in hybrids spreads to many new sites (often co-localizing with a partner protein called GFZF on telomeric transposon repeats) . The outcome is a massive disruption of gene expression in heterochromatin – transposons get activated and centromeric repeats are mis-regulated – ultimately causing lethality of the hybrid larvae . In essence, the chromatin insulator network in the hybrid is thrown into disarray because the architectural protein HMR (and its binding partner LHR) have diverged and cannot be properly integrated. This case highlights how changes in a chromatin-organizing protein between species can result in widespread architectural incompatibility: the hybrid’s nuclei experience a meltdown of normal heterochromatin and insulator function, leading to inviability. Notably, the HMR/LHR incompatibility conforms to the Dobzhansky–Muller model (each species is fine with its own version; it’s the combination that is lethal) and emphasizes that the evolution of chromatin architecture (here, via an insulator/heterochromatin protein complex) can drive speciation. Other Drosophila factors, such as nucleoporins and histone modifiers, show similar behavior (rapid evolution and causing hybrid issues), but HMR/LHR is one of the clearest examples tying it directly to 3D genome organization.

**Other Notable Examples**

- In yeast, differences in chromosome configuration (like the number and distribution of ribosomal DNA repeats) can cause hybrid instability. Although yeast do not have CTCF, they do have condensin/cohesin complexes organizing chromosomes. Rapid evolution of centromeric regions and associated kinetochore proteins in some yeast has been shown to cause hybrids to missegregate chromosomes, analogous to the cohesion protection issues in mice. This suggests a general principle that centromere and cohesion protein divergence can contribute to speciation across eukaryotes.
- In plants, polyploidy and associated chromatin changes (like altered loop density due to more DNA content, or nucleolar organizer activity) often lead to hybrid incompatibilities. Hybrids of different ploidy parents may suffer “triploid block” or endosperm failure partly because the chromatin from each parent dictates different developmental programs that cannot be reconciled in the hybrid seed. While often discussed in terms of gene dosage imbalance, an emerging view is that the 3D arrangement of paternal vs. maternal genomes in the hybrid endosperm nucleus is crucial – if they don’t interface correctly (due to differing heterochromatin content or loop domains), developmental gene expression goes awry and the hybrid seed aborts.
- The phenomenon of meiotic drive (where certain loci manipulate segregation in their favor) can also impose indirect selection on chromatin structure. For example, if a driver causes asymmetric spindle attachment, suppressors might evolve that alter centromere or heterochromatin configuration. These differences may not cause sterility within species (because suppressors restore fairness), but when two populations with different drive systems hybridize, the delicate balance is upset, potentially leading to failed meiosis. This has been proposed for some cases of cryptic drive systems contributing to hybrid sterility (e.g. in mice and Drosophila).

**Conclusion**

In conclusion, the architectural proteins CTCF, RAD21, and SMC3 are central to folding the genome into loops and domains, thereby ensuring proper gene regulation. CTCF provides positional cues and stop-signals for cohesin-mediated loop extrusion, while SMC3 and RAD21 (cohesin components) actively create and stabilize chromatin loops . Together, they establish the 3D genomic landscape that cells require for normal function. When this landscape is restructured – by inversion mutations, by gain/loss of insulator elements, or by evolutionary changes in chromatin-binding proteins – the result can be misfolded chromatin that misregulates genes. In hybrids between diverging populations, such architectural mismatches are unmasked: each parent contributes chromosomes that fold differently, and the hybrid’s cellular machinery cannot harmonize these differences. The outcome can be aberrant gene expression (e.g. an essential gene falling into a wrong loop domain and silenced) or catastrophic meiotic errors (chromosomes failing to pair or segregate). Empirical studies across taxa, from mammals to insects and plants, now implicate changes in chromatin architecture as important drivers of hybrid sterility and inviability. For instance, we saw that divergence in cohesin regulation can prevent proper chromosome separation in hybrid oocytes , divergence in recombination hotspot maps can halt spermatogenesis in hybrid males , and divergence in a chromatin insulator complex can unleash transposons and kill hybrid embryos . These examples illustrate the mechanistic breadth of “the 3D genome” as a substrate for reproductive isolation.

Looking forward, integrating high-resolution chromosome conformation capture (Hi-C) data for species and their hybrids, as well as functional tests like boundary deletions , will deepen our understanding of how loop-level differences translate into hybrid dysfunction. It is becoming clear that speciation is not only a story of DNA sequence divergence, but also one of chromatin folding divergence. As each lineage tweaks the loops and coils of its genome, it may eventually reach a point where an amalgamation with another lineage’s genome can no longer produce a coordinated, fertile organism. In this way, changes in chromatin architecture – sculpted by proteins like CTCF, RAD21, and SMC3 – are indeed agents of speciation, creating the very loops that keep species separate.

**Is there any mechanism where diverging loop extrusion could explain cis BDMI across several megabases**

Yes. Diverging loop extrusion dynamics can plausibly explain cis-acting Bateson-Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibilities (BDMIs) that span several megabases, under mechanisms involving:

1. disruption of topologically associating domains (TADs),
2. ectopic enhancer–promoter interactions,
3. insulation loss from differential loop anchors, and
4. chromatin accessibility/compartmentalization shifts.

These phenomena are increasingly supported by empirical evidence, especially from high-resolution chromatin conformation capture (Hi-C) and TAD-deletion studies.

**1.**

**Mechanistic Basis: Loop Extrusion as a Driver of Long-Range Regulatory Architecture**

Loop extrusion by cohesin (RAD21/SMC3) is known to organize chromatin into insulated domains on the order of 0.1–2 Mb, but long-range effects beyond this scale arise from nested looping, higher-order compartmentalization, and cross-talk between distal regulatory domains. Divergence in loop extrusion parameters — such as cohesin residence time, loading/unloading balance, or CTCF site orientation — can lead to altered folding at the megabase scale. If two lineages evolve different loop domain structures over a shared syntenic region, the 3D regulatory architecture of that region may become incompatible in hybrids, even in the absence of coding changes.

Key points:

- TAD boundaries can be multigenic, encompassing 10–30 genes across 1–5 Mb [Zhang et al., 2023, Nat Genet](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-023-01391-z).
- Enhancers can contact genes over multiple TADs, especially in development [Lupianez et al., 2015, Cell](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2015.03.038).
- Species-specific gain/loss of boundary elements (CTCF motifs) can rewire enhancer–promoter maps over several megabases [Krefting et al., 2018, Genome Biol Evol](https://doi.org/10.1093/gbe/evy099).

Thus, cis-acting BDMIs — where a hybrid carries only one parental haplotype across a multi-Mb region — may reflect incompatibilities in loop architecture that alter expression of multiple co-regulated genes, consistent with a “regulatory TAD module”.

**2.**

**Loop Architecture Divergence Creates cis BDMIs via Misexpression**

Lupiáñez et al. (2015) experimentally demonstrated that deleting TAD boundaries in mice (removing just a CTCF site) caused distal enhancers to ectopically activate proto-oncogenes over >2 Mb, producing limb malformations. In a cis hybrid context, such changes could be unmasked if:

- One parental haplotype has altered CTCF motif orientation, disrupting a loop domain;
- The homologous region retains the ancestral boundary;
- The hybrid thus exhibits allelic imbalance in enhancer targeting, leading to misexpression of dosage-sensitive or developmental regulators.

This matches the BDMI model: each haplotype is compatible in its own genomic context but becomes deleterious in combination.

Example hypothesis:

A regulatory hub in species A spans genes A1–A15 across 3 Mb within a looped TAD. In species B, a new TAD boundary evolves between A5 and A6 due to a CTCF site gain. Enhancers upstream of A1 no longer target A10–A15. In the hybrid, the species B haplotype fails to activate genes A10–A15 appropriately, causing partial loss of function in essential developmental pathways. This leads to a hybrid defect — a cis-acting incompatibility with no coding change.

**3.**

**Empirical Support: Diverging TADs and cis Regulatory Incompatibilities**

Recent comparative Hi-C studies provide evidence for interspecies divergence in loop extrusion landscapes:

- Krefting et al. (2018) compared TAD boundaries between humans and mice and found that only ~50–60% of boundaries are conserved, and many species-specific boundaries are under positive selection.
- Lemaitre et al. (2021) reported that boundary shifts in primates are associated with expression changes of genes several megabases downstream [eLife](https://elifesciences.org/articles/64620).

Such differences can result in large regulatory “real estate” reorganization, potentially establishing multi-Mb incompatibility domains.

In Drosophila, cis-BDMIs have been mapped to megabase-sized haplotypes with no obvious coding divergence, suggestive of non-coding regulatory mismatches [Satyaki et al., 2014, PLoS Genet](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1004610).

**4.**

**Relevance to Hybrid Incompatibilities**

Diverging loop extrusion profiles can cause:

- Loss of insulation → enhancers gain access to inappropriate targets;
- Loop rewiring → tissue-specific genes misregulated during development;
- Compartment switching (A↔B) → chromatin accessibility changes and epigenetic incompatibilities.

These can create:

- Cis-BDMIs spanning multiple megabases, as regulatory misexpression affects gene modules;
- Parent-of-origin-specific expression failure if imprinting boundaries are disrupted;
- Partial penetrance phenotypes, consistent with empirical BDMI data.

**Conclusion**

Yes, diverging loop extrusion architecture between lineages can cause cis-acting BDMIs over several megabases by altering enhancer–promoter communication, loop insulation, and chromatin accessibility. These incompatibilities reflect the emergence of lineage-specific regulatory domains that are incompatible in hybrid genomes, especially if one haplotype carries boundary shifts, inversions, or enhancer losses. Current studies using CRISPR boundary deletions, comparative Hi-C, and hybrid misexpression mapping provide mechanistic support for this hypothesis.
