## MAPT and Tubulin Post-Translational Modifications

MAPT (Microtubule-Associated Protein Tau) encodes the tau protein, which interacts with tubulin to stabilize microtubules and promote tubulin assembly into microtubules. This interaction is profoundly influenced by various post-translational modifications (PTMs) on both tau and tubulin.

### Key Tubulin PTMs Affecting Tau-Microtubule Interactions

**1. Polyglutamylation**

This is one of the most critical tubulin PTMs affecting tau binding. Polyglutamylation at tubulin C-termini differentially regulates the interaction of microtubules with MAPs, increasing negative charge at tubulin C-terminal tails.

Research demonstrates that polyglutamylation generated by TTLL1 and TTLL7 specifically modulates tau's affinity for microtubules, with both α-tubulin and β-tubulin polyglutamylation additively affecting tau binding. Importantly, Tuba4a lacking C-terminal polyglutamylation prevents the binding of tau and GSK3 kinase to neuronal microtubules, thereby strongly reducing phospho-tau levels.

**2. Acetylation**

Acetylation is primarily found on K40 of α-tubulins, which is exposed in the lumen of the microtubule, with αTAT1 and Mec17 being the major enzymes responsible for acetylation, and HDAC6 and SIRT2 being the enzymes involved in deacetylation. Acetylation can have both a direct inhibitory effect on tau function and an indirect activation effect by preventing phosphorylation in the KXGS motifs of the MTBR.

**3. Detyrosination/Tyrosination**

In neurons, two MT regions are distinguished: a labile MT region mostly composed of tyrosinated and deacetylated GTP-tubulin, and a stable MT region mostly consisting of assembled detyrosinated and acetylated GDP-tubulin.

### Genes Interacting with MAPT in the Context of Tubulin PTMs

**Kinases that Phosphorylate Tau:**

1. **GSK3B (Glycogen Synthase Kinase-3β)**
    - GSK3B phosphorylates tau at serine and threonine residues, causing detachment from microtubules and their disassembly
    - Tau mutants resistant to phosphorylation by GSK3B retained substantial toxicity and had increased affinity for microtubules
    - Loss of Tuba4a C-terminal polyglutamylation prevents the binding of both tau and GSK3 kinase to neuronal microtubules
2. **CDK5 (Cyclin-Dependent Kinase 5)**
    - CDK5 and GSK3B are the two main protein kinases that have an important role in the abnormal hyperphosphorylation of MAPT which leads to Alzheimer's disease
    - Cyclin dependent kinase 5 (cdk5) phosphorylates tau along with other kinases including GSK3β, CK1, PKA, ERKs 1/2, PKC, CaMKII, tau-tubulin kinases 1 and 2, and MARKs
3. **MARK Kinases (MARK1-4)**
    - Phosphorylation at serine residues in K-X-G-S motifs by MAP/microtubule affinity-regulating kinase (MARK1, MARK2, MARK3 or MARK4) causes detachment from microtubules and their disassembly
    - MARK2-mediated tau phosphorylation led to the dissociation of tau from HDAC6 and reduced tau and tubulin acetylation
4. **FYN, SYK, and ABL Kinases**
    - Tau can be phosphorylated by tyrosine kinases such as the SRC family members (LCK, SYK, and FYN) and the ABL family members (ARG and ABL1), with FYN, SYK, and ABL kinases phosphorylating tau at the Y18, Y197, and Y394 residues

**Tubulin-Modifying Enzymes:**

1. **TTLL Family (Tubulin Tyrosine Ligase-Like enzymes)**
    - **TTLL1**: TTLL1 is one of the two predominant neuronal polyglutamylases responsible for polyglutamylation, primarily modifying α-tubulin
    - **TTLL7**: TTLL7 is the other predominant neuronal polyglutamylase, primarily modifying β-tubulin
    - TTLL3, TTLL8, and TTLL10 represent polyglycylases whereas the specific function of TTLL12 is presently unknown
2. **CCP Family (Cytosolic Carboxypeptidases - Deglutamylases)**
    - **CCP1 and CCP6**: CCPs are tubulin-modifying enzymes that remove acidic amino acid residues, reversing polyglutamylation catalyzed by TTLL family enzymes
    - CCP5 was identified as a functional mammalian tubulin deglutamylase enzyme
3. **HDAC6 (Histone Deacetylase 6)**
    - HDAC6 is a tau deacetylase that protects against tau accumulation, with acetylation of tau inhibiting its function and promoting aggregation
    - HDAC6 is one of the enzymes involved in tubulin deacetylation
4. **ATAT1/MEC17 (α-Tubulin Acetyltransferase)**
    - αTAT1 and Mec17 are the major enzymes responsible for acetylation of α-tubulin at lysine-40
5. **SIRT2 (Sirtuin 2)**
    - SIRT2 is involved in tubulin deacetylation along with HDAC6
6. **TTL (Tubulin Tyrosine Ligase)**
    - Tubulin Tyrosine Ligase (TTL) is the enzyme responsible for tyrosination of tubulin dimers
7. **TUBA4A (Tubulin Alpha-4a)**
    - Tuba4a is a major tubulin isotype highly expressed in the postnatal nervous system and carries the longest polyglutamyl side chains, making it unique within the family of tubulins

This complex interplay between tau phosphorylation and tubulin PTMs creates a sophisticated regulatory system where tau's binding affinity for microtubules is modulated by both its own post-translational modifications and those on the tubulin substrate.

## How Tau Regulates Selective Directional Transport

### Differential Effects on Motor Proteins

Single-molecule studies show that dynein tends to reverse direction whereas kinesin tends to detach at patches of bound tau, with kinesin being inhibited at approximately 10-fold lower tau concentration than dynein. This differential sensitivity is crucial for directional regulation.

Tau reduces the processivity of kinesin and dynein by acting as an obstacle on the microtubule, with single-molecule assays indicating that kinesin-1 is more strongly inhibited than kinesin-2 or dynein, suggesting tau might act to spatially modulate the activity of specific motors.

### Creating Directional Bias

Studies using isolated endogenous cargoes with a complement of kinesin-1, kinesin-2, and dynein motors found that tau directs bidirectional cargoes towards the microtubule minus end by tuning the balance of forces exerted by kinesin and dynein teams.

Isolated phagosomes containing motor complexes of kinesin-1, kinesin-2, and dynein are regulated by tau, biasing cargo movement preferentially toward the minus ends of microtubules.

### Spatial Regulation Through Tau Gradients

In a healthy neuron, tau can be distributed in a proximal-to-distal gradient, where lower tau concentration at the cell body allows kinesin to efficiently bind to microtubules and initiate anterograde transport of cargo, whereas higher tau concentration at the synapse facilitates cargo release, while dynein-driven retrograde transport from the distal axon is not impeded due to dynein's lower sensitivity to tau.

### Cargo-Specific Regulation

Different cargoes respond differently to tau based on their motor complement:

Early endosomes are more sensitive to tau than lysosomes and are inhibited by both wild-type and phosphomimetic Y18E tau, while lysosome motility is strongly inhibited by tau, with Y18E tau preferentially inhibiting lysosomes in the cell periphery while centrally located lysosomes are less affected.

### Effects on Transport Parameters

Elevated levels of tau inhibit intracellular transport in neurons, particularly the plus-end-directed transport by kinesin motors from the center of the cell body to the neuronal processes. Tau overexpression alters the run length, but not the velocity, of cargos in both directions, preferentially inhibiting anterograde transport resulting in a shift in net movement of cargo toward the cell body.

## Mechanism of Action

Inhibition of microtubule motor activity occurs by direct competition of tau proteins with dynein and kinesin for microtubule binding, with the microtubule-binding domain of tau being sufficient to inhibit motor activity.

## Pathological Implications

In Alzheimer's disease, tau accumulates in the somatodendritic compartment, and according to this model, kinesin-driven anterograde transport of new material to the axon terminal would be severely compromised, leading to neurodegeneration.

## Summary

Rather than being a motor protein itself, tau acts as a **spatial and selective regulator of bidirectional transport** by:

- Creating physical obstacles that differentially affect kinesin (strongly inhibited) versus dynein (less affected)
- Establishing directional bias through its gradient distribution in axons
- Fine-tuning the balance between anterograde and retrograde transport
- Regulating cargo-specific transport based on motor protein composition

This makes tau a critical regulatory node in axonal transport, with its mislocalization or hyperphosphorylation in disease states causing catastrophic disruption of directional cargo delivery.

# **X-Linked Genes in Tubulin Modification and Microtubule Dynamics during Spermatogenesis**

## **Flagellar Assembly and Tubulin Modifications**

Several X-linked genes play crucial roles in sperm flagellum formation and stability, often indirectly affecting tubulin post-translational modifications (PTMs) by ensuring proper axonemal structure. **CFAP47** (Cilia and Flagella Associated Protein 47) encodes a core component of the sperm flagellar central apparatus. CFAP47 forms part of the “bridge” linking the two central microtubules (C1 and C2) of the axoneme . Cryo-electron tomography shows CFAP47 binds one central microtubule (C1) via its N-terminus and anchors to the other (C2) via its C-terminus, in coordination with HYDIN . This tethering is essential for maintaining the 9+2 axonemal architecture and thus for sperm motility – *Cfap47*-knockout mice exhibit disrupted central microtubule connections and markedly reduced sperm motility . Clinically, hemizygous CFAP47 mutations in men cause multiple morphological abnormalities of the sperm flagella (MMAF) and asthenozoospermia  . Although CFAP47 is not an enzyme, its structural role is critical for **stabilizing axonemal microtubules** and likely for the retention of tubulin PTMs (like *α*-tubulin acetylation and polyglutamylation) that normally enrich stable doublet microtubules in the flagellum. Indeed, immunostaining confirms CFAP47 localizes to the base of the flagellum and co-distributes with acetylated tubulin in sperm . Loss of CFAP47 destabilizes the axoneme, which would secondarily impair tubulin PTMs that depend on an intact structure.

Another flagellar gene is **DYNLT3** (Dynein light chain Tctex-type 3). DYNLT3 is a component of cytoplasmic dynein-2, the motor driving retrograde intraflagellar transport (IFT) in cilia/flagella  . In mammals, dynein-2 light chains including DYNLT3 are expressed in testes and required for building the sperm tail . While *Dynlt3* has not been individually knocked out in a sperm context, it belongs to the same light chain family as TCTEX1D2 (Dynlt2b), whose loss in mice causes severe flagellar malformation . DYNLT3 likely overlaps with its paralog DYNLT1 in function . **By linking dynein motors to cargo**, DYNLT3 may help transport tubulin-modifying enzymes or building blocks along the manchette and axoneme during spermiogenesis. For example, tubulin polyglutamylases (TTLL enzymes) and deacetylases must be shuttled into the growing flagellum via IFT; a functional dynein-2 complex (with DYNLT3 as a subunit) is required for this process  . Furthermore, in somatic cells DYNLT3 directly binds the spindle checkpoint protein Bub3 , reflecting its role in targeting dynein to kinetochore-associated cargos. In Chlamydomonas cilia, light chains including Tctex-type proteins are known to aid assembly and stability of axonemal structures . Thus, by enabling proper IFT and dynein-driven transport, DYNLT3 indirectly supports the **enrichment of stabilizing tubulin PTMs** (like luminal acetylation and glutamylation) in the flagellum that are crucial for motility.

Phosphoinositide metabolism can also impact ciliary tubulin modifications. The **OCRL** gene (inositol polyphosphate-5-phosphatase) is X-linked and better known for Lowe syndrome, but it localizes to the base of primary cilia and flagella. Loss of OCRL disrupts ciliary trafficking and results in abnormally **short or disorganized cilia** with defective axonemes . In *ocrl1* morphants (zebrafish), acetylated tubulin staining of ciliary axonemes is disorganized and cilia are shortened . This suggests OCRL normally helps recruit membrane or structural components for cilium/flagellum elongation. In spermatids, OCRL may facilitate vesicle trafficking to the developing flagellum; its absence could indirectly alter microtubule stability or the positioning of tubulin-modifying enzymes. (Notably, a homologous 5-phosphatase INPP5B partially compensates for OCRL in cilia formation , which might explain why Lowe syndrome patients do not invariably suffer sperm motility defects).

Planar cell polarity (PCP) proteins also intersect with flagellar biology. **PRICKLE3**, an X-linked member of the Prickle PCP family, is testis-expressed and though less characterized than Prickle1/2, it likely influences cytoskeletal dynamics. Prickle proteins localize to cell cortex and can recruit regulators of actin and microtubules. Indeed, studies on **Prickle1** (with which Prickle3 shares homology) indicate these proteins are involved in **microtubule and actin dynamics** . In the testis, PCP components help orient cells and structures; Prickle1 regulates Sertoli cell cytoskeleton and junctions via recruiting multiple regulatory proteins . By analogy, Prickle3 might contribute to organizing microtubule-based structures during spermiogenesis – for example, ensuring the proper planar orientation of the sperm tail or the polarized transport of vesicles. Although direct links between Prickle3 and tubulin PTMs are not reported, PCP pathways often modulate *axonemal* ciliary function in other systems. Thus, Prickle3 could indirectly affect the **flagellar stability** by influencing microtubule organization or by recruiting scaffolds that localize tubulin-modifying enzymes to the flagellum.

In summary, **none of the listed X-linked genes encodes a dedicated tubulin-modifying enzyme** (such as tubulin acetyltransferases or polyglutamylases). However, several have **indirect roles in regulating tubulin PTMs** by organizing or stabilizing the microtubule structures crucial for spermatogenesis:

- **Structural proteins of the flagellum (CFAP47)**: stabilize axonemal microtubules (central pair), thereby maintaining the environment for PTMs that support motility . Mutations cause flagellar defects and male infertility.
- **Dynein motor components (DYNLT3)**: recruit dynein to microtubules in spindles and flagella, facilitating cargo transport, spindle checkpoint silencing, and IFT . Through these, DYNLT3 ensures proper microtubule attachments and likely influences the distribution of stable tubulin along the manchette and axoneme.
- **Microtubule-associated regulatory proteins (DIAPH2, Emerin, Prickle3)**: these modulate microtubule stability and organization. DIAPH2 binds and stabilizes microtubules (important for spindle and possibly manchette integrity) . Emerin binds spindle microtubules and centrosomes, and its loss causes misorganized tubulin networks . Prickle3 and its paralogs connect signaling to cytoskeletal remodeling, potentially affecting microtubule behavior in elongating spermatids .
- **Gene expression regulators (DDX3X, HUWE1, RBM41)**: these ensure the timely production or degradation of proteins that act on microtubules. DDX3X is vital for translation of cell-cycle and differentiation regulators in germ cells . HUWE1-mediated proteolysis likely clears histones and other proteins, indirectly aiding the transition to protamines and possibly the removal of manchette tubulin at the end of spermiogenesis . While these do not touch tubulin directly, they set the stage for proper microtubule function by maintaining proteostasis.
- **Testis-specific but non-essential factors (SCML1, VSIG1, etc.)**: these are expressed during spermatogenesis and might have minor or redundant roles. SCML1 marks a unique meiotic organelle but is not required for fertility . VSIG1 is a junction protein with no effect when knocked out . Their contribution to microtubule-dependent processes is likely minimal or compensated by other proteins.

In conclusion, among the X-linked genes of interest, **CFAP47 and DYNLT3 stand out as directly impacting microtubule structures in spermatogenesis** – CFAP47 by physically crosslinking and stabilizing axonemal tubulin, and DYNLT3 by enabling dynein-driven transport and spindle stability. Others like **DIAPH2, Emerin, and Prickle3** serve supportive roles in microtubule capture, stabilization, or organization, which can indirectly influence the **post-translational modification landscape of tubulin** (since stable microtubules accrue PTMs such as acetylation, and conversely PTMs can further stabilize microtubules). The remaining genes are either involved in upstream regulatory pathways or have subtle effects that current models do not clearly link to microtubule modifications. Ongoing studies – e.g., high-resolution imaging of spermatid structures and gene knockouts in mice – continue to refine our understanding. As of now, the evidence emphasizes that **microtubule-dependent processes during spermatogenesis rely on a network of structural and regulatory X-linked proteins** working in concert with autosomal factors, rather than on any single X-linked enzymatic modifier of tubulin.
