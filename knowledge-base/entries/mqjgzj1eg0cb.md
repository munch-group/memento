## 1. Scope and summary

Spermiogenesis ends in one of the most extreme chromatin-remodelling events in biology: the near-complete replacement of the nucleosomal, histone-based genome by a protamine-based structure, producing the hyper-condensed sperm nucleus. This report summarises that compaction programme and then examines a specific, much narrower question — the role of the polyglycylation of nucleosome assembly protein 1 (NAP1) by the tubulin-tyrosine-ligase-like enzyme TTLL10.

The honest headline on the second point: the TTLL10–NAP1 link rests essentially on a single biochemical identification ([Ikegami 2008][ikegami2008]) plus the authors' own cautious speculation about its function. NAP1 is a well-characterised histone chaperone, and TTLL10 is a well-characterised polyglycine elongase, so a connection between a histone chaperone modification and the histone-to-protamine transition is mechanistically attractive — but it has not been functionally demonstrated, and the human-specific catalytic death of TTLL10 ([Rogowski 2009][rogowski2009]) makes the human relevance an open question rather than an established fact.

## 2. Chromatin compaction during spermiogenesis

In mammals, haploid round spermatids differentiate into motile spermatozoa during spermiogenesis, and the bulk of the core histones are replaced sequentially — first by transition proteins and then by protamines — to drive chromatin hyper-compaction ([Bao & Bedford 2016][bao2016]). The process is usually described as a stepwise programme.

First, testis-specific histone variants (among them TH2A, TH2B, H2A.L.2, and the spermatid linker-histone variants H1t, H1T2 and HILS1) are incorporated into nucleosomes, weakening histone–DNA contacts and destabilising the nucleosome relative to its canonical, somatic counterpart ([Bao & Bedford 2016][bao2016]). Second, a wave of histone post-translational modification — most prominently hyperacetylation of histone H4 — reduces the positive charge of the histone tails, loosens the histone–DNA interaction, and increases nucleosomal fluidity, priming the nucleosome for disassembly ([Bao & Bedford 2016][bao2016]). Bromodomain "reader" proteins, including the testis-specific BRDT, recognise these hyperacetylated tails and couple acetylation to histone eviction (well-established background; cited here only at the level the review supports — that acetylation-dependent reader recruitment drives the transition — [Bao & Bedford 2016][bao2016]).

Third, the destabilised histones are displaced by the small, basic transition proteins (TNP1 and TNP2), which are in turn replaced by the even more basic, arginine-rich protamines (PRM1 and PRM2); the protamine-bound DNA collapses into tightly packed toroids, yielding the condensed sperm nucleus ([Bao & Bedford 2016][bao2016]). The replacement is not total: a minor fraction of the genome — commonly reported on the order of ten to fifteen percent in humans — escapes protamine packaging and is retained as nucleosomes at particular loci, a feature implicated in the paternal contribution to the early embryo ([Bao & Bedford 2016][bao2016]).

Because protamine deposition requires the DNA to be transiently relaxed, the genome passes through a vulnerable intermediate in which torsional stress is relieved by transient DNA strand breaks (associated with topoisomerase TOP2B activity) that must be resolved before the mature, compacted state is reached ([Bao & Bedford 2016][bao2016]). This is the structural backdrop against which any cytoplasmic histone-handling machinery — including NAP1 — would have to operate.

## 3. NAP1 as a histone chaperone

Nucleosome assembly protein 1 (NAP1) is a conserved member of the NAP family of histone chaperones. Its defining activities are escorting the H2A–H2B histone dimer, mediating the nucleocytoplasmic shuttling and nuclear import of H2A–H2B, and participating in nucleosome assembly and disassembly ([Mosammaparast 2002][mosammaparast2002]). Structural work on NAP1 established it as a histone chaperone whose dimeric, acidic surface shields the basic, DNA-binding faces of histones during transit, and linked it mechanistically to the karyopherin Kap114 that imports H2A–H2B into the nucleus ([Park & Luger 2006][parkluger2006]). More recent structural analysis of the Nap1·H2A–H2B·Kap114 assembly shows that NAP1 and Kap114 act as co-chaperones for H2A–H2B and can facilitate targeted release of the histone cargo inside the nucleus, with NAP1 itself being predominantly cytoplasmic at steady state while shuttling between compartments ([Fung 2025][fung2025]).

Two features of this profile matter for spermiogenesis. NAP1 is a chaperone that moves histones between cytoplasm and nucleus and that can both deposit and remove H2A–H2B; and it is largely cytoplasmic at steady state. A cell engaged in the wholesale eviction of histones — and in shuttling the displaced histones out of a shrinking nucleus — is exactly the context in which a cytoplasmic H2A–H2B chaperone might be expected to be busy. This is the logical bridge to the TTLL10 observation, though it remains a logical bridge rather than a demonstrated pathway.

## 4. The TTLL10–NAP1 polyglycylation interaction

### 4.1 Polyglycylation and the TTLL enzymes

Polyglycylation is the post-translational addition of glycine side chains, singly (monoglycylation) or as chains (polyglycylation), to glutamate residues — classically in the C-terminal tails of axonemal tubulin. In mammals the modification is written by three tubulin-tyrosine-ligase-like enzymes: TTLL3 and TTLL8 act as initiases that add the first, branch-point glycine, while TTLL10 is the elongase that extends the glycine chain ([Rogowski 2009][rogowski2009]). TTLL10's elongase character is reflected in its enzymology: it requires a pre-existing monoglycine primer for high-affinity microtubule binding and elongates only from such branch points, is stimulated by tubulin glutamylation, and becomes self-limiting as the chains it builds grow ([Cummings 2024][cummings2024]). Consistent with its dependence on a primer, TTLL10 glycylates tubulin efficiently only when co-expressed with an initiase such as TTLL8 ([Ikegami 2009][ikegami2009]).

### 4.2 NAP1 as a non-tubulin substrate of TTLL10

The connection to chromatin comes from the original identification of TTLL10's glycylase activity on a non-tubulin substrate. Using an antibody against poly(glycine), a cytoplasmic ~60-kDa protein that accumulates in elongating spermatids was detected, purified, and identified by tandem mass spectrometry as nucleosome assembly protein 1 (NAP1); recombinant TTLL10 incorporated glycine into recombinant NAP1 in vitro, and mutational mapping placed the modification at the C-terminal glutamate pair Glu359/Glu360 ([Ikegami 2008][ikegami2008]). This is the finding that made NAP1 the first reported non-tubulin polyglycylation substrate and that anchored TTLL10 to the spermatid, rather than purely to the axoneme. Notably, the modified residues lie in NAP1's acidic C-terminal region — the same charge-rich domain implicated in its histone-shielding chaperone activity — so glycylation there is at least positioned to alter NAP1's interaction surface.

### 4.3 The proposed function — and how thin the evidence is

The functional interpretation offered for this modification was explicitly tentative. Because NAP1 accumulates in elongating spermatids precisely as the nucleus is compacting through the histone-to-protamine transition, the authors suggested that glycylation of NAP1 *could* contribute to the transcriptional repression and chromatin reorganisation that accompany spermiogenesis ([Ikegami 2008][ikegami2008]). That is the extent of the direct evidence: an identification of the modification and its site, plus a hypothesis. There is, to my knowledge, no published demonstration that polyglycylation alters NAP1's chaperone activity, its histone-binding or shuttling behaviour, or the kinetics or completeness of histone replacement — nor a loss-of-function experiment isolating the NAP1-glycylation arm of TTLL10 from its tubulin arm. Any stronger statement of a functional role would go beyond what the literature supports.

### 4.4 The human-specific complication

A further consideration sharpens the question rather than answering it. Human TTLL10 is transcribed but catalytically inactivated by amino-acid substitutions in its core domain, a loss that appears specific to the human lineage among the species examined ([Rogowski 2009][rogowski2009]). Since TTLL10 is the elongase, its inactivation means that polyglycine-*chain* elongation of NAP1 — the activity demonstrated in the mouse system — would not occur in modern humans. Whether NAP1 in human spermatids is nonetheless mono-glycylated by the surviving initiases (TTLL3/TTLL8), and whether the loss of chain elongation on NAP1 has any consequence for human sperm chromatin, are open questions that the existing data do not resolve. This places the NAP1–TTLL10 axis in the same evolutionarily interesting but mechanistically unsettled position as TTLL10's better-known role on the sperm axoneme.

## 5. Synthesis and open questions

The compaction programme itself is well established: variant incorporation and H4 hyperacetylation destabilise nucleosomes, reader proteins couple those marks to eviction, transition proteins bridge to protamines, transient DNA breaks accommodate the topological strain, and the genome ends up largely protamine-toroidal with a small retained nucleosomal fraction ([Bao & Bedford 2016][bao2016]). NAP1's role as an H2A–H2B chaperone and nucleocytoplasmic shuttle is equally well established ([Mosammaparast 2002][mosammaparast2002]; [Park & Luger 2006][parkluger2006]; [Fung 2025][fung2025]). What is *not* established is the bridge between them: that TTLL10-dependent polyglycylation of NAP1 modulates histone handling during the transition.

The most informative experiments would be the ones the field has not yet reported: testing whether glycylation at NAP1 Glu359/360 changes its H2A–H2B binding, deposition/eviction balance, or subcellular distribution; determining the glycylation state of NAP1 across spermatid stages in a TTLL10-active species and in human (where elongation is absent); and a separation-of-function approach that perturbs TTLL10's NAP1 activity independently of its tubulin activity. Until then, the NAP1–TTLL10 interaction is best described as a mechanistically plausible, evolutionarily intriguing, but functionally unproven node in the chromatin-compaction programme.
