## From Foundations to Futures: Building Equity in Genetics

**Presenter:** Jazlyn Mooney, *University Of Southern California*

**Abstract:** Modern advances in biology, medicine, and statistics (particularly at their intersection with genetics and computation) have transformed our understanding of human variation and disease. However, these disciplines are also rooted in 19th-century eugenic ideologies that shaped foundational data, analytical frameworks, and public policies in ways that continue to influence scientific and societal practices today. In this talk, I examine the entangled roots of these scientific fields with eugenics and trace how those legacies persist in contemporary research. I then draw on recent collaborative work in which we re-evaluate data sources, assumptions, and analytic choices with the aim of helping dismantle structural inequities embedded in scientific knowledge. By revisiting the past, I highlight how the scientific community can better confront bias, improve representation, and promote equity in genetic and evolutionary studies.

## Science Wise: Career mentorship through a storytelling interview podcast

**Presenter:** Emilia Huerta-Sánchez, *Brown University*

**Abstract:** Abundant evidence supports that science is more innovative when performed by teams with diverse lived experiences and technical expertises, highlighting the importance that all motivated early career scientists are supported and encouraged. We present Science Wise, a podcast to support early career scientists to thrive by amplifying the unspoken truths, struggles, and triumphs of relatable experienced scientists. Through storytelling, we offer practical career guidance and preserve collective wisdom across generations—so that listeners not only see what’s possible, but hear from those who helped make it so. Each episode of Science Wise shares the real-life journey of a scientist who has walked a path others now follow. Listeners gain access to career-shaping insights, honest personal stories, and practical strategies to help them navigate their own scientific careers—while honoring the legacy of those who came before. With the support of SMBE IDEA funding, we released a second season of Science Wise, featuring SMBE’s own Dr Anne Stone. We additionally released an episode where experienced scientists responded to listener questions. We discuss some of the key lessons we’ve learned through extended interviews with 15 inspiring scientists, as well as the potential of podcasts to reach broader scientific audiences and beyond.

## Can evolutionary studies on social traits promote 21st-century eugenics?

**Presenter:** Mehmet Somel, *Middle East Technical University*

**Abstract:** A growing number of studies are using genome-wide association studies (GWAS) to investigate genetic correlations with social traits, including educational attainment, income, and socioeconomic class. More recently, several population genetics studies have used these GWAS variants to infer selection on such social traits, while others have used social trait-related polygenic risk scores (PGS) to explain modern-day social hierarchies through natural selection. Here, we review methodological and social dimensions of this body of work. We first discuss technical issues involved, including the limits to controlling for confounding effects, the predominance of indirect effects, the biologically uninterpretable nature of the studied phenotypes, and ignorance of social science models that explain the emergence of social inequalities. We further show examples of how genetic and evolutionary studies on social traits are used to promote essentialist worldviews and appear to serve 21st-century eugenics and racism. We suggest that genetics and evolutionary studies on social traits have strong social impacts and deserve ethical scrutiny.

## Navigating Tribal Collaboration in Environmental and Genomic Research in California

**Presenter:** Alexandra Sasha Nikolaeva, *UC Berkeley*

**Abstract:** Collecting genetic data in California carries a responsibility to the Indigenous Peoples who have stewarded these lands for millennia. Traditional Ecological Knowledge is increasingly influencing land-management strategies; for example, California’s 2024 SB 310 recognizes tribal sovereignty over cultural burning and waives standard state burn-permit requirements.  Yet when research occurs on lands co-managed by federal or state agencies and Tribal partners, the only legally required authorization for sample collection is often the federal or state permit. Memorandums of Understanding for genetic research are uncommon, and many Tribes in California do not yet have established processes for approving permits for genetic data collection. <br /><br />At UC Berkeley, collaboration with Tribal Nations can be circuitous because different research activities, such as collecting materials, accessing or using data, setting up formal collaborations, or providing honoraria are handled by different campus offices. As a result, responsibilities are not always clearly outlined in research agreements, necessitating additional time, relationship-building, and a better understanding of what researchers can offer Tribal partners beyond requests for access. <br /><br />Currently, at least ~40,000 acres in California are under documented Tribal stewardship or co-management through state agreements, with additional acreage under federal co-stewardship. This talk will outline practical, ethical, and administrative considerations for collaborating with Indigenous partners and strategies for supporting Tribal sovereignty and mutual benefit in environmental and genomic research. I will draw on experiences from the Coast Redwood Genome Project and discuss what actually motivates Tribal communities to engage with researchers, based on conversations with my Tribal collaborators.

## From Specimen to Sequence: International Researcher Perspectives on Indigenous Data Sovereignty in Biodiversity Genomics

**Presenter:** Jocelyn Chee Santiago, *Arizona State University*

**Abstract:** Global biodiversity genomics initiatives are rapidly expanding large-scale sequencing and open data infrastructures to support evolutionary research, conservation, and comparative genomics. These efforts rely on extensive specimen collection and cross-institutional data sharing, often involving samples obtained from Indigenous territories. This raises questions about consent, governance, benefit-sharing, and Indigenous Data Sovereignty (IDSov), which affirms Global Indigenous Peoples’ rights to control the collection, use, and stewardship of data derived from their lands and resources. Despite increasing discussions of IDSov, little empirical work has examined how biodiversity and evolutionary genomics researchers interpret and apply these principles in practice.<br /><br />We conducted an anonymized, international online survey (24 close-ended questions) to assess researcher perspectives and decision-making across the genomics data lifecycle. The survey captured subdiscipline, career stage, institution type, and country of research, and randomly assigned respondents to one of three hypothetical vignettes focused on (1) specimen collection, (2) genome sequencing and data generation, or (3) reuse of publicly available sequence data. Vignette-based questions evaluated how respondents approach governance, permissions, and data-sharing decisions under realistic research conditions.<br /> <br />Preliminary results suggest that governance considerations are unevenly applied across project stages, with greatest attention at specimen collection but substantially less during downstream sequencing and secondary data reuse. Responses also highlight geographic and institutional asymmetries between sites of specimen collection and genomic analysis, and limited institutional guidance on IDSov-aligned data governance.<br /><br />We discuss implications for evolutionary genomics workflows and data reuse practices, and opportunities to integrate IDSov-informed governance through collaboration with Indigenous scholars and community partners.<br />

## EvoSur: Building an interconnected network for evolutionay biology in South America

**Presenter:** Paco Majic, *Embl Heidelberg*

**Abstract:** Evolutionary and molecular biology are rapidly advancing disciplines, yet South America remains underrepresented in global research networks due to persistent structural inequities. The ongoing brain drain of talented young scientists, a form of professional extractivism, undermines the development of a consolidated regional research community, weakening scientific infrastructure and limiting local training at both national and continental scales. Linguistic barriers between Portuguese- and Spanish-speaking communities further fragment collaboration, a problem exacerbated by limited funding for research, conference participation, and international training opportunities. Addressing these challenges requires building strong, sustainable connections among early-career researchers working within the region.<br />To meet this need, we created EvoSur, a regional initiative designed to promote equitable access to training, mentorship and collaboration networks among evolutionary biologists in South America. The first edition of EvoSur took place in Uruguay in November 2025, bringing together 25 students from all ten South American countries for an intensive week-long workshop. With nearly 200 applications for just 25 positions, the inaugural edition demonstrated substantial regional demand for accessible, high-quality training and networking opportunities.<br />In this talk, we will share outcomes and lessons from EvoSur’s inaugural edition drawing on participant and faculty feedback, and highlighting the process of community building, networking and training. We discuss how regionally rooted, multilingual training programs can strengthen scientific capacity, foster durable research networks, and counteract brain drain. EvoSur provides a transferable model for advancing inclusion, diversity, equity, and access within global evolutionary and molecular biology.

## Mapping JEDI Challenges in the Earth BioGenome Project


**Presenter:** Marcela Uliano Da Silva, *Wellcome Sanger Institute*

**Abstract:** The Earth BioGenome Project (EBP) is a global “network-of-networks” aiming to generate high-quality reference genomes for ~1.8 million named eukaryotes. Achieving this mission requires more than technical capacity; it depends on inclusive governance, equitable partnerships, fair attribution, and meaningful participation across geographies, institutions, and knowledge systems. In this talk, we present EBP’s Justice, Equity, Diversity, and Inclusion (JEDI) Committee’s efforts to date across its mandate in advisory, educational, and advocacy roles. We begin presenting the perspective of a forthcoming manuscript developed in response to feedback from the EBP community. Because the EBP is decentralized, JEDI issues are expected to manifest differently across regions and affiliated projects; meaningful action therefore requires first understanding how these challenges arise within biodiversity genomics across the globe. The manuscript maps ten cross-cutting themes—power, funding, capacity development, knowledge transfer, network expansion, epistemologies, community engagement, attribution, outreach, and compliance—and highlights the challenges they bring to participation, decision-making, recognition, and benefit-sharing in practice. Rather than proposing solutions, this work aims to build a common language and shared awareness that can support context-appropriate approaches across affiliated initiatives. We’ll also summarize activities undertaken to date, including workshops, training and outreach, and contributions to EBP governance to embed JEDI across the project’s structures. Recognizing the scale of the EBP and the persistence of global inequalities, we conclude by inviting symposium participants to engage in dialogue on practical strategies for advancing JEDI in biodiversity genomics, and to identify approaches that are effective, transferable, and responsive to the field’s most pressing challenges.

## Biomedical and life science articles by female researchers spend longer under review

**Presenter:** David Alvarez-Ponce, *University of Nevada, Reno*

**Abstract:** Women are underrepresented in academia—especially in STEMM fields, at top institutions, and in senior positions. This is due, at least in part, to the many obstacles that they face compared to their male counterparts. There has been substantial debate as to whether the peer review system is biased against women. Some analyses—mostly based on thousands of Economics research articles—have shown that manuscripts authored by women experience longer peer review times (defined as the time intervened from submission to acceptance) than comparable manuscripts authored by men. Other studies, however, have found no effect of author’s gender on acceptance delays, raising questions about whether the gender gap is specific to certain fields. We will present analyses of two separate sets of articles. By analyzing all articles indexed in the PubMed database (>36.5 million articles published in >36,000 biomedical and life sciences journals), we show that the median amount of time spent under review is 7.4–14.6% longer for female-authored articles than for male-authored articles, and that differences remain significant after controlling for several factors. The gender gap affects most disciplines, regardless of how well women are represented in each discipline; however, it is absent or even reversed in some disciplines (including Biology). By analyzing >49,000 research articles published in 11 Evolutionary Biology journals, we show that, in some of the journals, female-authored articles spent significantly longer under review; however, most of the observed differences vanish after controlling for the date of publication and the number of authors.

## Project Psyche: A case study of how to embed inclusivity within large-scale genomic projects

**Presenter:** Charlotte Wright, *Wellcome Sanger Institute*

**Abstract:** In an era of large-scale genome sequencing projects, high-quality genomes are being rapidly produced across eukaryotic diversity. Expanding genomic resources are opening new frontiers for evolutionary biology, but also present a challenge. Genomic analysis often requires computational power and technical expertise - both of which are unevenly distributed across the globe. This limits inclusion and prevents the potential of genomics to be realised by the global community. To address this challenge, the generation of genomes needs to be partnered with initiatives to build genomics capacity and reduce disparities in access to infrastructure, training, and funding.<br /><br />Here, we present Project Psyche, a collaborative genomics project that puts inclusivity first. Our community consists of over 150 researchers, conservation practitioners, and industry experts from over 34 countries. Together, we are generating and analysing chromosome-level genomes of all 11,000 species of butterflies and moths in Europe. Currently, we are analysing over 1,000 publicly available genomes as an open-community effort. By sharing the computational load and centrally hosting the dataset to share analytic products, we aim to increase participation. We also organise training workshops, research exchanges, joint sampling trips and in-person meetings across Europe to increase involvement. These activities are funded by a European Cooperation in Science and Technology Action ‘10kLepGenomes’ and we prioritise funding for early-career researchers, researchers from genders underrepresented in the field, and researchers from European Widening Countries. Together, we have built an inclusive consortium that is developing genomics capacity across Europe and beyond, and offers a model for other initiatives.
