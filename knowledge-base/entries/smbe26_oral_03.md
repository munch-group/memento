## Plant domestication under clonality and traditional management: perspectives from cassava collections

**Presenter:** Logan Kistler, *Smithsonian Institution*

**Abstract:** The evolution of plant domestication and global transition to agricultural food production were the most transformative processes in human history. Genetic and archaeobotanical research has focused disproportionately on annual seed crops.  However, many major economic crops are clonal species— potatoes, yams, sweet potatoes, sugarcane, and others—and we know much less about how clonal cultivation in human crop fields shapes domestication, population genomics, and genome evolution. Here I present an ancient and historic collections-based genomic survey of cassava (manioc, yuca) in the Americas, probing how traditional management and clonal reproduction have shaped the life history and population genomics in a vegetatively cultivated crop species. We also integrate crop diversity and interviews with traditional farmers in Brazil’s Xingu region to better understand traditional strategies for managing and sustaining crop biodiversity.<br />

## ANCIENT PLANT DNA DOCUMENTS CEREMONIAL CONTINUITY AT PICURIS PUEBLO

**Presenter:** Thomaz Pinotti, *University Of Copenhagen*

**Abstract:** Plants play a key role in ceremonial practices of Indigenous peoples of the Americas. In the U.S. Southwest, these relationships have persisted despite four centuries of colonial disruption, yet direct evidence for the long-term continuity of ceremonial plant use is rare. Scientific approaches, when in partnership with Indigenous communities, provide one pathway to document this knowledge while respecting its living ritual significance. In this study initiated by Picuris Pueblo, a federally-recognized Native American tribe from New Mexico, we generated ancient environmental DNA data from soil taken from the floor of an underground ceremonial chamber – known in Picuris as thètáne and in English by the Hopi word kiva – used between 1611 and 2016 CE. Across sixteen stratigraphic layers, we identify a stable assemblage of domesticated plants central to Pueblo subsistence and ceremony, such as maize, beans, pumpkin, sunflower, goosefoot and amaranths. We also detect plants with primarily ceremonial roles, such as coyote tobacco and hallucinogenic sacred datura, as well as taxa associated with construction and maintenance of the kiva, such as reed and oak. Strikingly, we find no evidence of European-introduced crops, such as wheat or barley, despite their early historical adoption at Picuris, suggesting a deliberate exclusion from ceremonial contexts. Genome-scale data from maize and its edible fungal pathogen (corn smut) demonstrate 2,000 years of genetic continuity between archaeological and historical Picuris lineages. Together, these results reveal enduring Indigenous plant use and highlight the power of collaborative ancient DNA research.

## Following the process of sunflower domestication through space and time with ancient DNA

**Presenter:** Benjamin Blackman, *University Of California, Berkeley*

**Abstract:** Where and how many times crop plants were domesticated, how quickly genetic diversity is lost during domestication, and how domestication syndromes are assembled through polygenic evolutionary change are questions of active research and frequent debate. We are addressing these questions with archaeological DNA approaches in the common sunflower, Helianthus annuus. Native American farmers living ~5000 years ago began transforming sunflower from a highly branched wild plant with small disks and seeds into a staple oilseed crop with a large head and large seeds on an unbranched stalk. We have assembled a time series of archaeological samples spanning from ~3800 to ~400 years before present and obtained endogenous DNA sequences from these samples through genome resequencing and sequence capture approaches, revealing how human cultivation altered sunflower genetic diversity through time. We find evidence of shifting agricultural practices, as revealed by multiple genetic bottlenecks and changes in crop-wild gene flow. Our analyses also resolve a longstanding controversy about where and how many times sunflower was domesticated. Through targeted resequencing of ~425 candidate genes, our archaeological time series reveals the timing and order of selective sweeps, providing insight into how the sunflower domestication syndrome was assembled through time. Finally, ~1300 years before present, we observe concurrent shifts in morphology, gene flow from wild into cultivated sunflower, and selective sweep frequency. We interpret these temporally coinciding shifts as signatures of a period of more relaxed selection, likely reflective of reduced cultural connectivity and/or the regional expansion of maize agriculture at this time.

## Two centuries of genetic diversity change in rice at different spatial scales

**Presenter:** Rafal Gutaker, *Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew*

**Abstract:** For decades, researchers have warned of the drastic diversity decrease in staple crops. While a critical loss of local crop varieties has been well documented, some argue that genetic diversity has been replenished by using exotic gene pools in breeding. We investigated the genetic diversity change in rice at three spatial scales, using genotype and phenotype data. We compare whole genomes of 450 rice samples sequenced from museum and herbarium specimens, and collected contemporarily at the historical sampling locations. On a continental scale, we observed a loss of multiple rice lineages and homogenization of varieties cultivated contemporarily. On a regional scale, dense temporal sampling in the Mekong Delta revealed genetic continuity of rice populations between 1869 and 1957. In 1960s and 70s, the Green Revolution initiated a major overhaul of rice agriculture in Asia, which is detectable in our data with a complete genetic ancestry replacement by 2023. Contemporary rice populations in the Mekong Delta maintain genetic diversity levels observed in historical populations, however, exhibit closer kinship measured by sharing of large haploblocks. We also observed a significant increase in kinship among individual plants taken from the same field, compared to historical fields, where such kinship is virtually indetectable. The close relatedness in contemporary varieties substantially limited breeding pool and diversity in key adaptive loci. Overall, these findings showcase the complexities in genetic diversity changes in rice, where on a regional scale, net genetic diversity of rice has not decreased, but their evolutionary potential has diminished.

## Tracking molecular mechanisms of plant adaptation to climate change with herbaria

**Presenter:** Patricia Lang, *UC Berkeley*

**Abstract:** To live across diverse environments, plants have to balance taking up CO2 to grow with the concomitant loss of water through their leaf stomatal pores. Increasing CO2 concentrations, together with climate change induced droughts and increasing temperatures should strongly alter this balance. However, while the genetics of stomata development in Arabidopsis thaliana are well-characterized, little is known about the natural variation of stomata genes across the climatic gradients of the plant's geographic range, let alone across the last centuries of climate change.<br />Using the 1001 A. thaliana genomes resource and genetic variation across a unique set of ~200 historical herbarium specimens dating as far back as 1817, we describe the global natural variation in core stomata genes. By combining published mutant phenotypes for these stomata genes with their genetic variation over time, we develop an experimentally-informed polygenic score for stomatal density to infer phenotype change over 200 years of climate change. Integrating a model organism's historical specimens and a molecularly understood phenotype allows us to understand how this species adapted in the past, at a phenotypic, genetic and ultimately a functional-molecular level. It thus provides a unique opportunity to decipher in detail whether and how plants respond to climate change.<br />

## Leveraging Herbarium Collections to access Natural Genetic Variation

**Presenter:** Polina Novikova, *Vib-ugent Center For Plant Systems Biology*

**Abstract:** Easy exploration of the natural genetic diversity can enh comparative and population genetics approaches for understanding the function and evolution of adaptive traits. We showcase our study based on whole genome sequencing of the entire herbarium collection of Siberian Arabidopsis lyrata. A. lyrata is highly variable outcrossing relative of Arabidopsis thaliana with multiple origins of autotetraploidy and multiple independent transitions to self-compatibility. Screening the genomes from the herbarium, we found autotetraploid A. lyrata lineages in Northern Urals and Central Siberia in addition to known Central European lineages. We showed that the establishment of Siberian tetraploids is mediated by introgression of adaptive alleles of meiotic genes from European tetraploid A. lyrata and Arabidopsis arenosa, despite large geographical distance. We combined the whole genome sequencing data of A. lyrata from several extensive studies, including ours, to cover the whole range of geographical distribution across Eurasia and North America reaching close to 1k samples. We provide interactive ways of genomic data exploration: a searchable genome browser with A. lyrata and A. arenosa alignments to their respective reference genomes, cross-linked by orthologous gene names, geographical distribution of samples, and their assignments to population structure clusters. As an example of use-case of this resource, available at arabidopsislyrata.org, we explored genome-wide associations with climate variables and latitude within one of the diploid A. lyrata lineages showing polygenic adaptation along day-length gradient in East Siberia.

## Historical Genomes Reveal a Centuries-Long Competition–Colonization Trade-off in Plant-infecting Pseudomonas

**Presenter:** Hernán Burbano, *University College London*

**Abstract:** Microbial competition drives rapid adaptation, often forcing organisms to specialize in new ecological niches. Adaptations that improve competitive ability can reduce performance in other environments, creating evolutionary trade-offs. Whether such trade-offs persist in nature, or are eroded as lineages adapt through compensatory changes, remains largely unknown. Here we show that a trade-off between competitive ability and host colonization has been stably maintained in natural plant-infecting Pseudomonas populations for centuries. Wild plant-pathogenic Pseudomonas compete using tailocins—phage-derived molecular weapons that bind to specific cell-surface receptors. Genomic surveys and functional assays reveal that the most broadly lethal tailocins remain rare. While tailocin production increases competitive killing, it also compromises plant colonization. Crucially, herbarium-derived historical genomes spanning two centuries demonstrate that the underlying polymorphisms are not transient. These time-resolved data reveal that the competition–colonization trade-off has persisted for at least 10⁵–10⁶ generations, providing direct evidence that this constraint is evolutionary stable rather than transient. Together, these results show that in natural populations, a trade-off between competition and colonization is fundamental and not easily overcome. This stability suggests that tailocin resistance may come with predictable fitness costs, highlighting tailocins as a promising therapeutic avenue where resistance could weaken pathogens in natural or clinical settings.

## A herbariomic approach to characterising medicinal black henbane (Hyoscyamus niger) populations in Britain

**Presenter:** Natalia Przelomska, *University Of Portsmouth*

**Abstract:** Plants have been recognised throughout human history for their diverse medicinal properties and are now embedded within healthcare systems worldwide. Despite being an invaluable resource, the population biology of most medicinal plant species is poorly understood, with many likely threatened with local or complete extinction. Here, we characterise the declining population of naturalised black henbane (Hyoscyamus niger) in Britain. As an opportunistic coloniser of disturbed habitats, it belongs to a unique class of biodiversity associated with pre-historic human migration and agricultural systems. It is therefore a suitable model for investigating a) how land-use changes over time have affected the persistence of archaeophyte populations and b) how ephemerality itself influences population genetic parameters. To interrogate this, we apply a panel of black henbane samples sourced from herbarium collections, supplemented with plants from present-day localities. The analysis is supported by the first assembly of a full draft genome for this species, conducted via long-read sequencing technology. On a species level, our results will provide a foundation for further study of both a) the colonisation and invasion capacity of black henbane in a global context and b) its genetic basis of synthesising known and yet-uncharacterised compounds of medicinal value.

## The phylogeography of the Wikstroemia genus – one phylogeny, 50 species, 60 islands, and 250 years of botany

**Presenter:** Ruben Cousins-Westerberg, *Lund University*

**Abstract:** Herbarium specimes are a wonderful resource for phylogenetics… if you can sequence them. Herbarium specimens are a wonderful resource for biogeographics… if you can figure out where they’re from. Through the twists and turns of label identification and geographical mystery solving – featuring squinting, translation tools, speculative interpretation, and the voyages of Captain James Cook – and through a lot of short read sequencing, we leverage 200 herbarium sheets to reconstruct the first ever genus level phylogeny of the Wikstroemia (Thymelaeaceae), a culturally and medicinally significant genus of flowering plants found throughout East, South, and Southeast Asia, as well as on most major Pacific islands. Using this phylogeny and the herbarium records as a backbone, we discuss the biogeographic history of the genus. We ask how the Pacific Island taxa dispersed and diversified from their origin in a common ancestor in Asia, trying to answer the question: How do plants cross oceans? We try to answer another question too, discussing the possibilities and limitations of using herbarium specimens for phylogenomics: How hard is it, actually?
