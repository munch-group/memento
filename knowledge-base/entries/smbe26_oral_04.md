## Understanding the roles of Endozoicomonas in marine animals: Mutualists, parasites, and everything in between?

**Presenter:** Claudia Pogoreutz, Université De Perpignan Via Domitia

**Abstract:**

The bacterial genus Endozoicomonas is globally prevalent in a diversity of marine animal hosts, and constitutes the genus most consistently associated with Cnidaria worldwide. Long considered key mutualists of corals due to its metabolic versatility, evidence of host co-diversification, and frequent decline during stress, ‘bleaching’, and disease, Endozoicomonas has become emblematic of coral bacterial symbiosis. Yet, definitive empirical evidence for its functional role, interactions within the holobiont, and breadth of lifestyles remains limited. Emerging reports of parasitic and pathogenic behaviours in other marine animal hosts further challenge the prevailing assumption of mutualism and highlight the need to re-examine their roles in cnidarian biology. In this talk, I will provide an overview of the state-of-the-art of this rapidly developing field, and showcase recent functional studies aimed at unraveling the biology of this enigmatic and ubiquitous bacterial family.

## Streptococcus amylase-binding proteins in geladas and baboons reveal convergent oral microbial adaptation to starch-rich diets in humans and primates

**Presenter:** Irina Velsko, Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology

**Abstract:**

The salivary alpha-amylase gene underwent a major copy number expansion in multiple mammals, and in humans this expansion has been interpreted as the result of selection imposed by dietary starch consumption. Specific human oral Streptococcus species carry amylase-binding proteins that hold active alpha-amylases adjacent to bacterial cells, ensuring bacterial access to host-derived starch break-down products. The presence of amylase-binding protein A (abpA) genes in oral Streptococcus species of humans and Neanderthals but not chimpanzees or gorillas suggests that the acquisition of abpA reflects co-evolution between commensal microbiota and their hosts due to host dietary adaptation. If so, abpA-carrying Streptococcus might also be present in the oral cavities of other salivary amylase-producing mammals. We investigated the oral microbiota of two primates, wild geladas (n=15) and baboons (n=19), plus 6 additional wild primate species (n=36) and pigs (n=19) for the presence of abpA using de novo metagenome assembly. A hidden Markov model identified genetically heterogeneous apbA genes with high confidence in contigs assembled from baboons and geladas but not from other primates or pigs, with the majority of genes in Streptococcus genomes. Phylogenetic analysis determined that the Streptococcus species carrying abpA in geladas and baboons are genetically distant from those in humans, suggesting independent acquisition by Streptococcus. Overall, we find evidence of oral commensal microbial co-evolution related to host dietary adaptation in starch-dependent, but not non-starch-dependent primates, suggesting that carrying oral streptococci with abpA may be an acquired primate trait correlated with levels of dietary starch consumption.

## Holo-omic insights into host–microbiome dynamics during starvation and compensatory growth in zebrafish

**Presenter:** Søren B. Hansen, University of Copenhagen

**Abstract:**

Host-associated microbes are increasingly recognized for their role in shaping adaptive phenotypes in vertebrates. Compensatory growth is an accelerated growth phase in fish that occurs when favorable conditions are restored after starvation. While several studies have identified host adjustment underlying this response, less is known about the microbial contribution. Given its role in digestion and nutrient processing, we applied a holo-omic approach to investigate the microbial dynamics in the zebrafish gut during starvation and refeeding, aiming to uncover hologenomic adaptations linked to compensatory growth.<br /><br />We performed holo-omic extractions on whole gut samples from 108 zebrafish followed by long-read DNA sequencing and short-read RNA-seq. Most DNA was of zebrafish origin; however, co-assembly of unmapped reads followed by binning recovered 19 high-quality and 24 medium-quality Metagenome-Assembled Genomes (MAGs), including core taxa of the zebrafish microbiota. Relative to the zebrafish genome, microbial coverage decreased during starvation, but increased again during refeeding, reflecting a dynamic microbial fraction of the hologenome. Notably, the coverage of specific MAGs, including Aeromonas veronii and Pseudomonas sp. exceeded zebrafish genomic coverage, suggesting that bacteria may outnumber host cells in the gut.<br /><br />Epigenomic data from long-read sequencing, together with paired RNA-seq, revealed genome-wide changes in host DNA methylation and gene expression during starvation and refeeding. Integrating these host responses with microbial gene functions highlighted a holo-omic interaction in dietary chitin degradation: host genes dominated initial breakdown, whereas microbial genes dominated downstream utilization. Notably Aeromonas veronii expressed high levels of chitin-utilization genes, consistent with enhanced chitin metabolism in the zebrafish gut.

## Leveraging large-scale long-read sequencing data to understand the diversity, drivers and evolution of Lepidoptera cobionts at unprecedented resolution

**Presenter:** Julien Martinez, Wellcome Sanger Institute

**Speaker Bio:** I am an evolutionary biologist studying interactions between insects and symbiotic microbes, particularly in the context of heritable bacteria that manipulate their host's reproduction or confer benefits such as protection against natural enemies. In my research, I have used a range of approaches, from bacterial genomics to phylogenetics and experimental evolution. My current research aims to provide new insights into the genomic diversity and evolution of host-microbe interactions across the tree of Lepidoptera (butterflies & moths) using high-quality and large-scale host sequencing data generated through Project Psyche and the Darwin Tree of Lif Project.

**Abstract:**

Project Psyche is a large-scale genome sequencing project that is creating an unprecedented opportunity to reveal hidden ecological and evolutionary dimensions of microbial partners (“cobionts”) of insects. Here, we screened high-quality PacBio HiFi genome datasets from 1,214 species of butterfly and moths and combined detection of rRNA markers with metagenome assembly methods to identify more than 700 host-cobiont combinations, and produced more than 600 high-quality cobiont genome assemblies. By integrating comprehensive host metadata and host phylogenetic information, we first resolved broad-scale patterns of cobiont presence and diversity, revealing the influence of host relatedness, life-history traits, biogeography, and symbiont mode of transmission. Furthermore, the availability of complete and highly contiguous cobiont genomes allowed us to move beyond cobionts’ presence–absence and co-infection patterns. In particular, we uncovered hidden functional variation, providing insight into some of the ecological strategies of symbionts, and the genomic basis of their intimate interactions with Lepidoptera hosts, such as reproductive parasitism and mutualism. Thus, this study both reveals extensive and previously undescribed microbial diversity and establishes a powerful genomic framework to study insect–microbe symbioses, their ecological roles, and their evolutionary consequences at unprecedented resolution.

## Zooming into Host–Microbiome Evolution via Micro-Scale Spatial Metagenomics

**Presenter:** Amalia Bogri, University Of Copenhagen

**Abstract:**

Understanding microbial contributions to host biology and evolution is central to the hologenomic framework, yet most metagenomic studies rely on bulk sampling that averages across spatial heterogeneity and masks local ecological and selective contexts. To address this limitation, we developed Micro-Scale Spatial Metagenomics (MSSM), a methodology that reconstructs host-associated microbial communities directly from intact gut tissue at micron-scale resolution. MSSM combines laser microdissection of cryosections, ultra-low-input shotgun metagenomics, and genome-resolved bioinformatics to characterise communities from microsamples as small as ~500 µm², corresponding to ~100 bacterial cells.<br /><br />Using the chicken gut as a model system, MSSM revealed fine-scale spatial heterogeneity in community composition and functional potential, pointing to spatially structured microbial assemblages and localised segregation of metabolic capacities. Additionally, MSSM recovered de novo assembled bacterial genomes absent from the conventional macro-scale dataset. At the strain level, MSSM uncovered coexisting strain lineages with distinct spatial distributions and host-specific occurrence patterns. At the SNP level, microdiversity analyses showed that clonal populations cluster at spatial scales below ~200 µm.<br /><br />We implemented MSSM alongside conventional metagenomics to investigate gut-associated microbiomes across controlled host perturbations, including dietary shifts, microbial supplementation, infection, and vaccination, in poultry and swine. Using large collections of spatially resolved microsamples, we discuss methodological insights, analytical constraints, and emerging spatial patterns that are not accessible through bulk sampling. By resolving microbial communities at the spatial scales at which ecological interactions and population structuring occur, MSSM expands the hologenomic toolkit and enables spatially explicit analyses of microbial evolution, niche partitioning, and host-associated interactions.

## What lives in, on, and around a lichen?

**Presenter:** Merce Montoliu Nerin, Wellcome Sanger Institute

**Abstract:**

Lichens are composite organisms in which filamentous fungi (mycobionts) and photosynthetic algae/cyanobacteria (photobionts) co-evolve to thrive in demanding habitats. In addition to the expected symbiotic organisms, lichens contain additional microorganisms, both prokaryotes and eukaryotes, found within the lichen, or on its surfaces. These organisms can help us expand our understanding of the ecology of the lichen and its interactions with the surrounding environment. Capturing this full biological complexity is possible by analysing lichen DNA sequencing data in a metagenomic framework.<br />At Tree of Life (ToL) at the Wellcome Sanger Institute we have generated high quality long-read sequencing data for 38 lichens. They are the result of collaborative efforts stemming from the Aquatic Symbiosis Genomics and the Darwin Tree of Life projects. We have explored the raw reads with k-mer based taxonomic profiling tools to identify organisms present, and we have assembled the data with the Metagenomeassembly pipeline, developed by ToL. Although DNA coverage limited the completeness of some eukaryotic assemblies, we recovered high-quality prokaryotic genomes and organellar sequences, as well as ribosomal DNA markers, enabling comprehensive taxonomic characterisation of the lichen symbiont communities. Moreover, complementary Hi-C data provided genomic interaction information, enabling additional insights into the organisation of lichen symbiotic communities and its interactions.<br />The session focused on hologenomics poses as the perfect stage to present our results, disentangling the complex community of organisms that were sequenced in each of our lichen samples, including insects, protists, and additional algae and fungi to the ones forming the lichen symbiosis.

## The New Zealand Butterfish - A model teleost to understand holobiome interactions

**Presenter:** Qing Xiang Charles Mak, University Of Auckland & New Zealand Institute for Bioeconomy Science Limited

**Abstract:**

Endemic to New Zealand, the butterfish (mararī; Odax pullus) is a temperate marine wrasse with an exclusive herbivorous adult phase. Given that herbivory is exhibited by only ~5% of fish species, the butterfish is a compelling candidate for more sustainable aquaculture diversification. Crucially, hindgut fermentation – a specialized microbial-mediated process involved in the breakdown of dietary algal material into readily assimilable short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), has been demonstrated to play an integral role in the species’ nutritional strategy. However, the respective contributions of the host and its commensal microbial community remain unresolved due to limited genomic resources. We assembled a high-quality butterfish genome using PacBio HiFi reads supported by complementary short-read data and multi-tissue RNA-seq for structural gene annotation. The draft assembly achieved high continuity and completeness (641 Mb; N50: 22.8 Mb; Compleasm 99.49%; BUSCO 98.3%), with 21,661 annotated protein-coding genes, of which 85.7% showed transcriptional support. We demonstrate that laminarinase, a key enzyme involved in the breakdown of structural polysaccharides in the species’ predominantly algal diet, is likely of microbial origin, reinforcing the importance of the host–microbe relationship. Additionally, we identified lineage-specific gene family expansions in immune regulatory and epithelial transport functions alongside a reduced emphasis on long-chain fatty acid handling. These patterns are consistent with host genomic adaptation to a fermentation-based nutritional strategy, reflecting long-standing symbiotic interactions with the gut microbiome and enhanced reliance on microbially derived SCFAs. Collectively, O. pullus represents a compelling model for investigating host genomic evolution within a holobiome framework.

## The role of Wolbachia in reproductive isolation and speciation in Drosophila paulistorum

**Presenter:** Linnéa Ekström, Uppsala university

**Abstract:**

Symbiont-driven speciation may occur when endosymbionts that influence host reproduction persist within hosts over evolutionary timescales. Drosophila paulistorum is a Neotropical species complex of six geographically overlapping but reproductively isolated incipient species, whose biology is strongly influenced by Wolbachia. Most notably, it contributes to reproductive isolation among the semispecies by affecting female assortative mating behavior and male semispecies-specific pheromone signaling. Moreover, while most Drosophila species in the willistoni group are facultatively infected with Wolbachia, D. paulistorum harbors an obligate infection. These findings suggest that Wolbachia may contribute to the ongoing sympatric speciation in D. paulistorum, providing a unique opportunity to explore the role of endosymbionts like Wolbachia in driving this process. <br /><br />We sequenced Wolbachia genomes from several D. paulistorum semispecies and related willistoni group species, together with their respective Drosophila host genomes. To investigate evolutionary relationships and the extent of co-evolution between host and endosymbiont, we combine comparative genomics, phylogenomics and divergence time estimates with a relaxed molecular clock model. Our results show that, although host and endosymbiont tree topologies are largely congruent, Wolbachia consistently appears younger than its hosts. This pattern indicates a higher turnover rate of Wolbachia than previously thought and reflects the combined effects of vertical inheritance and introgression shaping Wolbachia prevalence and persistence in Neotropical Drosophila. Together, these results advance our understanding of the interactions between bacterial symbionts and their hosts and provide new insights into the role of endosymbionts in shaping eukaryotic host evolution.<br />

## Metagenome-derived population genomics of a vertically transmitted endosymbiont during ecological speciation of its insect host

**Presenter:** André Bourbonnais, University of Copenhagen

**Abstract:**

The ecology and evolution of bacterial symbionts and their hosts are highly interdependent. This is clear from many examples of codivergence across host and symbiont phylogenies. Yet, little is known about the evolutionary dynamics of symbionts during the earliest stages of host divergence and speciation. Over the past 0.5-1M years, the specialist fly Tephritis conura colonized a novel thistle plant, initiating ecological speciation between different host plant lineages. This provides a natural system in which to test whether this has also led to divergence in their unculturable, vertically transmitted bacterial symbiont, Candidatus Stammerula tephritidis (Stammerula). Using microbial sequences mined from whole genome resequencing samples of Tephritis flies, we assembled the first metagenome-assembled genome (MAG) for Stammerula to a single 1.48 MB contig (99.99% complete). Its small size, AT bias, and lack of flagellar genes reflect adaptation to life inside the fly's midgut crypts. Functional annotation revealed that Stammerula can synthesize nine of the ten essential amino acids for insects, suggesting a nutrient-provisioning role. We then compared Stammerula population structure across host lineages and geographic regions. Population genomic analysis revealed Stammerula are broadly structured by fly lineage. Importantly, we found geographic distance was not correlated with population differentiation. Stammerula in ecologically divergent flies in sympatry can show stronger differentiation than those in allopatry. Genomic regions with strong signatures of both divergence and selection included fast-evolving toxin-antitoxin modules. Together, these results suggest symbiont evolution can track ecological and evolutionary divergence of hosts from its earliest stages.

## Disentangling the relationships between microbial taxa and metabolic outputs in a seaweed holobiont

**Presenter:** Maximiliana Bogan, The University Of Chicago

**Abstract:**

In molecular evolutionary biology, genotype-phenotype maps are common tools for understanding the associative relationships between genetic variation and phenotypic variation. Combined with mechanistic assays, these maps can elucidate the causal contribution of specific genes or mutations to specific phenotypic outcomes. Previous work has recognized the strength of this approach for understanding the contribution of microbial biodiversity to ecosystem function. However, for many ecologically important holobiont systems, we still lack empirical knowledge of the ecological and evolutionary mechanisms that give rise to observed associative relationships. <br />In this work, we used field-based metabolomic data to generate an associative map between bacterial community composition and the metabolic outputs of the bull kelp Nereocystis luetkeana. We applied antibiotics to kelp in situ and analyzed the resulting metabolic profiles in comparison to untreated controls to broadly address the question ‘how do kelp-associated bacteria affect host metabolic response?’ We then identified specific metabolic features associated with disturbance of the microbiome and will test the effect of these compounds on the growth of diverse kelp-associated bacteria in the lab. Eco-evolutionary theory predicts many possible metabolic interactions between microbial community members and kelp hosts. We investigate three hypotheses: 1) Compounds detected in environmental samples are toxic to kelp-associated bacteria. 2) Compounds detected in environmental samples are carbon sources for kelp-associated bacteria. 3) Compounds detected in environmental samples are protective against oxidative stress for kelp-associated bacteria. The results of this work will address different mechanistic hypotheses about the nature of host-microbe metabolic interactions in an understudied seaweed system.

## Ecology and evolution of the mammalian oral microbiome elucidated using museum specimen metagenomics

**Presenter:** Markella Moraitou, University Of Edinburgh

**Abstract:**

Despite growing interest, the microbiomes of wild animals, remain poorly characterized, especially for body sites beyond the gut, and largely due to the challenges of sampling in the wild. Here, we use dental calculus preserved on museum specimens to investigate the ecological and evolutionary drivers of the oral microbiome across 32 ecologically and phylogenetically diverse mammal species, constituting, to our knowledge, the most diverse dataset of animal oral microbiomes to date. We show that host diet and digestive physiology shape both the composition and function of the oral microbiome, consistent with patterns reported for the gut. However, in contrast to the gut, where herbivores harbour the most diverse microbiomes, oral microbiome diversity is highest in carnivores and omnivores, which are enriched in taxa and functions associated with protein and lipid degradation. Host phylogeny only weakly affects microbiome composition across broad mammalian diversity, but its importance increases within narrower host clades. At the same time, we identify several microbial taxa showing signatures of host co-diversification, many of which are rumen-associated and exhibit host-specific metabolism. Together, these findings show that the oral microbiome follows eco-evolutionary dynamics that are related to, but distinct from, those governing the gut microbiome, and demonstrate the relevance of this mostly overlooked microbial community for studies of wild animals.
