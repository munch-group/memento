## Whole-genome sequencing of Black Death victims reveals an immune gene under natural selection

**Presenter:** Harald Ringbauer, *Max Planck Institute For Evolutionary Anthropology Leipzig*

**Abstract:** To identify genetic variants relevant to mortality during the Medieval Black Death, we sequenced more than 400 individuals from molecularly confirmed Central European plague mass graves. We successfully recovered whole-genome sequences at ~1.0× coverage, generating high-quality ancient human DNA data from the initial outbreak (1346–1353 CE) of the second plague pandemic. These data provide sufficient statistical power to detect allele-frequency changes at common variants and enable screening for pathogen-driven natural selection. We identify a major risk allele in an immune gene that plays an important role in the innate immune response to bacteria. Notably, this variant occurs at exceptionally high frequency in present-day European populations, suggesting a genetic contribution to the severe impact of the Black Death in West Eurasia. Our results provide evidence that natural selection acted on the human genome during one of the most devastating pandemics in recorded history and help to resolve long-standing debates about the genomic impact of the Black Death.

## On Inferring the Virulence of Ancient Pathogens, Especially Prehistoric Plague

**Presenter:** Ruairidh Macleod, *University Of Oxford*

**Abstract:** While palaeogenomics has substantially revised understanding of ancient pathogens, as a data source in itself, it provides little insight into the actual virulence of presumed pathogens (especially as studies focus deeper into the evolutionary past). Prehistoric plague (Yersinia pestis) has been particularly controversial in this regard. Recurrent outbreaks across an extended familial pedigree in Neolithic southern Sweden and from France are posited to be linked to the Late Neolithic demographic decline. Virulence in the former case was disputed on the interpretation that the absence of the gene ymt in these early plague strains would have precluded flea-borne transmission, and instead caused food-borne enteritis, similar to the profile of Y. pseudotuberculosis. The discovery of apparently fatal outbreaks of plague amongst prehistoric hunter-gatherers at Lake Baikal expands on the availability of other lines of evidence to interpret virulence based on lethality, particularly through demography, though this in itself does not provide a causal explanation. Assessments of gene content based on pangenome analysis show unique configurations of genes and ORFs linked to virulence from which, alongside well-characterised genes, we undertake to infer probable host immunological interactions. In particular, the question of whether pathogens such as Y. pestis underwent attenuation of virulence in prehistoric contexts (where no documentation for pandemic-like scenarios in humans or animal hosts exists) particularly significant. This talk will focus on utilising distinct data types to inform models of virulence in prehistoric pathogens, a vital objective as the field aspires to move towards palaeogenomic epidemiology.

## Ancient Genomic Analysis of a New Mycobacterium tuberculosis Lineage from Iron Age Britain

**Presenter:** Megan Michel, *Harvard University*

**Abstract:** Tuberculosis is the leading cause of death due to infectious disease in contemporary human populations, and evidence of selection on TB-associated variants suggests that the disease has afflicted Eurasian populations for at least 2,000 years (Kerner et al. 2021, Akbari et al. 2024). Beyond this human impact, a paraphyletic clade within the Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex (MTBC) evolved to specialize on a diverse array of domesticated and wild mammals. Several human-infecting lineages fall within the animal-infecting clade, suggesting a complex history of inter-species transmission and adaptation; however, both the antiquity of these animal-adapted lineages and the genomic underpinnings of host specificity within the MTBC remain poorly understood. We report the oldest whole-genome Mycobacterium tuberculosis data generated to date from a 20–25-year-old female Iron Age individual from the site of Wetwang Slack in East Yorkshire, United Kingdom (Burial 9, c. 400-50 BCE). Molecular dating analyses incorporating this basal lineage may help to resolve long-standing debates regarding the antiquity of tuberculosis in human populations. Interestingly, while Burial 9 exhibits no pathological alterations, contemporaneous remains from both humans and livestock buried at Wetwang Slack display lesions characteristic of tuberculosis infection (Wooding et al. 2022). Consistent with these observations, phylogenetic analysis of modern and ancient MTBC diversity places the Wetwang lineage basal to all animal-infecting MTBC strains. While further sampling and analysis is needed, osteological and genomic evidence suggests that the Wetwang strain may represent a previously unsampled host-generalist lineage, providing an opportunity to shed light on the evolution of host specificity within the MTBC.

## Bayesian approaches to root phylogenetic trees under root uncertainty: evaluation using empirical and simulated ancient and modern pathogen data


**Presenter:** Maria Lopopolo, *Institute Pasteur*

**Abstract:** Accurately rooting phylogenetic trees is crucial for reconstructing the evolutionary and epidemiological histories of pathogens, yet it remains challenging when temporal signal is limited or outgroup information is uncertain. Here, we introduce a novel Bayesian framework for assessing statistical support for the root of phylogenetic trees that integrates molecular evolutionary models with sampling date information, aiming to robustly discriminate between alternative root placements. We benchmark the method using large-scale simulations under a range of evolutionary scenarios that are typical in ancient DNA studies, enabling us to systematically assess its sensitivity and specificity. To further explore its real-world utility, we validate the approach on well-characterized lineages of Yersinia pestis exhibiting both strong and weak molecular clocklike behaviour, as well as on empirical datasets from additional pathogens with unclear root placement. Preliminary results indicate that our method is broadly applicable and can provide improved root support and quantification of uncertainty, particularly in cases where standard root placement strategies are inconclusive. We discuss these findings and offer perspectives on the integration of this approach into future studies of pathogen evolution, highlighting its potential for enhancing confidence in evolutionary and epidemiological inference.

## First ancient genome of Streptococcus pyogenes from a pre-Columbian Bolivian mummy reveals its presence in the Americas before European contact

**Presenter:** Frank Maixner, *Eurac Research Institute for Mummy Studies*

**Abstract:** Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A Streptococcus; GAS) is a major human pathogen responsible for diseases ranging from pharyngitis to severe invasive infections. Despite its clinical importance and recent global resurgence, the evolutionary history of GAS remains poorly understood, particularly its presence in ancient human populations. To date, no genomic data from ancient S. pyogenes strains have been reported, leaving unresolved whether the pathogen has long coexisted with humans or emerged more recently in association with major historical events.<br />Here, we report the first genomic evidence of S. pyogenes in the pre-Columbian Americas. We analyzed a tooth sample from a naturally mummified individual dated to the Late Intermediate Period (1283–1383 cal AD) from present-day Bolivia. Shotgun metagenomic sequencing and de novo assembly enabled near-complete reconstruction of an ancient S. pyogenes genome.<br />Functional characterization using modern typing frameworks reveals close similarity between the ancient strain and contemporary GAS lineages associated with pharyngitis. The genome harbors core virulence determinants, although prophage regions lack streptococcal pyrogenic exotoxins. Phylogenetic analyses position the ancient Bolivian strain near the base of modern S. pyogenes diversity, and Bayesian dating suggests that most extant GAS lineages diversified globally within the past ~5,500 years.<br />These findings substantially extend the known temporal and geographic range of S. pyogenes, providing direct evidence that the pathogen circulated among Indigenous populations in the Americas prior to European contact. Overall, this study offers new insights into the long-term evolutionary dynamics and persistence of virulence in a globally important human pathogen.<br />

## Bacterial Disease and Viral Emergence in Holocene Hunter-Gatherers from Patagonia


**Presenter:** Maria C. Ávila-Arcos, *Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México*

**Abstract:** Patagonia was the last mainland territory of the Americas to be populated by humans ~ 14,000 yBP. The recovery of ancient pathogen genomes from this region offers a valuable perspective to explore the local impact of infectious diseases and their evolutionary processes. This study recovered ancient DNA (aDNA) from 32 individuals from Central Patagonia dating around 6000-100 yBP. We generated paleogenomic data, performed metagenomic characterization, and recovered one Hepatitis B virus (HBV) genome (6.8 X, 1,500 yBP) and four genomes of a leprosy-causing pathogen, Mycobacterium lepromatosis (1.1-16.2X, 4,000–100 yBP). Phylogenetics and recombination analyses of the extinct South American HBV lineages identified ancient American genomes that may have contributed to the diversification of modern H and F genotypes. Our recovered ancient HBV genome allows for a new calibration point for estimating the recombination rate and lineage split times. In parallel, we analysed ancient M. lepromatosis genomes, representing the oldest and southernmost lineages recovered to date. Their phylogenetic placement indicates an affinity with human-associated strains, suggesting a millennia-long pathogenic relationship between humans and M. lepromatosis, and adding additional calibration points to date the divergence from M. leprae, the other causative agent of leprosy. We evaluated the presence/absence of virulence genes in ancient and modern clades, as well as missense and stop-lost mutations with functional impact. Furthermore, we propose a hypothesis for the spread of M. lepromatosis to Europe and its establishment in red squirrels reservoirs. Together, these results provide an evolutionary perspective on the dynamics in ancient pathogens in the Americas.

## Tracing the origin and evolution of early smallpox in South America

**Presenter:** Bruno Romero, *Trinity College Dublin*

**Abstract:** Soon after European contact and throughout the Columbian exchange, Indigenous populations of the Americas experienced catastrophic demographic collapse driven by multiple factors, most notably infectious diseases. Among the most devastating and highly contagious of these pathogens was smallpox. Historical records identify repeated smallpox epidemics as a major contributor to Indigenous population decline and, consequently, to European colonial dominance across the continent. Yet the genetic identity of the smallpox strains responsible for these epidemics remains unknown. Here, we report two cases of smallpox infection from an Inca/Early Colonial-era community in northern Chile, including the first near-complete single contig assembly (~220×) of an ancient Variola virus genome from the Americas. These genomes form a now-extinct lineage diverged in the early 12th century, after the diversification of early medieval European lineages but prior to the emergence of 17th–18th century European strains that ultimately gave rise to all 20th-century smallpox. Using the high-coverage genome as a temporal anchor, we detect elevated disruptive mutation rates during the 16th–18th centuries, a period when colonial activity intensified globally. These findings provide the first direct molecular evidence of smallpox introduction to the Americas through European contact and uncover how its evolution was accelerated by widespread transmission among previously unexposed populations.

## Insights from ancient DNA into deep-time evolution of oral microbes in the Cusco region of the Peruvian Andes

**Presenter:** Tanvi Prasad Honap, *University Of Zurich*

**Abstract:** The Cusco region of the Peruvian Andes has a long history of human occupation and regional interactions, making it a valuable context for examining health and disease in ancient populations. Here, we employed ancient DNA and metagenomics approaches to investigate the evolutionary trajectories of oral microbes and pathogens across approximately 6,000 years of Andean history. Shotgun sequencing of dental calculus and tooth samples yielded ancient DNA for 23 individuals from four archaeological sites in the Cusco region: the Archaic-period (6,350-4,150 BP) hunter-gatherer site of Kasapata, the Formative-period (2,350-2,050 BP) village of Yuthu, and two Middle Horizon (1,350-950 BP) sites including the Wari colony of Hatun Cotuyuc and the nearby village of Ak’awillay. The Epstein-Barr virus, a ubiquitous modern pathogen associated with infectious mononucleosis, was detected in an Archaic-period hunter-gatherer, indicating its long-standing presence in this region. Partial genomes of opportunistic oral pathogens, such as the periodontitis-associated Tannerella forsythia, Treponema denticola, and Porphyromonas gingivalis, as well as the caries-associated Streptococcus mutans, were recovered from multiple individuals. Phylogenetic analysis of four Tannerella forsythia genomes revealed that the ancient Peruvian strains cluster within a lineage previously identified in pre-European Contact era individuals from the Americas and absent in modern-day individuals. Together, these findings demonstrate the utility of ancient metagenomics for reconstructing past health and disease ecologies and provide a framework for future investigations into long-term host-pathogen dynamics in the Cusco region.

## Ancient Treponema pallidum genomes reveal phylogenetic diversity and lineage persistence through time in South American populations

**Presenter:** Davide Bozzi, *University Of Lausanne*

**Abstract:** Treponema pallidum is a pathogen responsible for several diseases in humans around the globe. Its origin and spread have been subject of debate for centuries. Recently, the retrieval of ancient genomes has greatly deepened our understanding of T. pallidum evolution and global spread. Here, we expand on this framework by reporting additional ancient T. pallidum genomes from Peru obtained using a targeted pathogen DNA enrichment approach. These genomes span a broad time period and encompass various cultures, including the Moche and the Inca civilizations. Our results reveal a high phylogenetic diversity of T. pallidum in pre-Hispanic Peru, lending support to previous research conducted in several South American regions. Additionally, we increase the coverage of a previously reported ~5.500 years-old highly divergent genome from the Northern Andes by almost 10-fold, allowing for a deeper investigation of its genomic features and a more accurate estimation of its divergence from present-day subspecies. We demonstrate that this lineage survived up until the first centuries CE in Peru, suggesting a multi-millennial presence in South American populations. These results set the ground for a better understanding of the spread and persistence of treponemal diseases in South American populations through the millennia.

## Co-isolation of ancient DNA and RNA from non-formalin-fixed lung specimens reveals host-pathogen interactions during the Industrial Revolution in Great Britain.

**Presenter:** Isabelle Du Plessis, *Fred Hutchinson Cancer Center*

**Abstract:** Advances in the field of ancient pathogen genomics have expanded our ability to understand microbial sources of past epidemics, primarily through the study of ancient DNA (aDNA). While reconstructed historical genomes provide insights into pathogen evolution, there is limited information about host-pathogen interactions and their contributions to disease due to limitations in ancient RNA (aRNA) recovery. In this study, we successfully co-isolated aDNA and aRNA from eleven non-formalin-fixed lung samples with a record of respiratory disease from three museums across Great Britain, with collection dates spanning c. 1740-1939 C.E., a period marked by a rise in population density and spread of infectious disease, driven by industrialization. This protocol allowed for the reconstruction of pathogen genomes and recovery of both host and pathogen transcripts, including Human rhinovirus A, Streptococcus pneumoniae, Moraxella catarrhalis, and Haemophilus influenzae. We detected expression of putative virulence-associated bacterial genes and host immune response genes, revealing potential contributors to disease. Notably, the S. pneumoniae capsule polysaccharide (cps) locus was associated with a serotype targeted by contemporary vaccines, and transcripts for genes involved in immune modulation, exotoxin production, and stress survival were identified. The integration of genomic and transcriptomic data goes beyond pathogen discovery, allowing detailed interrogation of host–pathogen interactions, reshaping our understanding of host response dynamics in historical epidemics, and enabling direct comparisons with modern microbial infections.

## Identifying the presence of Yersinia pestis in Medieval Turkey during the Second Plague Pandemic

**Presenter:** Ravneet Sidhu, *McMaster University*

**Abstract:** Yersinia pestis, the causative bacterium of the Plague, killed ~30-50% of Europe’s population during the Black Death (1348-1353), the initial epidemic wave of the second Plague pandemic (1346-1840). This devastating disease persisted in cyclical outbreaks across Afro-Eurasia for 500 years, and its descendants established global reservoirs, which continue to cause outbreaks today. Genomic studies of ancient Y. pestis recovered from archaeological remains have attempted to trace the bacterium’s origins, locate reservoirs, and understand these patterns of persistence and reintroduction. One significant region currently lacking genomic evidence of Y. pestis is the Ottoman Empire. Situated between Asia and Europe, Eurocentric narratives continuously paint the Ottoman Empire as an importer of Plague into Europe. Here, we screen 289 human and animal remains from across Medieval and Early Modern Turkey for evidence of Y. pestis and its reservoirs. We recover two ancient Y. pestis genomes from early outbreaks of the pandemic at the historically significant site of Nicaea, providing the first genomic evidence of ancient Y. pestis in the region. We then grapple with the complications of weaving together genomic and historical narratives to identify specific epidemic waves. As well as test the impact of stringent and standard mapping criteria on variant calling and subsequent phylogenetic positioning. We then place the samples on an updated second pandemic phylogeny and interpret the significance of their placement with respect to understanding the trans-continental dynamics of Plague spread during the second Plague pandemic.

## A deeply divergent Yersinia pestis lineage identified in Early Iron Age Romania

**Presenter:** Caitlin Mitchell, *University Of Tuebingen*

**Abstract:** Ancient pathogen genomics has advanced our understanding of the evolutionary history of Yersinia pestis, the causative agent of plague, revealing its presence in prehistoric populations as early as ~5,500 years ago and uncovering previously unknown genetic diversity with distinct transmission modes and disease potentials. Among these, two lineages exhibit distinct gene contents, suggesting that flea-adapted and non-flea-adapted strains coexisted from the Bronze Age into the Iron Age. However, the epidemiological behavior and demographic impact of these strains remain largely unexplored. Here, we investigate an early Iron Age context in present-day Romania dated to the 11th - 9th century BCE, representing communities associated with Babadag culture. Metagenomic screening of 16 individuals revealed the presence of Y. pestis and whole-genome capture enabled the reconstruction of Y. pestis genomes from three individuals. Genomic analyses identify a deeply divergent lineage that branches basal to all currently known Y. pestis diversity. Its genomic features include the absence of key adaptations associated with flea-borne transmission and the presence of large deletions (>100 kb in total), affecting virulence-associated genes. Some of these deletions are also found in Y. pseudotuberculosis, possibly reflecting the ancestral state of the Y. pestis genome, while others appear to be unique to this lineage. Ongoing molecular dating and pangenome analyses aim to further clarify the divergence timing and gene content of this lineage. The analysis of time-stamped pathogen genomes from the archaeological context of a transitory period in Iron Age Romania demonstrates how ancient pathogen genomics can shed light on prehistoric epidemiological processes.

## The deadly past of Yersinia enterocolitica: insights from paleogenomics

**Presenter:** Margaux Lefebvre, *Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology*

**Abstract:** Yersinia enterocolitica is one of the pathogenic bacteria within the Yersinia genus, alongside Yersinia pseudotuberculosis and Yersinia pestis. In humans, Y. enterocolitica primarily causes yersiniosis, an enteric infection characterized by fever, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and vomiting. In some cases, infection progresses to septicemia, which in modern populations is largely restricted to strains belonging to “virulent” biotypes (1B, 2, 3, and 4). In contrast, biotype 1A, the only group lacking the virulence plasmid pYV, is considered “non-virulent” in humans. Using a dataset of 56 ancient and 249 modern genomes, we show that the relationship between biotype and disease severity has shifted over time. All ancient Y. enterocolitica genomes were recovered from teeth, consistent with septicemia cases, yet all genetically seemed to belong to biotype 1A and lack of the virulence plasmid pYV. Biotype 1A thus appears to have been the dominant lineage associated with invasive infection in the past, potentially explaining its high diversity today. By comparing ancient and modern genomes from biotype 1A, we identify multiple loci associated with virulence that were present or intact in ancient strains but are absent or pseudogenized in modern ones. These patterns indicate a long-term evolutionary trajectory toward attenuated virulence in biotype 1A, rather than solely recent acquisition of pathogenicity in other biotypes. Our results highlight the importance of ancient pathogen genomes for reconstructing past disease severity, and demonstrate how paleogenomics can reveal shifts in pathogen virulence relevant to palaeoepidemiology and host–pathogen coevolution.

## Genomic signatures of increasing disease burden in recent prehistory

**Presenter:** Gili Greenbaum, *The Hebrew University of Jerusalem*

**Abstract:** Diseases induce some of the strongest selection pressures experienced by human populations, leaving deep imprints in immune-related genomic regions. While individual pathogens mainly induce directional selection for resistance alleles, cumulative disease burden is expected to generate balancing selection that increases genetic diversity, particularly in the major histocompatibility complex (MHC). To identify temporal patterns of diversity change, we developed a new pipeline for extracting heterozygosity from ancient genomes, ImpHet, and validated it using a novel framework that mimics aDNA-specific biases by simulating sequencing read structures of ancient genomes. Using these methods, we estimated heterozygosity for 3,847 ancient individuals across all genes and genomic windows and quantified changes over the last 12,000 years, focusing on the cultural centers of Southwestern Eurasia and Eastern Asia. Although MHC genes compose <1% of all genes tested, more than 66% of genes showing significant increases in heterozygosity were located in the MHC, and rates of MHC heterozygosity increase were 16-fold (Southwestern Eurasia) and 7-fold (Eastern Asia) higher than genome-wide rates. Thus, the MHC emerges as the primary genomic region of recent diversity increase. Rates of change for individual genes were highly correlated between cultural centers (r = 0.91), indicating convergent genic-level selection pressures. Finer temporal analyses revealed pronounced peaks in MHC diversity increase around ~7,000bp, coinciding with settlement intensification, increased connectivity, and expanding animal domestication. These results suggest that the most intense disease burden followed the transition to sedentism but preceded urbanization, and support the hypothesis that disease burden increased substantially in recent prehistory.

## Parvovirus B19 from early modern warships suggests genotype 2 continuity in Europe, bridging a millennium-long gap

**Presenter:** Zoé Pochon, *Centre for Palaeogenetics, Stockholm*

**Abstract:** Early modern warships were crowded, highly structured communities that facilitated pathogen transmission and provide a time-bounded setting to contrast multiple introductions with onboard amplification. We performed shotgun sequencing of calcified tissues from 31 sailors from the Mary Rose (n = 19) and Kronan (n = 12) and screened for viruses. Human parvovirus B19 (B19V), which causes fifth disease (slapped-cheek syndrome) and can lead to severe anaemia and foetal complications, was detected in 21 individuals (14/19 and 7/12). We reconstructed two complete B19V genomes and one partial genome and recovered low-coverage fragments from eight additional individuals, with authenticity supported by post-mortem damage, characteristic ssDNA coverage patterns and read-level corroboration. All were assigned to genotype 2, a lineage no longer detected in acute infections where genotype 1 predominates today. Phylogenetic analyses place the three genomes within genotype 2 and show the early modern sequences branching basal to twentieth-century genotype 2 diversity. Together with early medieval European genomes (~1000 CE) and twentieth-century clinical material, these sequences bridge a near-millennium gap in the European record and are consistent with potential genotype 2 continuity over that interval. Notably, within-ship genetic diversity across individuals is inconsistent with a single-source onboard outbreak and instead supports multiple introductions and long-term tissue persistence, aligning with predominantly childhood exposure and decades-long persistence of B19V DNA in bone. Our results show that submerged shipwreck assemblages can preserve authentic viral DNA and can be used to extend time-stamped pathogen genomes in ways that inform hypotheses about past circulation and transmission context.

## Humans as reservoirs: Historical microbiomes uncover hidden zoonotic pathways

**Presenter:** Kristen Rayfield, *Stony Brook University*

**Abstract:** The rise in emerging and re-emerging infectious diseases encourages deeper investigations into human-animal-environment interactions. Zoonotic disease transmission events are most frequently recognized within the terrestrial environment in a single direction of transmission from nonhuman animals to people. However, these human-mediated epidemiological events include marine ecologies. Historical alterations to the marine biome linked to disruptive human activities (e.g., upland conversion, pollutant runoff, and exploitation of marine species) have shaped modern marine mammal populations such as California sea lions (CSL) (Zalophus californianus). Using dental calculus (n=60) subsampled from CSL housed at the Santa Barbara Natural History Museum and at the Smithsonian Institute we reconstruct CSL oral microbiomes and link microbial diversity to anthropogenic alterations. Metagenomic analysis reveal that CSL have maintained a stable and unique oral microbiome signature that is different from other mammalian species; suggesting the oral microbiome may be less prone to alterations than the gut microbiome. CSL host DNA was regularly recovered. We also recovered Klebsiella pneumoniae, a human associated pathogen linked to mortality events in CSL populations. Eight K. pneumoniae-positive samples underwent genome enrichment, yielding 71.17% coverage of the K. pneumonia reference genome at 1X depth in one sample. We provide a novel approach in using dental calculus as a sentinel for documenting shifting historical ecologies. Our analysis demonstrates the potential for recovering pathogen genomic data from marine mammal dental calculus, offering valuable insights into epidemiological events of marine mammals using museum specimens. Incorporating such analyses can open the door in developing datasets to identify unrecognized zoonotic pathways.
