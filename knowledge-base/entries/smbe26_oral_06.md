## tSNP: A dimensionality reduction algorithm for genetic data

**Presenter:** Ori Sharon, The Hebrew University of Jerusalem

**Abstract:**

Population structure reflects non-random genetic relationships shaped by geography, culture, and history. Dimensionality reduction methods, most notably principal component analysis (PCA), are widely used to visualize genome-wide single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) data in two or three dimensions<br />while trying to preserve patterns of population structure. However, these generic methods do not explicitly exploit the ternary nature of SNP genotypes, which encode the count of reference alleles per locus (either 0, 1, or 2), and may therefore fail to capture subtle structure.<br />Here, we introduce tSNP, a dimensionality reduction algorithm specifically tailored to ternary genetic data. It represents SNPs as lines, and genotypes as dots, and finds a two-dimensional embedding that optimizes geometric relationships between them in a way that captures allele-sharing patterns that are<br />informative about population structure. We benchmark its performance against PCA, UMAP, and t-SNE on multiple simulated and empirical datasets, and examine cases where tSNP outperforms other methods in revealing large-scale patterns of population structure. These results indicate that directly exploiting the ternary representation of SNP data is a promising direction for dimensionality reduction in population genetics.

## Rethinking human AMY1 copy number evolution in light of demographic history

**Presenter:** Andrea Soler I Núñez, Uppsala University

**Abstract:**

Dietary change has long been considered a major selective force in human evolution, with increased salivary amylase (AMY1) copy number (CN) often interpreted as an adaptation to starch-rich agricultural diets and cited alongside other genes (e.g. LCT, FADS) as a paradigm of dietary adaptation. Here, we present droplet digital PCR estimates from 390 individuals representing 30 Sub-Saharan populations and combine them with previously published AMY1 CN estimates from 810 to 1307 individuals worldwide, to re-evaluate this model within a phylogenetically informed, ancestry-aware framework. Our analyses indicate that AMY1 CN is not consistently associated with the practice of agriculture across geographical subsets and, when all populations are considered together, the differences in AMY1 CN are significantly smaller than previously reported, complicating arguments for its biological significance. We also find that population structure and shared ancestry account for a considerable part of the observed variation in AMY1 CN worldwide. Lastly, phylogenetic tests further indicate baseline differences in AMY1 CN between Sub-Saharan and non-Sub-Saharan populations, pointing at deep evolutionary processes shaping present-day variation. These results challenge the long-standing “agriculture hypothesis” and put forward demographic history, rather than subsistence strategy, as the primary driver of AMY1 CN evolution worldwide.

## Strong bottlenecks constrain adaptive evolution in a host–parasite metapopulation

**Presenter:** Pascal Angst, University of Basel

**Abstract:**

Two important characteristics of metapopulations are extinction–(re)colonization dynamics and gene flow between subpopulations. Colonization is often associated with population bottlenecks, which lead to low genetic diversity and may constrain the adaptive evolution of a species and its co-dispersed symbionts. We investigated the role of host population structure on host and parasite evolution in a long-term metapopulation study. We used pooled co-sequencing of hosts and parasites to characterize spatiotemporal genomic variation in more than 100 subpopulations of the planktonic crustacean Daphnia magna and its microsporidian parasite Hamiltosporidium tvaerminnensis. Compared to non-metapopulations, we found reduced (synonymous) genomic diversity, weak purifying selection, and low rates of adaptive evolution in the D. magna metapopulation. In the parasite genome, we found subpopulation-specific deleterious runs of homozygosity, which originate from loss of heterozygosity events during asexual recombination, and which are fixed in subpopulations following host-mediated parasite population bottlenecks when host and parasite co-disperse. These findings support the idea that genetic bottlenecks are a main driver of evolution in highly dynamic metapopulations. Thus, we shed light on the genomic consequences of extinction–(re)colonization dynamics to an unprecedented degree, giving a genomic perspective of the effects of colonization on genome-wide allele frequencies. Our results demonstrate that the focal metapopulation evolves differently from non-metapopulations and that ignoring the effects of metapopulation dynamics in population genomic studies can lead to strongly biased inferences.

## Fine-scale genotyping reveals assemblage-wide introgression in ants

**Presenter:** Guillaume Lavanchy, Lund University

**Abstract:**

Introgression following hybridization is increasingly recognized as an important driver of species and genome evolution. However, its relevance for evolution depends on its frequency, which has been challenging to measure. To provide a snapshot of the amount of introgression ongoing in nature, we genotyped a whole assemblage consisting of 4126 ant colonies. 23.2% of all 82 local species showed signs of introgression, which is more than twice higher than previously thought. Both the amount of introgression (0.001% – 17.3%) and its contribution to genetic diversity (-0.16% – 29%) were variable among species. Classical prezygotic reproductive barriers (mating phenology, ecological niche, fine-scale habitat use) did not predict introgression, suggesting that the main barriers to gene flow in these ants are postzygotic.

## Deep-Time History of Balkan Brown Bears

**Presenter:** Danijela Popović, Centre of New Technologies, University of Warsaw

**Abstract:**

The brown bear (Ursus arctos) is an iconic megafaunal species that survived the Late Pleistocene extinctions. Once widespread across Europe, its range has been drastically reduced by intensive hunting and large-scale habitat loss. Today, European brown bears persist in ten fragmented populations, three of which occur in the Balkans, with Serbia likely forming a historical contact zone among them. Our ongoing project investigates the evolutionary history of Balkan brown bears over the past 50,000 years by integrating genomic analyses of ancient and modern DNA with stable isotope analysis.<br />Preliminary analyses of Holocene specimens from Serbia reveal unexpectedly high mitochondrial DNA diversity, indicating that past genetic variation greatly exceeded that of present-day populations. To investigate this further, we are generating new whole-genome sequence data from ancient and modern individuals and extending our temporal range to include Late Pleistocene and Holocene material. These genomic data are being integrated with information from contemporary populations to reconstruct long-term genetic change.<br />This interdisciplinary framework allows us to reconstruct temporal and spatial patterns of demographic change, ancestry, admixture, and migration. Stable isotope analyses of bone collagen provide insight into diet and environmental conditions, while direct radiocarbon dating establishes a robust chronological framework. Together, these approaches will clarify how brown bears persisted in the region over millennia, deepen our understanding of long-term population dynamics, and inform future conservation strategies.<br />The project is funded by the National Science Centre, Poland (2024/53/B/NZ8/02682).

## Local genomic adaptation to rabies in raccoons

**Presenter:** Taylor Ackley, North Carolina State University

**Abstract:**

Zoonotic diseases pose a major risk to human, domestic animal, and wildlife health, especially in an increasingly connected world. High-mortality pathogens act as a strong selective pressure and can drive local adaptation in wildlife reservoir species. Rabies virus is present in over 150 countries and is virtually 100% fatal in most mammalian species if left untreated. It is maintained by various bat and carnivore reservoirs including the raccoon, a reservoir for raccoon rabies virus variant (RRV) in eastern North America. Evidence suggests that eastern raccoon populations exhibit increased tolerance and survival of RRV after nearly 70 years of its presence in these populations. To investigate the genomic basis for adaptation to rabies in raccoons, we used whole-genome sequencing to test for genomic signatures of positive selection.We detected genome-wide evidence of selection in RRV-endemic populations, identifying 148 candidate genes under selection. Functional enrichment analyses revealed significant overrepresentation of pathways related to neuronal transport, viral entry and replication, translational control, and innate immune signaling. By revealing diverse molecular targets of selection, this study advances understanding of host–virus coevolution in natural populations and highlights how reservoir species may evolve resilience to highly lethal zoonotic pathogens, with important implications for disease maintenance and management.<br />

## From Core to Periphery: Mapping Evolutionary Potential Across Species Ranges

**Presenter:** Anna Brüniche-Olsen, University Of Copenhagen

**Abstract:**

Peripheral populations are often thought to exhibit reduced genetic diversity and increased genetic differentiation due to smaller effective population sizes and greater isolation, yet this widely accepted idea—central to the center-periphery hypothesis—has rarely been tested using whole-genome data. Understanding how genomic diversity, inbreeding, and mutational load are distributed across species’ ranges is critical for evaluating the evolutionary potential of populations under environmental change. Here, we integrate whole-genome sequencing data from 88 avian species with geographic range data to investigate how spatial position within a range affects key genomic metrics. By combining estimates of heterozygosity, inbreeding, and genomic load with spatial measures of peripherality, we aim to disentangle the effects of demography, drift, and selection across biogeographic gradients. This work provides a foundation for understanding how evolutionary processes shape genetic variation within species.

## Positive Selection Across Scales: Genetic Architecture of Adaptation and Connectivity in Wild Sunflowers

**Presenter:** Zhiqin Long, The University Of British Columbia

**Abstract:**

Background<br />Positive selection is a key evolutionary force driving adaptive evolution across multiple biogeographic scales, with different evolutionary consequences. At local scales, positive selection can promote local adaptation by accumulating advantageous mutations within populations, thereby contributing to local divergence. At broader scales, it may facilitate the spread of advantageous alleles across populations, thereby connecting populations in geographically widespread species or even between closely related species. Moreover, positive selection may underlie parallel adaptation among species exposed to similar abiotic or biotic conditions. The genetic architecture underlying these adaptive processes is expected to vary. Migration–selection balance theory predicts that genes involved in local adaptation will exhibit concentrated architectures and higher pleiotropy to counteract the homogenizing effects of migration, whereas genes repeatedly involved in species-wide or interspecific sweeps may show reduced pleiotropy, as complex architectures can slow adaptive responses. Species-wide sweeps are also predicted to be enriched for genes involved in genetic conflict. In this study, we ask: (1) how positive selection contributes to the adaptation and connectivity of species across multiple biogeographic scales, including local, species-wide, and interspecific scales, and (2) how genetic architecture varies with adaptive process and geographic scale.<br /><br />Methods<br />We analyzed a whole-genome resequencing dataset of 1,229 individuals from four Helianthus species, with distributions ranging from narrow (H. argophyllus, 297 individuals) to intermediate (H. petiolaris ssp. petiolaris, 144; ssp. fallax, 199) to wide (H. annuus, 589). To detect locally adaptive loci, we applied Wavelet Spatial Genetic analysis (Lasky et al., 2024), which captures spatial patterns of genetic relatedness across multiple scales without discretizing populations or knowing selective pressures in advance. To identify species-wide and interspecific selective sweeps, we integrated a haplotype-based statistics with a site frequency spectrum–based method. Tissue specificity of genes was evaluated using RNA-seq data from five tissues in H. annuus to estimate levels of pleiotropy. Gene family duplication events were inferred using OrthoFinder3 based on chromosome-level genome assemblies.<br /><br />Preliminary Results<br />Our results identified thousands of locally adaptive genes across all four species. Functional annotation reveals strong enrichment for genes involved in abiotic stress responses. These candidates significantly overlap with genes identified in previous genotype–environment association (GEA) analyses (Todesco et al., 2020), supporting the interpretation that heterogeneous patterns of genetic relatedness often arise in response to local environmental pressures. These genes also show higher pleiotropy than genomic background genes, consistent with theoretical expectations. At species-wide and interspecific scales, globally adaptive genes are enriched for functions related to chromosomal segregation, cell cycle regulation, defense, and reproduction, and are strongly shared across species, supporting pervasive parallel evolution. Contrary to expectations, globally adaptive genes exhibit elevated pleiotropy and are enriched in low-duplication gene families, suggesting selection may favor genes with larger effects. Notably, globally adaptive genes overlap substantially with locally adaptive genes at broader scales, indicating that positive selection may facilitate the spread of beneficial alleles and promote connectivity among populations and species.<br /><br />Overall, our results highlight positive selection as a key evolutionary force shaping diverse biological processes, with strong parallelism across species. The genetic architecture of adaptation is consistent across multiple geographic scales.

## Massive genetic diversity loss within Asian elephant subspecies: new insights for determining conservation priorities

**Presenter:** Jeroen Kappelhof, Wageningen University & Research

**Abstract:**

In this study, we present a comprehensive genomic analysis of population structure, genetic diversity, and genetic load across the four recognized Asian elephant (Elephas maximus) subspecies using whole-genome sequencing data from 81 wild-born individuals. We reveal striking contrasts in inbreeding among subspecies. The Bornean elephant (E. m. borneensis) shows the highest inbreeding, with approximately 65% of the genome occurring in runs of homozygosity (ROH), and the lowest genome-wide heterozygosity, averaging ~46 heterozygous sites per 100 kb. The Sumatran subspecies (E. m. sumatranus) displays intermediate inbreeding (35% ROH) and heterozygosity (~101 sites per 100 kb), whereas the mainland and Sri Lankan subspecies (E. m. indicus and E. m. maximus) exhibit similarly low inbreeding (~10–11% ROH) and high heterozygosity (~130–134 sites per 100 kb). When ROH regions are excluded, heterozygosity levels converge across all subspecies (~136–155 sites per 100 kb), indicating that observed differences in genetic diversity are primarily driven by inbreeding-associated loss of heterozygosity rather than post-divergence accumulation of unique variation. Despite pronounced inbreeding, genetic load remains relatively low in all subspecies, including E. m. borneensis, particularly within highly conserved genomic regions, consistent with effective purging of deleterious alleles. The unexpectedly high genetic diversity of the island-dwelling E. m. maximus likely reflects historical gene flow associated with intensive human-mediated elephant trade, raising questions about its current taxonomic status. Given its extreme inbreeding and low genetic diversity, E. m. borneensis emerges as the highest conservation priority. Our results suggest genetic rescue as a viable management option, with limited risk of outbreeding depression.

## The genomic basis and evolution of maternal genetic effects on early-life immunity in Soay sheep

**Presenter:** Gina Henderson, University Of Edinburgh

**Abstract:**

Maternal effects, where a mother’s genetics and environment influence her offspring’s phenotype beyond the direct effects of inherited alleles, are a key source of heritable variation and can shape evolutionary potential in natural populations. One important example is the transfer of antibodies from mothers to offspring, providing crucial early-life immune protection but at a physiological cost that may constrain immune evolution. Despite their importance, the genetic basis and evolutionary consequences of maternal effects in generating immune variation remain poorly understood. We investigate the genomic basis and evolutionary consequences of maternal genetic effects on early-life immunity in wild Soay sheep (Ovis aries), naturally infected with the gastrointestinal parasite Teladorsagia circumcincta. Anti-parasite immunoglobulin A (IgA) plays a central role in defence against this parasite and exhibits high heritability and genetic variation in adults (h² = 0.57, Iᴀ = 0.38). Using >3,500 IgA measures from newborn lambs, we show that maternal genetic effects explain a significant proportion of phenotypic variation in neonatal IgA (Mg² = 0.31), while the lambs' own genetic contribution is effectively zero (h² = 0.007). Genome-wide association analyses reveal that variation in lamb IgA is partly driven by a large-effect locus on chromosome 12 in maternal genomes, identifying a specific genomic mechanism underlying maternal immune effects in the wild. However, despite a clear genetic basis to maternal antibody transfer, there was no evidence of selection acting on this region. These results provide new insight into the genetic basis and evolution of maternal immune effects in the wild.

## Assessing dog introgression through structural variants in European refugial wolf populations

**Presenter:** Daniele Battilani, Fondazione Edmund Mach

**Abstract:**

European wolves have recently undergone a remarkable demographic recovery, however the potential conservation implications of wolf-dog hybridization across a broad range of genomic variation remains unclear. For example, wolf-dog hybridization can alter the adaptive potential of wild wolf populations, yet its genomic consequences have been inferred from single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) alone. Here, we tested whether structural variants (SVs) capture patterns of admixture and introgression between wolves and dogs in three European wolf populations from glacial refugia (Dinaric-Balkan, Iberian, and Italian), where wolf-dog hybridization has been relevant over the last 30 years. Moreover, we included a broad representation of domestic dogs, plus Karelian wolves and three outgroup species to infer introgression. Admixed and non-admixed individuals were first identified using genome-wide SNP-based analyses, providing an independent reference to assess SV-based signals. Then, we characterized deletions, duplications, and inversions and implemented tailored dual approaches to identify putative recent and historical dog introgression separately within each wolf population and SV category. Deletions and duplications captured broad-scale population structure and revealed signatures of genome-wide admixture, although with lower resolution than SNPs, whereas inversions only distinguished wolves from dogs. We identified both putatively recent and historical dog introgression, highlighting heterogeneous dynamics across refugia. Gene ontology analyses of candidate introgressed SVs suggested potential functional roles that might have shaped physiology, behavior and ecology during population evolution and following recent admixture. Overall, our results show that SVs provide a complementary perspective to SNPs, adding an additional layer for disentangling the genomic impacts of wolf-dog hybridization.

## Genomic signatures of recent inbreeding are associated with rarity and habitat specialisation among flies of the UK.

**Presenter:** Elisa Mogollon Perez, University Of Oxford

**Abstract:**

While monitoring programs have revealed dramatic shifts in population size and distribution in various focal insect species, the status of many insect populations remains unknown. Diptera (flies) are a ubiquitous and ecologically diverse order of insects, and while there is growing evidence of community-level declines, much of the knowledge we have on species-specific populations trends is anecdotal. At the same time, the growing availability of high-quality genomes offers new opportunities to investigate demographic history and evolutionary resilience in wild populations. Using reference genomes from the Darwin Tree of Life survey of eukaryotic genomes in Britain and Ireland, I examine patterns of genetic diversity in 150 UK fly species spanning the Dipteran phylogeny. I identify genetic variants within each reference individual and quantify genome-wide heterozygosity and runs of homozygosity (ROHs) as a measure of recent inbreeding. My analyses reveal that inbreeding is associated with observed species rarity and habitat specialisation, but not body size or trophic level, when accounting for phylogenetic relatedness. Forward-in-time simulations show that a demographic scenario of recent population decline can recapitulate genome-wide patterns of ROHs observed in several species. Together, these analyses demonstrate how single, high-quality genomes can provide valuable insights into the genetic status and potential evolutionary resilience of species across an ecologically diverse clade of insects.

## Is genetic differentiation between species homogeneous across mammals?

**Presenter:** Mathilde Barthe, Cnrs

**Abstract:**

Speciation progressively reduces gene flow between lineages, leading to increasing genetic differentiation. In the absence of a widely accepted threshold for species delimitation, it has been proposed to use genetic differentiation observed among already recognized taxa as a comparative benchmark. However, this approach assumes that genomic divergence and population structure are reliable proxies for taxonomic rank and that such thresholds can be transferred across independent species complexes. To test this assumption, we examined how genetic differentiation varies across taxonomic levels. We compiled 69 publicly available whole-genome assemblies spanning eight mammalian genera. We estimated pairwise sequence divergence (Da and Dxy) and genomic differentiation using FST-like Index. Species-level pairs consistently exhibited higher FST, Da, and Dxy values than pairs belonging to lower taxonomic ranks. These results indicate that genomic differentiation metrics broadly track taxonomic rank and may provide an informative guide for species delimitation. We further applied this comparative framework to an emblematic taxonomic case, the black-backed jackal, Lupulella mesomelas, which includes two geographically disjunct populations in Eastern and Southern Africa. Our findings illustrate how genome-wide divergence metrics can help refine species boundaries.

## Effects of habitat arrangement on gene flow and genetic diversity in a grassland-specialist butterfly (Cupido minimus)

**Presenter:** Danielle Clake, Lund University

**Abstract:**

Loss of biodiversity is a global concern with many contributing factors, including human-driven land cover change and the resulting habitat loss. The impacts of habitat arrangement have also received much attention in recent years following a meta-analysis indicating that habitat fragmentation could have positive outcomes for biodiversity after accounting for the impacts of habitat loss. While the effects of habitat arrangement have been examined for variables such as species abundance and species richness, few studies have explored impacts on gene flow and genetic diversity. We address how habitat amount and arrangement influence functional connectivity and genetic erosion in pollinators, focusing on the grassland specialist butterfly Cupido minimus. This species exhibits population structure at distances of less than 8 km. Based on whole genome sequencing of 200 individuals collected from 40 sites in southern Sweden we estimate functional connectivity by assessing FST and clustering. We then compare the genetic distances between populations to the amount and arrangement of habitat separating them. We also assess the effects of habit amount and habitat arrangement surrounding each population on level of inbreeding, approximated by runs of homozygosity and accumulation of genetic burden (genomic inference of deleterious mutations). The insights gained through the landscape genomic methods in this study are important both for conservation planning in areas with finite space available for habitat, and for better understanding how landscape patterns can influence functional connectivity, with consequences for the prospects of population persistence.

## Adaptive evolution under extreme genetic erosion in invasive Spotted Lanternfly

**Presenter:** Fang Meng, New York University

**Abstract:**

Loss of genetic diversity is increasingly recognised as a fundamental component of the global biodiversity crisis, yet its evolutionary consequences remain poorly understood. This uncertainty is particularly acute in biological invasions, where potential founding events, and rapid environmental change impose extreme genetic erosion, yet adaptive evolution and population expansion frequently occur in novel environments. Here, we use whole-genome sequencing to examine the interplay between urbanization, genetic erosion, demographic history, and natural selection during the invasion of the spotted lanternfly (Lycorma delicatula). <br />We analysed genome-wide variation from native populations in Shanghai, China, and invasive populations across the northeastern United States. Demographic reconstruction reveals three sequential bottlenecks associated with urbanisation in the native range and subsequent invasion events, resulting in pronounced reductions in genome-wide heterozygosity, elevated inbreeding, and extensive runs of homozygosity in the invasive population. Despite severe genetic erosion, we detect strong signatures of natural selection in both native and invasive ranges. In the native range, urban and rural populations show genomic divergence at loci associated with stress response, metabolism, immunity, and detoxification, indicating habitat-specific selection prior to invasion. In the invasive range, genotype–environment associations and population genomic scans identify ongoing selection linked to climatic and anthropogenic gradients. Notably, heterozygosity at loci under selection is maintained despite genome-wide diversity loss, suggesting that adaptive genetic variation can persist under extreme demographic constraint. Together, these results demonstrate that evolutionary processes remain active in populations experiencing substantial genetic erosion and highlight how environmental change may shape molecular evolution prior to and during biological invasions.

## Gene expression and disease susceptibility of the genetically depauperate Channel Island fox (Urocyon littoralis)

**Presenter:** Kimberly A Schoenberger, University of Southern California

**Abstract:**

Although conservation of endangered species is often focused on the time-sensitive aspect of increasing population numbers to avoid extinction, it is important to examine the unintended side effects if such efforts are successful. The Channel Island fox (Urocyon littoralis) is a species that approached near extinction in the early 2000s, with population declines up to 90% in four of its six island-specific subspecies. Remarkably, through interventions including captive breeding, numbers have increased substantially, with all time highs in recent years. However, the species’ health and longevity remains at risk from genetic complications associated with long-term small population size as well as recent bottlenecks. One of the biggest health concerns is high levels of ear mite infections documented in three island subspecies. Specifically, inflammation from mites has been associated with development of ear tumors in Catalina Island foxes, but not in other similarly mite-infected subspecies. This is especially noteworthy because the original Catalina population decline was also driven by disease, specifically canine distemper virus. Our research explores underlying genetic factors contributing to the unique disease susceptibility of Catalina foxes through RNA-sequencing of foxes on all six islands in varying health and infection conditions. Preliminary Weighted Gene Co-Expression Network Analysis (WGCNA) shows individuals with early tumor indicators clustering distinctly from those with only mite infection, with key differentiation in genes associated with inflammation response and cancer development. Additional analysis is underway, seeking to further explore these pathways and identify specific immune differences contributing to tumor and disease development in Catalina foxes.
