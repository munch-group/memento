## Evolution of a Diving Response: Divergent Transcriptomic Adaptations in Aquatic Versus Terrestrial Mammals.

**Presenter:** Giovanna Selleghin-Veiga, University of Campinas

**Abstract:**

Some mammalian lineages, such as cetaceans and pinnipeds, transitioned from land to sea and evolved unique adaptations to this new environment. One key adaptation is the diving response, a series of physiological and metabolic adjustments that maximizes oxygen use during dives. This process can lead to extremely low blood saturation, leading to tissue hypoxia followed by reoxygenation upon surfacing. Although hypoxia/reoxygenation can induce oxidative stress by generating reactive oxygen species, these marine mammals show no evidence of associated tissue damage, indicating highly efficient protective mechanisms. Here, we investigated the basis of this adaptation by analysing transcriptomes of cultured cells exposed to treatments that mimic diving. The expression profiles varied across species, ranging from a modulated response in deep divers to a nonspecific response in terrestrial species. In the treatments, only marine mammals presented differentially expressed genes with enriched GO terms related to response to oxygen levels. Meanwhile, the terrestrial species responded by modulating energy metabolism and mitochondrial function, reflecting insufficient oxygen to meet cellular demands. Finally, deep diving species showed the top upregulated genes involved in the response to oxygen levels, indicating the higher effort allocated in protective mechanisms compared to non-adapted species. Overall, we characterized the cellular strategies that enable marine mammals to respond to oxidative stress in chronic cycles of hypoxia and reoxygenation. Our experimental data revealed how their cells evolved a targeted and efficient response that prevents oxidative damage, distinguishing them from terrestrial species lacking these adaptations.

## The Vertebrate Genomes Project Phase I: A global reference genome resource

**Presenter:** Giulio Formenti, The Rockefeller University

**Abstract:**

The Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP) aims to produce complete and near error-free reference genomes for all ~70,000 vertebrate species. Organized in four phases, it progressively targets all vertebrate orders, families, genera, and eventually all species. Here we present the completion of VGP Phase I, which includes a representative reference genome for over 90% of vertebrate orders, and for additional species deeper within those orders, totaling over 600 species from around the planet and 1.2 trillion base pairs of primary haplotype sequence. These genomes were assembled and annotated between 2018-2026, contributing to a period of rapid evolution in genome sequencing, assembly, and annotation methods, multiple scientific initiatives, and international partnerships. Among the highest quality available, most have become the primary references of the species in the public databases. The emerging analyses across these more complete vertebrate genomes, revealed the reconstructed common ancestor genome of all vertebrates, diverse sex chromosome evolution, genome structure complexity and 3D architecture specific to each vertebrate clade, epigenetic patterns specific to and universal to all vertebrates, and more accurate understanding of gene families evolution, such as complex immunoglobulin loci, odorant receptors, miRNAs, and cancer-associated genes. Of the species sequenced, 27% are listed as Vulnerable to Critically Endangered by the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species, enabling us to characterize species extinction risk. The VGP Phase I collection of complete to near complete genomes now allows unprecedented investigations, opening up a window into what it will be like to have a representative DNA collection of all extant life.

## Comparative Population Genomics of Two Pipefish Species in the Baltic Sea

**Presenter:** Eli Taub, University Of Copenhagen

**Abstract:**

Understanding how species exist across variable environmental conditions is crucial for predicting how environmental change will impact their future viability. Marine species in the Baltic Sea present a system for exploring evolution to novel environmental conditions, since the Baltic Sea has existed only within the last few thousand years and has different salinity and temperature from the neighboring North Sea. Here, we examine the population genomics of two pipefish species in the order Syngnathiformes (Syngnathus typhle and Nerophis ophidion) which occur across the Northeast Atlantic Ocean and Baltic Sea, sampling individuals across an environmental gradient of 7 sites where the species co-occur. We assess the population structure, admixture, and rates of genetic variation between the sites, relating these metrics to the environmental conditions of the sites and the evolutionary history of these species. By comparing the population genetics of the two species, we aim to explore the parallels and dissimilarities in how the species evolved in response to novel environmental conditions and their present-day population dynamics, helping to understand the current biogeography of these species and their potential to adapt to future environmental change.

## Comparative genomics reveals chemosensory gene family evolution within a conserved genome architecture in Culex

**Presenter:** Sonia Cebrián-Camisón, Estación Biológica De Doñana (ebd-csic)

**Abstract:**

Culex mosquitoes are major vectors of West Nile virus and other pathogens affecting humans and wildlife. However, their evolutionary genomics, particularly genome-wide architecture and gene family evolution, remains poorly studied beyond Culex pipiens complex. Here, we apply a comparative genomics approach across Culex species present in the Mediterranean basin (Culex pipiens forms pipiens and molestus, Culex modestus, Culex perexiguus, Culex laticinctus, and Culex theileri) and other members from Culex pipiens complex (Culex quinquefasciatus and Culex pipiens pallens). We integrate phylogenomics, genome-wide synteny and comparative analyses of gene families to investigate genome evolution and chemosensory diversification.<br />Using eight high-quality reference genomes, we resolved their evolutionary relationships and clarified the placement of closely related members of the Culex pipiens complex. Synteny analyses revealed conserved chromosomal architecture with limited rearrangements between taxonomic units, indicating that diversification in ecological traits such as host preference have evolved against a stable genomic background. Chemosensory gene families showed interspecific variation in gene repertoires while maintaining similar genomic organization. Odorant-binding proteins (OBPs), olfactory receptors (ORs) and gustatory receptors (GRs) showed conserved clustered distributions but substantial differences in gene family sizes. Culex quinquefasciatus and Culex pipiens pallens exhibited the largest gene repertoires, while species outside the Culex pipiens complex consistently displayed smaller gene family sizes. <br />These results clarify evolutionary relationships within Culex and highlight the dynamic evolution of chemosensory gene families within a conserved genome structure. Our findings reveal gene family turnover as an important driver of diversification within Culex, offering new insights into sensory ecology and vector-associated traits.<br />

## A deep-learning-based score to evaluate multiple sequence alignments

**Presenter:** Ksenia Polonsky, Tel Aviv University

**Abstract:**

Multiple sequence alignment (MSA) inference is a central task in molecular evolution and comparative genomics, and the reliability of downstream analyses, including phylogenetic inference, depends critically on alignment quality. Despite this importance, most widely used MSA methods optimize the sum-of-pairs (SP) score, and relatively little attention has been paid to whether this objective function accurately reflects alignment accuracy. Here, we evaluate the performance of the SP score using simulated and empirical benchmark alignments. For each dataset, we compare alternative MSAs derived from the same unaligned sequences and quantify the relationship between their SP scores and their distances from a reference alignment. We show that the alignment with the optimal SP score often does not correspond to the most accurate alignment. To address this limitation, we develop deep-learning–based scoring functions that integrate a collection of MSA features. We first introduce Model 1, a regression model that predicts the distance of a given MSA from the reference alignment. Across simulated and empirical datasets, this learned score correlates more strongly with true alignment accuracy than the SP score. However, Model 1 is less effective at identifying the best alignment among alternatives. We therefore develop Model 2, which takes as input a set of alternative MSAs generated from the same sequences and predicts their relative ranking. Model 2 more accurately identifies the top-ranking MSA than the SP score, Model 1, and several widely used alignment programs. Using simulations, we show that selecting MSAs based on our approach leads to more accurate phylogenetic reconstructions.

## Using petabase-scale sequence alignment to describe the host and phylogenetic diversity of microsporidian parasites 

**Presenter:** Aaron Reinke, University of Toronto

**Abstract:**

Microsporidia are large group of parasites that are suggested to infect most types of animals. Using a recently developed resource of assembled publicly available sequencing data, we have performed large scale sequence alignment, discovering over 15,000 data sets containing microsporidia sequences. Over 50% of previously reported microsporidia 18S SSU RNA sequences are found in our data and we identify 18S sequences representing over 1300 novel species of microsporidia. Our data shows the diversity of animals and environments where microsporidia are found, including several phyla of animals not previously reported to be infected by microsporidia. We also identified several novel species of microsporidia associated with humans and laboratory mice. Data sets containing microsporidia protein coding genes were identified, and at least 10% complete genomes were recovered from over 4000 datasets. These genomes are from about 400 species and all 6 major clades as well as the minor clades Caudosporida and Areosporida are represented. No novel clades are identified, but many novel groups are observed within the major clades. Arthropods serve as hosts across all clades, and we observe phylogenetic groups of microsporidia species that show enrichment for specific arthropod classes or animal phyla. For many microsporidia species we recover dozens of strains including many high-quality genomes for Enterocytozoon bieneusi, which phylogenetically clusters in a host specific manner. Together our analysis enhances our understanding of the sequence and host diversity of microsporidia and provides a foundation for studying microsporidia genome evolution.

## CMAPLE 2: High-Performance Phylogenetic Inference for Millions of Pathogen Genomes

**Presenter:** Nhan Ly-Trong, The Australian National University

**Abstract:**

Phylogenetic analysis is essential to genomic epidemiology, for example, in tracing the origin and evolution of SARS-CoV-2 variants during the COVID-19 pandemic. We previously introduced CMAPLE, an efficient phylogenetic inference method integrated into the IQ-TREE software, capable of reconstructing phylogenetic trees from up to one million SARS-CoV-2 genomes. However, CMAPLE is a sequential algorithm and hence cannot cope with the rapidly growing volume of pathogen data.<br />Here, we present CMAPLE 2, which parallelises tree search through a parallel sample placement algorithm and a parallel subtree pruning and regrafting (SPR) algorithm. CMAPLE 2 also improves memory efficiency by compressing data structures using multiple references along the tree instead of a single reference genome. In addition, CMAPLE 2 implements SPR-based Tree Assessment (SPRTA), an efficient and interpretable approach for assessing confidence in phylogenetic trees. When inferring a one-million-genome SARS-CoV-2 phylogeny using 48 CPU cores, CMAPLE 2 reduces runtime from 11 days (with sequential CMAPLE) to 17 hours (a 16-fold speedup) while decreasing memory usage from 15.4 GB to 11 GB of RAM. CMAPLE 2 can now analyse four million SARS-CoV-2 genomes within 12 days using 41 GB of RAM, a task that the sequential CMAPLE cannot complete.<br /><br />References<br />- Ly-Trong et al. CMAPLE: efficient phylogenetic inference in the pandemic era. Mol Biol Evol, 2024<br />- De Maio et al. Assessing phylogenetic confidence at pandemic scales. Nature, 2025<br />- De Maio et al. Rate variation and recurrent sequence errors in pandemic-scale phylogenetics. Nat Methods, in press, 2025

## New genome assemblies from neglected phyla illuminate the evolution of Ecdysozoa

**Presenter:** Alessandro Formaggioni, University Of Copenhagen

**Abstract:**

Ecdysozoa is the second largest clade within Metazoa and includes eight phyla. Little is known about the early evolution of the clade. Morphological features would group Priapulida, Kinorhyncha, and Loricifera into the clade Scalidophora. Moreover, the organization of the scalidophoran nervous system closely resembles that of Nematoda and Nematomorpha, leading to the proposal of the clade Cycloneuralia. Both hypotheses have been questioned by phylogenomic studies, opening a discussion on the evolution of ecdysozoan traits in relation to the inter-phyla relationships.<br />Previous phylogenies have mostly relied on transcriptomic data, particularly for phyla that are exclusively microscopic (i.e., Loricifera, Kinorhyncha and Tardigrada). For specimens smaller than 1mm, the DNA yields obtained after the extraction cannot meet the minimum requirements for long-reads sequencing. Therefore, long-reads high quality assemblies can only be achieved through amplification of the extracted DNA. <br />In this project we produced genome assemblies of six microscopic ecdysozoan: one Tardigrada (Florarctus antillensis), two Priapulida (Tubiluchus corallicora and Meiopriapulus fijiensis), two Kinorhyncha (Setaphyes dentatus and Echinoderes sp.) and two Loricifera (Armorloricus elegans and Nanoloricus valdemari), with the latter four being the first assemblies representing their phylum. For each specimen, the DNA extract has been amplified using two approaches, the long-range PCR and the multiple displacement amplifications. Combining the two methods, we achieved contiguous (N50 > 1 Mb) and complete (BUSCO > 90%) genomes from single microscopic individuals. With the inclusion of the new genomes, we will assess hypotheses such as Scalidophora and Cycloneuralia and characterize the evolution of pathways underlying key ecdysozoan traits. <br />

## Testing the adequacy and the accuracy of models of amino-acid substitution across evolutionary timescales

**Presenter:** Mattia Giacomelli, University Of Barcelona

**Abstract:**

Models of amino-acid substitution are central to phylogenomic inference, yet their performance remains insufficiently tested. Here, we use simulation-based analyses to address two complementary aspects of model performance: model adequacy and phylogenetic accuracy. First, using empirical datasets spanning diverse evolutionary depths, we assess model adequacy with respect to across-site compositional heterogeneity, employing complementary metrics that capture both site-level diversity and the amino-acid frequency spectrum. In parallel, we perform targeted simulations to test the ability of widely used amino-acid substitution models to recover known topologies under varying degrees of compositional heterogeneity. When real datasets exhibit across-site compositional heterogeneity, site-homogeneous models consistently perform poorly in both adequacy and accuracy. Site-heterogeneous mixture models perform substantially better, with infinite-mixture models showing the best overall performance. Importantly, accuracy under infinite-mixture models remains high even when datasets are across-site compositionally homogeneous, due to their ability to effectively auto-tune toward simpler models. Together, our results demonstrate that evaluating both model adequacy and accuracy is essential for robust phylogenomic inference.

## An updated perspective: what genes make a tree a tree?

**Presenter:** Siri Birkeland, Natural History Museum, University of Oslo

**Abstract:**

We learn early on how to tell trees apart from other plants. However, it has proved hard to distinguish trees from other plants at the genetic level, and it is believed that there are no unique ‘tree genes’. With the rapid increase in available tree genomes, we can perform new comparative and evolutionary analyses of plant life histories and growth forms. Here we provide a fresh perspective on the genetic foundation for woodiness and perenniality in angiosperms by analyzing selection pressures and gene family evolution in the rosids using genomic data. We examine genes distinguishing trees from herbs and discuss future directions for uncovering the genetic factors that define a tree in this new era of tree genomics.

## A macroevolutionary gene network reveals diapause evolutionary dynamics beyond the circadian clock and predicts microevolution

**Presenter:** Saurav Baral, Stockholm University

**Abstract:**

Diapause is an alternative developmental pathway evolved independently in many insects to synchronize life cycles with resource abundance. While subsets of this essential phenotype have long been studied at a single species level, the genomic basis of the full diapause syndrome remains poorly understood. Remaining unknown is whether convergent diapause syndromes employ shared mechanisms. This paucity of insights has fueled a long-standing debate about how life cycle synchronization evolves. Using a comparative genomic analysis spanning diverse diapause transitions in butterflies, we identified a large network of coevolving genes unique to diapausing species. The network is composed of functional modules spanning circadian regulation, metabolism, and cell cycle control. We tested whether this macroevolutionary scale network predicts microevolutionary dynamics, finding that allelic variation in the diapause network is significantly enriched in signatures of local adaptation across latitudes, but only in diapausing species. Thus, we empirically show that diapause evolves through modular, coevolving gene networks, components of which regulate species/population specific diapause phenotypes. This novel perspective on this complex phenotype opens a new horizon of inquiry for understanding seasonal adaptation across evolutionary scales, while demonstrating the power of using comparative genomics to dissect polygenic phenotypes.<br />

## The Toll-like receptor signalling pathway repertoire of cartilaginous fishes: loss of TLR4-LPS signalling as a case study

**Presenter:** Zewei Yang, UCD

**Abstract:**

Cartilaginous fishes (sharks, rays, and chimaeras) represent the earliest-diverging lineage of jawed vertebrates relative to mammals. Studies of their immune gene families have differentially shown both conservation and rapid evolution on both the level of sequence similarity and repertoire diversification through duplication and loss. Until recently however, fragmented genome assemblies have complicated both annotation and cross-species comparisons. Toll-like receptor (TLR) 4, which is an essential receptor for mammalian recognition of the lipopolysaccharide on the outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria, has previously been suggested to be absent from the elephant shark genome. Here, taking advantage of newly available chromosome-level genome assemblies from representatives spanning the major lineages cartilaginous fish lineages, and taking the TLR4-LPS pathway as a case study, we evaluate whether a TLR4-LPS signalling pathway is present in cartilaginous fishes. Employing careful homology searches, phylogenetics and synteny analyses we construct a TLR backbone tree and classify TLR orthologs and copy-number variations across vertebrates. We also examine the repertoire evolution of TLR signalling components in cartilaginous fishes compared to other vertebrate lineages. Our findings suggest that TLR4 and associated LPS-specific signalling molecules are absent in cartilaginous fishes, whereas many other signalling molecules and TLRs appear present. This has important implications for our understanding of the cartilaginous fish immune system and possibly for the perceived importance of LPS recognition for vertebrates. We are performing complementary functional analyses and are applying our approach to other immune gene families.

## Tripling known fungal mitogenomes through large-scale public data reuse

**Presenter:** Ammar Abdalrahem, Research and Development Institute (IRD)

**Abstract:**

Many fungal sequence datasets are generated for different scientific purposes and are deposited in public repositories, yet their mitochondrial genome remains largely unexplored. Fungal mitochondrial genomes are critical for understanding phylogenetics, evolution, and ecology of the Kingdom Fungi. Public sequence repositories therefore provide a large resource of genomic data. We developed a reproducible workflow for retrieving and assembling mitochondrial genomes from 12,902 fungal short-read sequencing datasets publicly available in the Sequence Read Archive (SRA), resulting in complete circular genomes representing 2,695 unique species. This effort expanded fungal mitochondrial genome diversity by nearly 3X. The new dataset contains novel yet undescribed mitochondrial genomes at numerous taxonomic levels, including 15 classes, 64 orders, 178 families, and 544 genera. Taxonomic analysis showed wide-ranging ecological representation, including human pathogens, plant pathogens, edible mushrooms, and industrial species. We faced different layers of challenges while working at a public repository scale, including variable sequence quality, lack of metadata information, incorrect taxonomic annotation, and cases where the same species appeared under different names or taxonomic IDs. Our workflow integrates filtering and validation steps that enable mitochondrial genome recovery. By leveraging underused SRA sequencing data, this study demonstrates that public genomic repositories can serve as a powerful platform for data mining, expanding genomic resources while reducing the financial and environmental costs of generating new data. The resulting dataset provides a substantial resource for fungal phylogenetics and evolutionary studies and highlights the potential of large-scale public data reuse in genomic research.

## Genome characterization of rainbow trout-associated Midichloria-like organism reveals a novel, potentially pathogenic bacterial lineage in a vertebrate host

**Presenter:** Argelia Cuenca, National Institute of Aquatic Resources (DTUaqua).Technical University of Denmark

**Abstract:**

The family “Candidatus Midichloriaceae” remains the least characterised lineage within the Rickettsiales, and its diversity, biology, and host range are still poorly understood. Members of this intracellular group typically associate with protists and invertebrates, and species of Midichloria are especially notable for their ability to invade arthropod mitochondria. Because Rickettsiales lineages have been linked to the ancestral origin of mitochondria, Midichloriaceae provides an important model for investigating intracellular evolution and symbiotic interactions.<br />In 2008, a previously unknown Rickettsia-like bacterium was detected in skin lesions of rainbow trout with red mark syndrome (also called strawberry disease). This Midichloria-like organism (MLO), closely related to Midichloria mitochondrii, represents the first documented member of this bacterial family in a vertebrate host, suggesting a remarkable expansion of host range.<br />Here, we present the complete 1.08 Mb genome of the MLO, assembled as a single circular contig. Phylogenomic analyses reveal that MLO forms a deeply divergent sister lineage to Candidatus Midichloria, supporting its status as a novel genus within the family. The genome shows a limited metabolic repertoire, underscoring its obligate intracellular lifestyle. Furthermore, the presence of numerous unique genes absent from related taxa highlights its distinct evolutionary trajectory and provides new opportunities to investigate potential host shifts into vertebrates and mechanisms shaping host–mitochondrial interactions.<br />

## Cellular Innovation through De Novo Gene Birth Driven by Promiscuous Protein–Protein Interactions

**Presenter:** Yuxiang Huang, 西湖大学

**Abstract:**

The traditional view holds that cellular novelty arises mainly from modifications of existing genes. However, recent genomics suggests that functional proteins can also emerge from non-coding regions—a process called de novo gene birth. To explore the mechanisms and prevalence of this phenomenon, we tested a library of 10⁵ random protein sequences for their ability to rescue E. coli growth under heat stress. Strikingly, nearly 20% of these sequences significantly enhanced thermal tolerance. Further analyses showed that these peptides improve post-stress viability by modulating the expression of host proteins and/or by protecting heat-stress-related proteins through promiscuous protein–protein interactions. These findings suggest that de novo gene birth may be a more widespread and mechanistically plausible source of evolutionary innovation than previously thought, underscoring the inherent capacity of random polypeptides to functionally integrate with native cellular systems.
