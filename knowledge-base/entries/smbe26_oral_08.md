## Evolutionary dynamics of extinct and extant moose hybridization in North America

**Presenter:** Amanda V. Meuser, *Stockholm University*

**Abstract:** The ancestor of stag-moose (Cervalces scotti and C. latifrons) entered North America via the Bering Land Bridge around 800 thousand years ago (ka) and moved southwards during interglacial periods, eventually inhabiting all of boreal North America. Just before the submersion of the Bering Land Bridge (14-10.5 ka), the modern moose (Alces alces) also entered North America from Eurasia and spread southwards as the ice sheets retreated after the Last Glacial Maximum. Cervalces sp. became extinct in North America around 10 ka, likely due to the large-scale environmental change at the end of the Pleistocene (11.7 ka).  Hybridization commonly follows secondary contact of closely-related species after range expansions. Thus, it is possible that the two species hybridized upon contact. To test this, we have generated ancient genomic data from multiple Cervalces sp. samples from Yukon, Canada and have compared these to 40 modern A. alces genomes. The results provide novel insights into the movements and evolution of moose in North America in relation to past climatic changes.

## Paleolithic prevalence of human color vision diversity

**Presenter:** Yizhen Wang, *The University of Tokyo*

**Abstract:** Humans are unique in color vision among catarrhine primates (African/Asian monkeys, apes and humans) showing diversity with “deficiency” phenotypes. The phenotypic variation is mainly due to variation of the L and M (collectively called “L/M”) opsin genes by gene conversion among them, replacing a portion of one gene with that of another. Amino acid difference at the residue 180th encoded in exon 3 accounts for ~5 nm and those at 277th and 285th in exon 5 collectively for ~25 nm of the ~30 nm spectral difference between L and M opsin visual pigments. We quantified the nucleotide differences in each exon and intron among L/M opsin genes for each individual as “L/M diversity”. We analyzed genomic data of 2,533 individuals globally from the 1000 Genomes Project and our own targeted capture (TC) project. We also analyzed public data from 17 neolithic and paleolithic ancient humans and two archaic hominins (Neanderthal and Denisovan), as well as our TC data from non-human catarrhines (14 species). While non-human catarrhines and archaic hominins showed as high L/M diversity of exon 3 as of exon 5, current and ancient (both neolithic and paleolithic) humans showed lowered L/M diversity of exon 3. These results suggest that natural selection against gene conversion among L/M opsin genes has been relaxed in exon 3 with smaller spectral effects and thus human color vision diversity has already been prevalent since the paleolithic era. This challenges a notion of “deficiency” for this ancient common variation.

## Characterization of archaic ancestry in 63,000 Japanese individuals from the Tohoku Medical Megabank

**Presenter:** Mikel Lana Alberro, *Max Planck For Evolutionary Anthropology*

**Abstract:** Biobanks that provide genetic information and phenotypes for large cohorts have been used to explore the impact of introgressed Neandertal DNA on phenotypic diversity and disease susceptibility in present-day people. To date most studies have focused primarily on European populations who carry Neandertal introgressed DNA. We have characterised Neandertal and Denisovan introgression in 63,000 Japanese individuals from the Tohoku Medical Megabank Organization (ToMMo) biobank using a reference-free Hidden Markov Model (hmmix). We describe the set of Denisovan and Neandertal haplotypes in ToMMo and assess their relationship to the high-quality genomes of four Neandertals and two Denisovans. We confirm that Denisovan ancestry in Japan derives from two pulses of introgression from genetically distinct Denisovan populations, and find a negative correlation between Jomon and Denisovan ancestry using modern Japanese genomes. Using GWAS summary statistics, we investigate the impact of archaic introgression on SNP-heritability on traits from Biobank Japan. We also identify regions enriched for Neandertal and Denisovan DNA and describe high-frequency archaic haplotypes in the Japanese population that have significant replicable associations to human health outcomes in multiple East Asian biobanks.

## Functional characterization of archaic introgression reveals immune system adaptation in Near Oceania

**Presenter:** Steven Reilly, *Yale University*

**Abstract:** Understanding the evolutionary and phenotypic impacts of adaptive human variation has been a difficult to achieve goal. Near Oceanic populations—which harbor exceptional archaic introgression levels—remain especially underrepresented in genomic and functional studies. Here we comprehensively characterize archaic introgressed variants using massively parallel reporter assays (MPRAs) and deep learning based variant classification.<br /><br />Using 278 high-coverage Near Oceanic genomes produced by the Tucci lab, we analyzed 33,355 high-frequency archaic variants, identifying 167 missense, 10 loss of function, and 60 predicted splice-disrupting variants. The vast majority (97%) are non-coding, with significant enrichment in immune cis-regulatory elements (OR=1.30, p=6.5e-94), including T and B cells. 51 variants overlap fine-mapped associations in the UK Biobank and Biobank Japan, including for white blood cell count and height. <br /><br />We applied MPRA to assess the regulatory impacts of archaic variants in immune cell lines. We identified 1,551 significant expression modulating variants (emVars) linked to 1,422 candidate target genes. Pathway analysis identified putatively functional adaptive variants in multiple components (JAK1, OAS1, STAT2, MX2, TRIM10) of both the interferon gamma and alpha signaling pathway across multiple populations. We detect 4 functional emVars near the antiviral OAS1 gene on a Denisovan haplotype unique to Oceanic populations distinct from the COVID-19 protective Neanderthal version found in Europeans and East Asians. At T-cell differentiation TRPS1 locus, we identify expression increasing emVar predicted to increase STAT1::STAT2 affinity that resides in an active hematopoietic enhancer. Together, our functional analysis suggests parallel immune system adaptation from archaic sources.<br />

## Pleistocene wolves genome revealed new insight on migration and hybridization in North American canids 

**Presenter:** Alberto Carmagnini, *LMU*

**Abstract:** Species boundaries are extremely porous in the genus Canis and interspecific gene flow appears to be ubiquitous across its phylogeny. This is particularly evident in North America where the morphological and ecological variation of wolf-like canids is represented by a group of distinct lineages which are known to hybridize extensively. Although the exact origins of these lineages and the timeframes of these admixture events is yet unclear.<br /><br />Migration and population replacement driven by the climatic fluctuations in the Late Pleistocene further complicate the picture while habitat depletion and persecution by humans in the last few hundred years have dramatically altered their present range and census, making it impossible to reconstruct the evolutionary history of these lineages from modern genomes alone.<br /><br />In this study, we sequenced the genomes of pre-LGM canid specimens (23,000 to 43,000 cal yrs BP) from Natural Trap Cave in Wyoming, USA, at the southern end of the Pleistocene ice-free corridor. We analysed these sequences along with publicly available modern, historical, and ancient genomes. <br /><br />These data confirm that hybridization had taken place between canid taxa in the contiguous USA prior to the Last Glacial Maximum. Our results expand our understanding of the known diversity and distribution of the group of Late Pleistocene American Canis providing new insights into the complex history of migration and gene flow that shaped the ancestry of modern North American canid populations.<br />

## Ancient DNA from birch tar artefacts: insights into population structure and gendered practices in Neolithic Alpine Europe

**Presenter:** Anna White, *Globe Institute, University of Copenhagen*

**Abstract:** Birch tar is emerging as an alternative source of ancient DNA, particularly in archaeological contexts where human remains are scarce or absent. As part of the AlpGen project, we have screened over 100 birch tar artefacts from Neolithic Alpine lake settlements to assess their potential for biomolecular analysis. In a region where skeletal material is largely lacking, birch tar provides a unique archive through which ancestry, population structure, and genetic relationships between communities can be explored. Many artefacts contain enough ancient human DNA for genetic sex determination, enabling new approaches to studying gendered practices in the production and use of birch tar. The recovered DNA also allows us to investigate ancestry and population structure among lake-dwelling groups, shedding light on the genetic makeup of Neolithic Alpine communities. The presence of oral microbial DNA helps identify chewed tar and supports the reconstruction of oral microbiome composition and past oral health, offering a potential alternative to dental calculus. In this paper, we present initial findings from AlpGen, showing that birch tar can preserve ancient biomolecules at a scale suitable for population-level research. These results underscore its value as a biomolecular archive for understanding the genetic and cultural landscapes of prehistoric Europe.

## The Green Sahara genomic landscape: tracing ancestries across North Africa and Sahel through joint analysis of modern and ancient genomes

**Presenter:** Flavia Risi, *Sapienza University Of Rome*

**Abstract:** Africa, the continent with the greatest genetic diversity, has long been underrepresented in large-scale genomic research, limiting our understanding of its demographic and migratory history. Recent advances in sequencing, bioinformatics, and interdisciplinary integration have enabled a more detailed reconstruction of African population dynamics. A key episode was the Last Green Sahara (12,000–5,000 years ago), when humid conditions transformed the desert into a fertile region that fostered mobility and interaction among distant groups. The subsequent aridification reshaped these connections, concentrating exchanges along different geographic axes, particularly the Nile Valley and the Sahelian belt.<br />In this study, 118 new high-coverage whole-genome sequences from Sahelian and North African populations were analysed alongside modern and ancient comparative data. Results show marked genetic continuity along the Nile Valley, confirming its role as a stable demographic corridor. At the same time ancestral components display a clear north–south gradient along the axis, reflecting a stratification shaped by both ancient and later historical events. The central Sahel displays strong genetic cohesion despite cultural and linguistic diversity, indicating long-term interaction and gene flow. An eastern ancestral component, introduced in the region at the end of the Last Green Sahara (around 4,000 years ago), is best represented today by the Nuba people of Sudan and serves as a genetic link between the Sahel and Nile systems. Finally, isolated populations such as the Copts of Sudan and the Berbers of the Siwa Oasis (Egypt) display strong local genetic identities, suggesting evolutionary trajectories divergent from the patterns observed at the regional scale.

## Gene regulatory signatures of archaic introgression across human tissues and cell types

**Presenter:** Kitty Murphy, *Globe Institute, University Of Copenhagen*

**Abstract:** Archaic humans, including Neanderthals and Denisovans, contributed to modern human genomes multiple times through a process known as archaic introgression. These introduced archaic DNA fragments have shaped human evolution and genetic diversity, and with the increasing availability of omics datasets, there is now an opportunity to infer the regulatory impacts of these fragments across many tissues and cell types. The existence of introgression deserts, regions strongly depleted of archaic DNA, suggests that some archaic genetic material is incompatible with modern human biology and potentially contributes to human uniqueness. Furthermore, as ancient DNA is preserved mainly from remains in colder climates, we may never be able to study the molecular profiles of archaic populations from warmer regions like South Asia. Here, we leveraged the large-scale sequencing efforts of GTEx (n=800) and UK Biobank (n=500,000) to generate maps of archaic introgression and introgression deserts. Using a reference-free method for detecting archaic introgression, we identify all archaic groups that contributed DNA to modern genomes. Using gene expression data for >50 tissues, scRNA-seq, methylation patterns, and H3K27ac data, we explored whether introgressed DNA and introgression deserts are associated with gene regulatory changes across specific tissues, including skin, testes, and brain regions, and cell types, such as T cells of the immune system and melanocytes of the skin.

## Archaic Introgression in ancient Eurasians

**Presenter:** Marco Rosario Capodiferro, *Trinity College Dublin*

**Abstract:** Archaic introgression, the DNA inherited from archaic humans, has been an important source of variation for the adaptation of modern humans outside sub-Saharan Africa. <br />Despite its significance, most of our knowledge comes from analyses of genomes belonging to present-day individuals, while only limited insights have been obtained from the large datasets of ancient genomes available so far. <br />When, and through how many events, modern humans encountered and admixed with Neanderthals and Denisovans remains an open chapter in our evolutionary history. Equally unresolved is how archaic introgression influenced past human adaptation and how these inherited regions evolved within modern genomes.<br />Imputation using a panel of present-day individuals is known to be a valuable way to improve ancient DNA quality. It has recently been shown to be highly effective at recovering genotypes within archaic introgression regions, thereby improving our ability to identify introgressed segments in the genomes of ancient individuals. We applied this approach to investigate a dataset of nearly two thousand ancient individuals from Eurasia. Our preliminary analyses reveal spatial and temporal patterns of archaic introgression across the continents and highlight genomic regions of archaic origin that appear to be over-represented in specific ancient populations.

## Multiple Denisovan lineages shaped the genomic diversity of Asian populations

**Presenter:** Charlotte Antoine-Derouet, *UMR7206 - Éco-Anthropologie, Muséum National d'Histoire Naturelle*

**Abstract:** Recent fossils discoveries in Asia, accompanied by the sequencing of Neanderthal and Denisovan genomes, highlight the complex hominin diversity and peopling history of this continent. However, investigations of Neanderthal and Denisovan in modern human ancestry have largely focused on European, East Asian and Oceanian populations, leaving Central and Southeast Asia comparatively understudied. <br /><br />In this study, we investigated Neanderthal and Denisovan ancestry in Central and Southeast Asian populations. We estimated that Neanderthal ancestry proportions range from 2.5% to 2.8% across these populations. Using the f4-ratio statistic, Denisovan ancestry was undetectable in some Central Asian populations, while in other Asian populations it was in low amount (<1%), comparable to those observed in East Asian populations. <br /><br />Using Sprime to further characterize Denisovan introgressed genomic fragments, we revealed that Denisovan ancestry in Asian populations clusters into distinct groups. Notably, Southeast Asian populations exhibit three distinct clusters of Denisovan introgressed segments, suggesting admixture with at least three genetically distinct Denisovan populations. In contrast, Denisovan ancestry in other Asian populations appeared to derive from only two Denisovan sources. The Sprime results were confirmed using an independent analysis using a CRF approach to identify introgressed segments. <br /><br />These results shed a new light on the complex admixture history between early Homo sapiens and archaic hominins during the peopling of Asia and highlight the extensive diversity in these extinct populations.

## The Evolution of HLA Diversity in a Native American Population from Ancient to Modern Times

**Presenter:** Diyar Hamidi, *University Of Hamburg*

**Abstract:** The interaction between host and pathogens is a dynamic evolutionary process that has led to extensive genetic variation in immune-related genes, particularly within the human Major Histocompatibility Complex (HLA). While pathogen-mediated balancing selection is thought to be a major driver of this diversity, the relative contributions of other evolutionary forces—such as admixture and genetic drift—remain unclear. Moreover, the specific mechanisms of balancing selection and the pathogen-specific versus broad protective effects of HLA alleles are still under debate. To address these questions, we investigate HLA Class I and II diversity in pre- and post-European contact Native American populations from Xaltocan, Mexico—providing a temporal perspective on the impact of pathogens during European invasion of the Americas. Using ancient DNA-specific HLA genotyping, we characterized allele frequency changes over time. We observed that seven HLA groups showed significant frequency changes between ancient and modern populations. To evaluate whether these changes could be explained by genetic drift alone, we will use drift-only simulations, followed by tests for signatures of selection. This study provides critical insights into how pathogen exposure and evolutionary forces have shaped HLA diversity in Native American populations. More broadly, our temporal approach offers a valuable model for disentangling the complex drivers of immune gene evolution in human history.

## An archaeogenomic study of Black Death victims from Southern Europe

**Presenter:** Oguzhan Parasayan, *Institut Pasteur*

**Abstract:** Infectious diseases have been a major driver of human mortality throughout history, imposing strong selective pressures on human populations. The Black Death, caused by Yersinia pestis, was among the most catastrophic pandemics in European history, claiming an estimated 30–60% of the population between 1347 and 1352. Although ancient DNA studies have identified candidate genetic variants potentially associated with resistance during the second plague pandemic, these findings remain unreplicated, largely due to limited genome coverage and small sample sizes.<br />Here, we present 130 newly generated ancient genomes (mean coverage 3×) from individuals excavated from a Black Death–associated mass burial (1348–1350) and a post–Black Death church cemetery (1353–1545) in Toulouse, southwestern France. These imputed genomes were analyzed together with a comparative dataset comprising 730 ancient genomes (>0.5×; 3rd–18th centuries) and 4,790 modern genomes from France and the broader western Eurasian region. Presumed plague victims and descendants of survivors showed no differences in age or sex distributions but exhibited marked differences in genetic relatedness, consistent with distinct burial practices. We find no evidence for genetic discontinuity in southwestern France since the Middle Ages, enabling direct temporal comparisons across the Black Death.<br />Interestingly, allele frequency differences between presumed plague victims and survivors were further amplified when comparing medieval and contemporary populations, consistent with directional selection of plague resistance. While preliminary, these results suggest a long-term genetic legacy of historical epidemics, with potential implications for understanding human evolutionary responses to infectious disease.

## Signals of hybridization between commercial and wild bumblebees revealed by temporal genomics

**Presenter:** Michael Mitschke, *Stockholm University*

**Abstract:** Hybridization and the transfer of genetic material between domesticated and wild species are of major conservation concern. The buff-tailed bumblebee (Bombus terrestris) has been bred for crop pollination since 1987, and commercial colonies are traded globally. Hybridization between commercial and wild lineages was found in Spain and Poland, but not in Great Britain or Sweden. However, despite the high dispersal capacity of bumblebees, these studies assigned unadmixed individuals by using limited spatial distances.<br /><br />To further investigate hybridization between commercial and wild bumblebees in Great Britain and Sweden, we used historical DNA to generate genomes for dry-pinned museum specimens (~50) collected before the first distribution of commercial hives. The historical samples serve as a baseline to detect signals of hybridization across modern samples (~170).<br /><br />Contrary to previous results limited to modern data, we find signals of hybridization between commercial and wild bumblebees in both Sweden and Great Britain in population structure and D-statistics. We find evidence for a major inversion on chromosome 10 present in commercial and some modern wild bees, but not in historical samples. This could indicate the transfer of potentially adaptive genes. Our findings highlight the importance of museum genomics to complement modern genomic data with a temporal perspective.<br />

## Ancient DNA and functional genomics retrace the path to modern chicken productivity

**Presenter:** Yuemin Li

**Abstract:** Chicken is the world’s most consumed source of animal protein, yet intensive production emerged only within the last ~100 years, accelerating after World War II as specialised broiler and layer lines were established. Modern commercial lines were largely developed in the 1960s by breeders in North America and Europe using genetic material from European flocks effectively transforming them into modern commercial stock. Chickens, however, were domesticated in tropical Southeast Asia over 3,500 years ago and spread to Europe by ~2500 years ago. Their expansion into temperate Europe likely required substantial phenotypic change, the genetic basis of which remains poorly understood. In fact, despite major advances in breeding over the last century, the extent to which the distinctive genetic makeup of European chicken populations, shaped by over 2,000 years of selection, outside of their ancestral range, and 19th-century admixture with East Asian populations, provided the standing variation required to establish modern high-productivity lines remains unclear. To quantify the impact of ancient and recent selection on the genomic and phenotypic evolution of European chickens, our consortium assembled a dataset comprising >5,000 genomes, including 134 ancient nuclear genomes spanning over 2,000 years, as well as >7,000 bulk, and 127,000 single-cell RNA-seq profiles. These data allow us to resolve the timing and number of introductions into Europe, identify genetic bases of adaptation to temperate environments, and quantify shifts in selection intensity leading to modern high-productivity lines.

## ratePlacer: A Phylogenetic Framework for Molecular Dating of Ancient Environmental DNA

**Presenter:** Maya Lemmon-Kishi, *University Of California Berkeley*

**Abstract:** Ancient environmental DNA (aeDNA) from permafrost, lake and marine sediments provides a rich source of genetic data that captures broad perspectives of past biodiversity. Accurate dating is crucial for discovering ecologically relevant patterns from aeDNA, and increasingly samples are too old for C-14 dating. While molecular dating allows for sample ages to be estimated from the recovered genetic material itself, the fragmented and damaged nature of short-read ancient DNA from multiple taxonomic sources poses significant challenges. We have developed ratePlacer, a phylogeny-based method for analyzing aeDNA that can combine information from many short reads in a sample while accounting for DNA damage to provide maximum likelihood estimates of sample ages. Simulations demonstrate that ratePlacer accurately dates samples even under the fragmented, damaged conditions characteristic of aeDNA and outperforms tip dating methods like BEAST for taxonomically mixed samples commonly found in aeDNA. However, applying ratePlacer to exceptionally old sediment samples revealed previously uncharacterized substitution patterns that substantially complicate molecular dating. These patterns deviate from typical ancient deamination damage, suggesting that aeDNA should be carefully evaluated in future genomic and evolutionary analyses.

## Paleogenomics Reveals Arctic Range and Aquatic Specialization in the American "cheetah", Miracinonyx trumani

**Presenter:** Molly Cassatt-Johnstone, *University Of California, Santa Cruz*

**Abstract:** The extinct “American cheetah” (Miracinonyx trumani) has long been considered a North American ecological analog of the African cheetah based on convergent cursorial morphology, yet this paradigm rests on limited data and untested assumptions about its evolutionary relationships and ecology. Here, we generate nuclear paleogenomes (22- and 38-fold coverage) from M. trumani specimens from Wyoming (ca. 23 kya) and Yukon (ca. 30 kya), plus two additional mitogenomes from Yukon, definitively placing this enigmatic felid within the evolutionary tree of cats. Phylogenomic analyses confirm M. trumani as sister to pumas (Puma concolor), not cheetahs, with divergence ~2.6 million years ago during rapid Plio-Pleistocene climate oscillations. The Yukon specimens extend the species' known range by over 20˚ latitude into Arctic environments, revealing populations that diverged 43,000 years ago yet maintained similarly low effective population sizes despite occupying vastly different habitats. Stable isotope analyses expose striking ecological differentiation, showing that the Arctic population occupied an elevated trophic position consistent with exploitation of anadromous fish, while the Wyoming population functioned as a terrestrial generalist predator. Genomic scans identified positive selection in sensory perception genes  associated with auditory and visual processing and loss-of-function mutations in circadian regulators, providing mechanistic insights into behavioral flexibility. Our results suggest that, rather than specialized pursuit predators, M. trumani exhibited remarkable ecological versatility across their range, and underscore how morphological convergence can obscure the true diversity and complexity of extinct megafaunal ecosystems.
