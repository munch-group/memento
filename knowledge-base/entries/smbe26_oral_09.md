## Parallel Molecular Evolution Across Replicated Anolis Lizard Adaptive Radiations

**Presenter:** Aryeh Miller, *Washington University in St. Louis*

**Abstract:** Whether convergent phenotypes arise from similar or distinct genetic underpinnings is a central question in evolutionary biology. In this study, we sequenced the genomes of all 435 extant anole lizard (Anolis) species to dissect the molecular basis of their convergent adaptation and parallel adaptive radiations in the Greater Antilles. We find that anoles exhibit widespread positive selection on loci which regulate developmental processes. Using branch-level selection screens, we find these lizards exhibit a genome-wide excess of parallel positive selection, indicating that repeated co-option of loci has shaped these adaptive radiations. In addition, we find that selective regimes at genes underlying limb development—a hallmark of convergent evolution in anoles—co-vary with phenotypic state, with each ecomorph experiencing distinct selection pressures. Lastly, we show that a transcription factor involved in limb development has undergone remarkable parallel evolution in minute and slender twig-specialist anoles, discovering a single missense mutation at a highly conserved position arising independently in three subgenera over approximately 40 million years, mirroring the parallel evolution of twig anole craniofacial and limb morphologies. Together, these findings provide a unified view that natural selection can predictably yield convergence which scales multiple biological hierarchies, from the assembly of ecological communities, to the phenotypes of individual species, to the specific allelic variants potentially underlying such phenotypes.

## The genomic underpinning of variation in vision across a global sample of ant workers

**Presenter:** Joel Vizueta, *University of Copenhagen*

**Abstract:** The ants have evolved a stunning global diversity with more than 15,000 extant species belonging to over 330 genera. Their foraging strategies are highly diverse with some species relying entirely on chemical cues while others depend primarily on visual perception. Some species can discriminate between colors and exhibit remarkable orientation skills and visual acuity while other ants are blind. This functional diversity is correlated with substantial variation in eye morphology across the worker castes, ranging from prominent compound eyes to eyes having been reduced or entirely lost. Here, we provide new insights in the evolution of these visual systems using a comparative genomic dataset generated by the Global Ant Genomics Alliance (GAGA). Analysis of 163 ant genomes shows that the five genes encoding opsins – G-protein-coupled receptors in photoreceptor cells – are conserved across the ants, with episodic gene losses in some species potentially affecting the ability for color vision. The opsin genes also show different selection constraints and decreased expression in species with reduced eyes. We further show that reductions in eye size are related to foraging habits and diet. Finally, we identify signatures of selection on genes and pathways involved in Drosophila eye development, providing insights into the molecular mechanisms associated with the ants visual systems.

## The day after the drug: Investigating drug-induced cellular memory

**Presenter:** Marina Khachaturyan, *Tel Aviv University*

**Abstract:** Many cancer patients who initially respond to first-line therapy show diminished response to subsequent cycles of treatment. This reduced therapeutic response often precedes acquisition of any genetic alterations, suggesting that drug-exposed cells are epigenetically distinct from drug-naïve cells. It has been established in other cellular contexts that epigenetic reprogramming creates sustained memory of prior stimuli across generations of cells. Nevertheless, it is currently unknow whether cancer cells have capacity to build memory to previous drug insults. Here, we combine lineage-tracing with longitudinal single-cell multiome sequencing and patient data to delineate the mechanisms underlying cancer cell drug-induced cellular memory. Using a model of non-genetic drug resistance, we show that despite resuming normal proliferation, a subset of cells retains features inherent to the on-drug state even following a prolonged drug-holiday period indicating memory properties. This cellular memory is primarily encoded by changes in the chromatin landscape. We further show that the capacity of cells to form the memory is lineage-specific and that while some lineages have high memory capacity others readily revert to naïve state once the drug is removed. Differential memory capacity is mediated by the transcription factors C/EBPα and C/EBPβ, known regulators of memory in innate immune cells (i.e. trained immunity). Finally, we show that C/EBPα is transiently expressed in cancer cells of patients under treatment. Taken together, our study reveals how treatment is selectively inscribed in the epigenetic landscape of cancer cells. These insights may guide novel strategies to sustain treatment efficacy by targeting cancer cell memory.

## A fast-evolving C-terminus underlies species-specific function of an orphan gene required for sperm nuclear shaping

**Presenter:** Geoff Findlay, *College Of The Holy Cross*

**Abstract:** Orphan genes are lineage-restricted and encode proteins without detectable homology.  In Drosophila melanogaster, the orphan gene saturn is required for male fertility and encodes an adaptively evolving protein, but its cellular function and the consequences of its rapid evolution have remained mysterious.  We used a CRISPR-generated null allele, tagged rescue constructs and immunofluorescence to demonstrate that saturn is required for spermatid nuclear shape changes.  Null males are near-sterile, show defective spermatid nuclear shape, and fail to complete sperm individualization, while immunofluorescence in wild-type males shows that Saturn protein localizes to the condensing nuclear membranes.  To understand the functional consequences of Saturn’s adaptive evolution, we performed gene swap experiments in which we assayed the ability of saturn orthologs from other Drosophila species to restore fertility in D. melanogaster males lacking endogenous saturn.  The degree of fertility rescue declined with evolutionary distance, culminating in the complete inability of the D. ananassae ortholog to restore fertility, despite its robust expression and proper localization to spermatid nuclei.  By constructing chimeric proteins containing portions of the melanogaster and ananassae orthologs, we mapped this cross-species incompatibility to Saturn’s rapidly evolving C terminus, suggesting that this region may coevolve with other spermatogenesis components.  Finally, using an antibody raised to the ananassae Saturn ortholog, we found that this protein’s subcellular localization is conserved in its native species, suggesting that Saturn evolved this localization pattern ancestrally.  These results reveal an essential role for an orphan gene in spermiogenesis and demonstrate concrete functional consequences to its rapid, adaptive evolution.

## Functional characterization of the male-specific mitochondrial ORFan protein in DUI bivalves reveals a role in spermatogenesis

**Presenter:** Julie Brémaud, *Université de Montréal*

**Abstract:** Bivalves represent a rare and intriguing exception in the animal kingdom, in which atypical mitochondrial genes may influence sex determination or sex differentiation. This possibility is linked to Doubly Uniparental Inheritance (DUI), a unique system of mitochondrial transmission found in over 100 bivalve species, whereby males inherit mitochondria from both parents while females inherit exclusively from the mother, like all other animal species. In DUI species, “male mitogenomes” carry morf, a taxonomically restricted ORFan gene with no homologs outside DUI lineages. Although the function of morf remains enigmatic, it has been hypothesized to participate in male-specific sex determination or sex differentiation, or alternatively in the molecular mechanisms underlying DUI itself. Understanding how taxonomically sex-restricted genes can acquire essential functions is fundamental in evolutionary biology, yet the molecular networks through which ORFan proteins like MORF operate remain largely unexplored. Western blot analyses revealed that MORF is exclusively expressed in male gonads of two distantly related DUI bivalves, Mytilus edulis and Venustaconcha ellipsiformis, during the reproductive season, suggesting a key role in spermatogenesis. Immunocytochemistry localized MORF in mitochondria and acrosomes of late spermatids and mature sperm, positioning it at critical sites for gamete maturation and fertilization. Co-immunoprecipitation and GST pull-down coupled with mass spectrometry identified MORF’s partners. Gene Ontology analyses revealed a predominance of extracellular matrix proteins, particularly laminin and collagen, alongside ribosomal and cytoskeletal proteins. These results illustrate a distinctive, conserved and sex-specific role for MORF in spermatogenesis and mitochondrial dynamics providing insights into molecular innovations underlying alternative mitochondrial inheritance systems.

## Single-cell phylodynamics reveal rapid late-stage colorectal cancer expansions

**Presenter:** David Posada, *University of Vigo*

**Abstract:** Colorectal cancer (CRC) arises through successive genetic mutations and clonal expansions, yet the temporal and spatial dynamics of these processes remain incompletely resolved. We conducted whole-genome sequencing of 350 single cells from multiregional tumor samples in seven CRC patients and applied Bayesian phylodynamic models that explicitly account for technical noise and phylogenetic uncertainty. Our analyses indicate that most malignant lineages originate decades before clinical diagnosis, remain in prolonged indolent phases, and undergo rapid expansion within approximately two years of detection. Tumors exhibited pronounced spatial structuring with limited interregional mixing, whereas substitution rates were consistently elevated in malignant cells, varied across patients, and were uncoupled from growth tempo. These results provide one of the most detailed reconstructions of CRC evolutionary history to date, revealing extended periods of stasis followed by sudden growth, with important implications for early detection, intervention, and spatially informed sampling strategies.

## Crossover and consequences: are individual recombination rates under selection?

**Presenter:** Susan Johnston, *University of Edinburgh*

**Abstract:** Meiotic crossovers are a fundamental feature of sexual reproduction. They ensure proper chromosome segregation and shuffle linked alleles into novel haplotypes, facilitating adaptive evolution. However, they can also introduce novel mutations and break apart favourable haplotypes. These trade-offs suggest potential impacts on individual reproductive success and offspring survival, yet empirical studies are rare. A major hurdle is that direct estimates of crossover positions in females are highly invasive, and genomic estimates in both sexes can only be obtained for individuals with genotyped offspring. These introduce sampling biases, as fitness analyses cannot include individuals with no offspring. Genomic prediction (GP) uses genomic data to estimate genetic and phenotypic values in unmeasured individuals, and is a transformative tool in predicting human disease and breeding outcomes in crops and livestock. Here, we use a GP approach in a long-term study of house sparrows (Passer domesticus) to determine sex-specific genomic estimated breeding values (GEBVs) for individual crossover rates in >2,600 phenotyped and >2,900 unphenotyped individuals. We then relate GEBVs to sex-specific reproductive success and survival measures. Our findings suggest potential stabilising selection against extreme values of ACC, especially in male sparrows. By inferring crossover rates in individuals without offspring, our approach overcomes the “missing fraction” problem and provides rare evidence of selection on recombination in a wild population.

## Disentangling patterns of variation in the human genome: the roles
of mutation, recombination and selection revisited

**Presenter:** Gustavo Valadares Barroso, *University Of Wisconsin-Madison*

**Abstract:** Decades of theoretical and empirical work have struggled to reconcile competing views on how evolution<br />shapes patterns of genetic variation. We now understand the genome as a mosaic molded by both neutral<br />and selective forces, but quantifying their relative contributions remains an open challenge. A major<br />obstacle has been the tendency to analyze each evolutionary process in isolation, which is problematic<br />because they leave similar signatures on sequence data. Consequently, correlation-based analyses have<br />talked past each other. To address this gap, we develop structural equation models describing how mutation, recombination and selection jointly shape the genomic landscape of diversity. This approach offers a more realistic representation of biological interactions and enables rigorous evaluation of hypothesized causal structures, marking both conceptual and practical improvements over previous studies. Analyses of human data reveal large variation in the explanatory power of candidate models across chromosomes. We find that previous studies likely overestimated the predictive accuracy of background selection; although it emerges as the overall main driver of diversity, mutation rate variation has a comparable impact. We also show that recombination increases diversity more strongly through its impact on background selection than through its mutagenic effect, resolving a longstanding debate. We conclude that modeling molecular processes substantially improves our ability to explain the genomic landscape of diversity. At the same time, evidence for unmodeled covariance between mutation rates and density of constrained sites revives an ongoing discussion, although further studies are needed to determine its origins. <br />

## What sets the upper bound on animal cell size?

**Presenter:** Rachel Mueller, *Colorado State University*

**Abstract:** Molecular interactions play out within the physical arena of the cell. Animals with extremely large cells are rare – why? What constrains cell size? I address this question using salamanders; they have evolved cells <50x larger than other vertebrates, alongside gigantic genomes and nuclei. When cells and nuclei are large, intracellular distances are long. We modelled RNA diffusion out of the salamander nucleus to quantify delays in gene expression. We show that the extra time it takes RNA to diffuse to the nuclear envelope introduces delays that slow oscillatory gene expression kinetics, providing an explanation for the slow rate of salamander embryogenesis. Using phylogenetic comparative methods, we show that cells are constrained to smaller sizes when life history includes the time-sensitive, vulnerable developmental transformations of metamorphosis. Using electron microscopy, we show that salamander organelles scale proportionately with cell size, with no substantial changes in shape or network structure required to maintain functionality. This proportional scaling is consistent with organellar building blocks being incorporated from a constant cytoplasmic pool (limited precursor model). Although simple, this model requires protein concentration homeostasis in salamanders’ large cytoplasmic volumes. Notably, experimentally induced large cells show proteome dilution from insufficient biosynthesis. We show that half-lives of RNAs and proteins are longer in large cells to accommodate longer transit times; we posit that this slow turnover also 1) maintains protein concentration homeostasis, and 2) lowers metabolic cost. Thus, slow molecular turnover overcomes some challenges, but slow gene expression kinetics and effects on development nevertheless constrain cell expansion.

## Frequency-dependent immune selection in tumors inferred from neoantigen depletion patterns

**Presenter:** Ryan Gutenkunst, *University Of Arizona*

**Abstract:** Despite abundant evidence that immune surveillance shapes cancer development and growth, the timing and triggers of immune recognition during tumor evolution remain largely unknown. Mutations in cancer cells can generate novel peptides (neoantigens) that the immune system recognizes as foreign, leading to killing of cells expressing recognized neoantigens. To test potential recognition mechanisms, we compare forward simulations of tumor growth with tumor sequencing data from immunosuppressed and immunocompetent patients. In our simulations, a fraction of mutations create neoantigens, the immune system stochastically recognizes individual neoantigens, and cells carrying recognized neoantigens experience immune killing. We simulate three recognition scenarios: (1) no immune recognition (neutral baseline); (2) immediate recognition upon neoantigen appearance (constant selection); and (3) recognition triggered once a neoantigen-bearing lineage exceeds an abundance threshold (frequency-dependent selection). To compare simulations with data, we compute the ratio of neoantigenic to neutral passenger mutations across allele frequency bins within tumors. The neutral and constant-selection models predict approximately flat ratios across frequencies, whereas the threshold model generates relative depletion of neoantigens at high frequencies. This predicted depletion pattern is qualitatively consistent with observed data, while the alternative models are not. We are now quantitatively fitting the threshold model to estimate how recognition thresholds and killing strength vary with neoantigen quality and patient immune status. This work demonstrates that evolutionary modeling can distinguish among competing hypotheses for immune recognition dynamics using standard single-time-point tumor sequencing data.

## Haplotype specific structural variation within a single human genome

**Presenter:** Rebekah Rogers, *UNC Charlotte*

**Abstract:** T2T complete chromosomal assemblies offer new avenues to study genome rearrangement and copy number variation in human populations.  With new highly contiguous haplotype-resolved genomes, we can now identify variation in gene content and gene organization within a single individual across maternal and paternal haplotypes.  Using genes as anchors to identify common genomic coordinates, we identify breaks in synteny that correspond to inversions, deletions, and gene family expansion.  Our new method, STUNG, is available as a user friendly software with interactive GUI.  This computational approach serves as a useful template for analysis of emerging T2T haplotype resolved genomes in the future. We identify inversions, deletions, duplications that drive differences in gene content even across the two haplotypes for a single individual. Among these include very large gene families (with over 50 gene members) previously intractable for copy number analysis with precision.  We are able to fully resolve copy number for these large gene families across each haplotype, revealing variation that otherwise would be impossible to nail down.  We further identify  rapidly evolving gene families known to be unique primates with known copy number variation in humans now even with variation within a single individual.  These results point to very rapid changes in duplicate gene content within human populations including genes with known clinical relevance.  With this increased resolution and haplotype precision  we can gain greater understanding of how copy number changes and rearrangements evolve within populations and how they contribute to genotype diversity for individuals.

## The origin and evolution of alkaloid sequestration in poison frogs

**Presenter:** Julia Albuquerque de Pinna, *Vrije Universiteit Brussel*

**Abstract:** The majority of frogs use a proteinaceous skin secretion to fend off predators. However, in Dendrobatidae and several other frog families, these gene-encoded toxins have been evolutionarily replaced by lipophilic alkaloids sequestered from a specialized diet. As these replacements imply the evolution of mechanisms that protect the frogs from the alkaloids’ neurotoxic effects and promote the accumulation of the alkaloids in the frogs’ skin glands, they represent a remarkable case of evolutionary innovation. To elucidate the origin of toxin sequestration, we investigated patterns of adaptative evolution in (1) Saxiphilin and Alkaloid-Binding Globulin (ABG), two secretory proteins previously proposed to be involved in detoxication, transport and storage of alkaloids and (2) voltage-gated sodium channels (Nav) known as the most common target of neurotoxic alkaloids. Genomic screening and phylogenetic analyses show that the Saxiphilin and ABG genes of alkaloid-sequestering frogs have orthologues in a wide range of non-sequestering frogs, suggesting repurposing of preexisting genes at the origin of toxin sequestration. Gene duplication events and signatures of positive selection within Dendrobatidae indicate that protein diversification and adaptive evolution may have further optimized alkaloid sequestration in this clade. Predicted structures for the secretory proteins and sodium channels demonstrate aminoacid substitutions in positions that confirm strong selective pressure for toxin processing and resistance. This is the first comprehensive phylogenetic study that targets evolutionary trends in alkaloid sequestration and brings new insights on how this special mechanism evolved in poison frogs.

## Genomic Plasticity and Evolutionary Innovation in Avian Metabolic Adaptation

**Presenter:** Xinyu Zhang, *Zhejiang University*

**Abstract:** Genomic plasticity—the capacity of genomes to accommodate functional change under shifting physiological demands—provides a fundamental substrate for evolutionary innovation. Birds exhibit exceptionally high metabolic rates among vertebrates, yet substantial variation in metabolic capacity persists across avian lineages. How genomic plasticity contributes to this metabolic diversity remains poorly understood. Here, through an integrated comparative analysis of 363 bird species, we show that avian metabolic diversification is underpinned by distinct genomic innovations operating at both coding and regulatory levels. In Passeriformes, we identify a passerine-specific amino acid substitution in ABCC8, which encodes the SUR1 regulatory subunit of the ATP-sensitive potassium channel. Functional assays demonstrate that this substitution reduces channel sensitivity to ATP, effectively shifting the energetic threshold for channel inhibition and providing a mechanistic basis for adaptive modulation of glucose regulation. Beyond protein-coding evolution, we uncover accelerated evolution within a highly conserved non-coding regulatory element that is specific to high-metabolic-rate lineages. This regulatory innovation enhances expression of FUBP1, a key regulator of antioxidant pathways, thereby strengthening resistance to oxidative stress associated with elevated metabolic demand. Together, these findings illustrate how genomic plasticity enables evolutionary innovation through complementary molecular mechanisms, integrating changes in energy sensing and oxidative buffering. Our results highlight the joint contributions of coding and regulatory evolution to physiological diversification and provide new insights into the genomic basis of metabolic adaptation in birds.

## A Blast from the Past: ‘Living Fossil’ Fish Genomes Bridge Gene Regulation Across Long Evolutionary Distances

**Presenter:**  

**Abstract:** Identifying orthologous cis-regulatory elements (CREs) across distant vertebrate lineages remains challenging. Many CREs are undetectable across taxa due to sequence divergence beyond recognition, CRE turnover, and the emergence of lineage-specific regulatory elements. This disconnect is especially pronounced in widely used model systems like rodents and teleost fishes, which exhibit rapid molecular sequence evolution.<br /><br />Teleosts, including key biomedical models like zebrafish, are often used to study vertebrate genome function and regulation. However, a lineage-specific teleost genome duplication (TGD) drastically reshaped their genome structure and regulatory networks. Their accelerated sequence evolution, likely driven by the TGD, has obscured many ancestral CREs, complicating efforts to trace the evolution of gene regulation across vertebrates.<br /><br />In contrast, holostean fishes – gars and bowfins – retain some of the slowest-evolving vertebrate genomes. These “living fossils” provide a unique window into early vertebrate evolution. Their unduplicated genomes, conserved development, and archaic morphologies offer critical insights into deep homologies that have been masked by rapid evolution in derived teleost and tetrapod lineages.<br /><br />Leveraging haplotype-resolved genome assemblies from multiple holostean species, we use comparative genomics, single-cell transcriptomics, and epigenomic profiling in holosteans to reveal ancestral CREs and recover hidden gene regulatory landscapes across bony vertebrates. The evolutionary stability of holostean genomes enables the identification of conserved vertebrate CREs that would otherwise remain hidden for fast-evolving taxa.<br /><br />Our work underscores the immense value of including slow-evolving lineages across the Tree of Life to reconstruct the deep history of gene regulation and uncover the genome regulatory logic that shapes vertebrate biodiversity.<br />

## Tandem repeat variation is functional with signatures for directional and balancing selection in humans and chimpanzees

**Presenter:** Rori Rohlfs, *University Of Oregon*

**Abstract:** Tandem repeats (TRs) are highly mutable DNA elements that comprise nearly 8% of the human genome and influence gene regulation, protein coding, and disease. Despite their functional importance, our understanding of TR evolution has remained limited, as their repetitive nature hindered accurate sequencing with short-read technology. Here, using telomere-to-telomere (T2T) reference genomes for seven ape species and population-scale long-read sequence data for humans and chimpanzees, we generated a comprehensive comparative catalog of TRs. We identified over 3 million TR loci per species and nearly 2 million homologous loci between humans and chimpanzees, with overrepresentations of TRs in 5’UTRs and promoters. We see consistent evidence for stabilizing selection on TRs, particularly those located in UTRs and CDS. For instance, TRs in UTRs and CDS have increased conservation across the examined apes, reduced genetic variation within species, and increased correlation of mean allele lengths between humans and chimpanzees. To control for locus-specific mutation rate variation, we adapted an HKA-like approach to detect TRs with signatures of directional or balancing selection by comparing their divergence between species to their diversity within species. For instance, one TR with a strong signature for positive selection is in the 5’UTR of PROX1, a transcription factor essential for embryonic nervous system development, whereas we see a TR with a strong signature for balancing selection overlapping an exon in PCGF3, a gene involved in innate immune activation. Together, these results establish a population and species-resolved framework for studying TR evolution and interpreting TR variation in functional contexts.

## Profiling mutation rates and characteristics of somatic structural variants in the aging brain using long read sequencing

**Presenter:** Caitlin F. Connelly, *University Of California, Berkeley*

**Abstract:** Somatic mutation drives cellular evolution across the lifetimes of individuals. Recent research has found that different species and tissues show differences in the rate and signatures of somatic single nucleotide variants that accumulate through life. However, somatic structural variants (SVs) have remained almost entirely unexplored in healthy aging tissues despite their known roles in aging-associated processes due to technical challenges. We developed methods to identify somatic structural variants in single molecules using long-read PacBio sequencing data. Simulations of the most common SV type (deletions) found a high true positive rate (45-71%) and low false positive rate (~5%). We then applied these methods to PacBio data from mouse hippocampus and sperm. We found a lower mutation rate for somatic SVs in sperm compared to hippocampus samples, as expected, as well as differences in the types of SVs. We also found enrichment of deletion breakpoints overlapping expressed genes in each tissue. The rate of L1 retrotransposon insertions derived from the sperm data was consistent with previous publications in mouse, validating our ability to detect insertions. In the hippocampus, we found on average 20 large deletions (>1 Mbp) per sample, corroborating findings from human neurons using single cell whole genome sequencing. Finally, we applied these methods to brain samples from young and old mice and characterized somatic SV accumulation with age in different brain regions. These results highlight our ability to accurately characterize somatic SVs and will shed light on the mutational processes affecting structural variant formation in different tissues throughout aging.
