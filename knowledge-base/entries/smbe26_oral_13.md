## Human societies, past and present, in light of ancient DNA

**Presenter:** Mehmet Somel, Middle East Technical University

**Abstract:**

Perhaps akin to biological organisms, human societies are organized in myriad ways and change continuously at diverse rates, shaped by material conditions (e.g., the economy), historical contingency, stochasticity, but also agency and collective action. The proximal mechanisms driving social change, however, are only superficially understood, as reconstructions of past societies from archaeological and anthropological evidence have clear limitations. In recent years, archaeogenomics has become a complementary source of information about past societies. Here, I will discuss what we have learned about changing kinship rules from ancient DNA data contextualised with archaeological, bioarchaeological, and ethnographic evidence. I will focus on Anatolia 12,000-5,000 BP, which saw the emergence of sedentism, farming, and later, of hierarchical societies. In early sedentary communities, we find flexible rules of household social organisation, reminiscent of organisation patterns of modern-day foragers. Regarding residence rules, we infer widespread matrilocality in Neolithic Anatolia, coupled with special treatment towards adult and subadult female burials. These female-focused practices suggest a shift in gender roles in later millennia, while the timing and causes remain unclear. We further find consanguinity to be rare in the Neolithic period but more common in later periods, likely in response to emerging property relationships. While contributing to the reconstruction of the emergence of institutionalized inequality in West Eurasia, these findings may also help amend public misconceptions about the eternity and inevitability of today’s prevalent structures, from gender roles and the family to individualism and private property.
