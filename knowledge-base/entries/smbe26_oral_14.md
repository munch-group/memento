## Genetic load, drift debt, and the evolutionary management of vulnerable populations

**Presenter:** Cock Van Oosterhout, *University of East Anglia (UEA)*

**Abstract:** Conservation management still relies largely on demographic trends, and on maintaining genome-wide diversity as a practical surrogate for fitness and adaptive potential. However, populations can remain genetically vulnerable long after numbers begin to recover due to a drift debt. In this talk, I focus on genetic load: the burden of deleterious variation carried by individuals and populations, and its consequences for fitness, adaptive potential, and long-term persistence. I argue that the next challenge is not only to document genomic erosion, but also to move towards more direct estimates of fitness from genomic data, and to quantify how harmful mutations affect survival, reproduction, and viability of real populations. This is complicated by drift debt, because the genetic consequences of past bottlenecks can continue to unfold for many generations after recovery, masking true extinction risk and recovery potential. Using work on threatened bird populations such as the pink pigeon, together with forward-in-time simulations, I show how bottleneck history, inbreeding, and runs of homozygosity shape realised and masked load, and how purging may remove some highly deleterious variants while allowing other harmful mutations to accumulate. I then discuss new approaches that move beyond simple variant scoring, using digital twin simulations and AI-informed genomics to translate genomic data into biologically meaningful estimates of fitness effects. Together, these approaches can help evolutionary genomics and conservation move towards more predictive management, improving estimates of long-term viability and recovery potential, and guiding decisions on genetic rescue, captive breeding, and population recovery in a rapidly changing world.

## Genomic diversity across 200 plant species of variable Red List status

**Presenter:** Jules Perez, *UC Berkeley*

**Abstract:** Protecting the genetic diversity of threatened species arose as a major conservation priority in recent years. In vertebrates, IUCN Red List status scales reliably with metrics of genetic diversity. However, given the vast differences in life history between plants and animals, it is unclear if this relationship holds for the plant kingdom. This study investigates the degree of contemporary genetic diversity loss in plants through a meta-analysis of 200 species with publicly available genomes and resequencing data and an evaluation on the IUCN Red List.  We re-called several measures of diversity (nucleotide diversity, heterozygosity, frequency of runs of homozygosity, and historical effective population size) in species from their original published data and a standardized bioinformatic workflow. We then combined this with data on 15  functional traits—including life form, lifespan, and reproductive mode— for each species to investigate how threat status affects diversity and what species traits potentially attenuate or exaggerate diversity loss. Preliminary results indicate that the mating system explains the most variation in genetic diversity across plant species. These findings suggest that plant extinction risk assessments based on genetic diversity must integrate life-history traits to accurately contextualize genetic health.

## A practical framework for identifying genetic subpopulations and ESUs: insights for IUCN assessments and broader management

**Presenter:** Deborah Leigh, *Senckenberg Nature Research/ Goethe University*

**Abstract:** The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Red List and Green Status of Species guide global conservation priorities by evaluating species’ extinction risk and recovery status. Although such frameworks are often thought to include genetic diversity information and are considered a proxy for genetic erosion risk by some, this aspect of biodiversity remains underrepresented in assessment criteria due to technical and historical constraints. Incorporating genetic diversity into IUCN assessments is vital to ensure accurate extinction risk estimates. However, to guarantee equitable access, steps toward this goal cannot rely on molecular data alone.<br /><br />Here, we outline a flexible framework proposed by members of the IUCN SSC for Conservation Genetics that integrates molecular and non-molecular evidence to identify meaningful genetic units (IUCN-defined subpopulations and ESUs). Inclusion of such units in IUCN assessments could enhance representation of adaptive genetic diversity, better informing conservation planning and decision-making. Acceptance would also pave the way for the inclusion of genetic erosion–related criteria and realign IUCN assessments with the new genetic indicators within the Convention on Biological Diversity’s Kunming–Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework. We demonstrate the application of this framework to dozens of species with published Green Status of Species assessments, highlighting which information has been key to the successful reuse of molecular data, as well as the challenges faced.

## Genera: An Integrative AI Framework for Conservation Management using Comparative Genomics at Scale

**Presenter:** Ayshwarya Subramanian, *Cornell University*

**Abstract:** Background: Genomic data provides a critical window into how species adapt to environmental pressures. In prior work (PMID: 37104572), we developed machine-learning(ML) models that predict mammalian extinction risk using genomic variables, demonstrating that genomic variables are predictive of extinction risk and outperform ecological variables alone when used in combination. Notably, these models enable risk prediction for species classified as data-deficient by the IUCN. However, translating these genomic insights into actionable conservation management practices remains a significant bottleneck.<br /><br />Approach: Supported by the Bezos Earth Fund, we present a technical outline and conceptual framework for “Genera,” a generative AI assistant designed to bridge this gap. Our roadmap scales previous ML models trained on 240 short read-based mammalian genomes (Zoonomia consortium) to incorporate 581 high-quality vertebrate genomes (Vertebrate Genome Project Phase 1) and next-generation AI models.  We are building a multimodal deep learning architecture that integrates genomic foundation models with Large Language Models to jointly process DNA sequences, inbreeding metrics, and ecological metadata. This framework aims to translate high-dimensional genetic data into actionable management insights by generating interactive, natural-language conservation reports that evaluate simulated genetic rescue strategies based upon predicted extinction risk and genomic drivers of vulnerability. <br /><br />Outcomes: Currently in the pilot phase, Genera aims to serve as a decision-support tool for practitioners -- assisting in breeding strategies, translocations, and reintroduction planning. We present our architectural roadmap and invite community feedback to co-develop this resource over the next two years, ensuring it meets the needs of the global conservation and evolutionary biology communities.

## Functional genomic diversity and migratory survival: implications for evolutionary management of a critically endangered parrot

**Presenter:** Soleille Miller, *University of Sydney*

**Abstract:** <br />The orange-bellied parrot (Neophema chrysogaster) is a critically endangered migratory parrot endemic to Australia, facing an 87% chance of extinction by 2038. Following repeated population crashes in the wild, the species reached a low of just 3 wild-born females and 12 males in 2016. Despite intensive conservation efforts commencing in the 1980s, modelling has shown that juvenile survival during migration remains one of the top threats to population recovery. Using 94 resequenced genomes of wild (N=45) and captive (N=49) birds combined with a comprehensive meta-dataset, we investigated the genomic basis of migratory success.  Using sequences from all possible unrelated wild-born individuals between 2015 and 2023, we investigated genome-wide associations to identify candidate genomic regions potentially linked with successful migration discovering 6 significant regions. We examined the presence and frequency of these candidate genotypes within the captive breeding population that provides individuals for release. Using these insights, we provide guidance for the recovery team on selecting the most suitable captive individuals for release, aiming to retain the successful migratory genotype while preserving overall genome-wide genetic diversity. This study provides a next-step critical framework in the field of conservation genomics for the management of threatened species whereby functional genomic data is associated with a known phenotype and used to inform management actions. <br />

## Genomic Consequences of Translocations: Lessons from the Critically Endangered Pyrenean Brown Bear

**Presenter:** Léa Auclair, *National Museum Of Natural History Of Paris*

**Abstract:** In the face of the biodiversity crisis, translocations are a key conservation tool, yet their high costs often limit the number of individuals translocated. The critically endangered Pyrenean brown bear population, comprising only 10 genetic founders after reinforcement, exemplifies these challenges.  Since reinforcement, fitness data and the pedigree of all individuals have been then tracked.<br />Using whole-genome sequencing of 32 bears, we assessed runs of homozygosity (ROH), genetic diversity, and mutational load. We compared these metrics to the long-isolated Apennine brown bear population in Italy, which has a similar census size. Our results reveal that the Pyrenean population exhibits greater variance but lower values in ROH size and realized load among individuals compared to the Apennine population. <br />We further quantified the impact of inbreeding depression on individual fitness using reproductive success data for the Pyrenean population. The reproductive success is negatively correlated with both ROH-based inbreeding coefficients (FROH) and realized mutational load, with a stronger effect observed for the latter. These results contrast with the long-term persistence of the Apennine population despite its higher load, suggesting a complex interaction between historical demography and selection in shaping genetic variation.<br />Finally, forward simulations for the Pyrenean population using SLiM projected future trajectories of ROH, mutational load, and genetic diversity under alternative management scenarios. Simulations predict increasing FROH over time, leading to the unmasking of recessive deleterious mutations and further threatening population viability.<br />Our results highlight the genetic debt of some translocation programs and emphasize the need to integrate genomics into conservation planning.

## Assessing adaptive potential in Scandinavian populations of Willow ptarmigan (Lagopus lagopus) using temporal genomics

**Presenter:** Maja Svedberg, *Stockholm University*

**Abstract:** Predicting species’ responses to rapid environmental change is challenging but is especially important for cold-adapted species having limited capacity to shift ranges or adapt physiologically. For these species, genetic adaptation may be crucial for long-term survival. <br />One powerful tool to determine if species can adapt to rapid changes in climate is the usage of historical specimens. By comparing the genomic sequences of historical specimens with modern descendants, we can track evolutionary trajectories to determine the response to recent climate change and predict their future adaptive capacity. Here, we focus on willow ptarmigan (Lagopus lagopus), a Scandinavian species vulnerable to climate change. Reduced snow cover disrupts seasonal timing and causes camouflage mismatches, leading to lower survival and population declines across Scandinavia. The impact of these population declines to current levels of genetic diversity and degree of genomic erosion remains unstudied. Here, willow ptarmigan acts as a system to understand adaptation to environmental change in seasonal environments by documenting recent decline in populations with temporal genomic approaches combining museum archives and modern sampling.<br /><br />To study recent demographic trends and adaptive potential, we conducted population genomic analysis across a temporal scale using approximately 400 modern and historical samples of Scandinavian willow ptarmigans. These genomes were combined with available genomic data for willow ptarmigan from Siberia, Scotland, North America to further contextualize levels of diversity in Scandinavia. This comprehensive spatial and temporal genomic dataset will be combined with demographic modelling and population viability analysis to assess the future evolutionary potential of willow ptarmigan.

## Harnessing genetic rescue: from genomic evidence to management decisions

**Presenter:** Aja Noersgaard Buur Tengstedt

**Abstract:** Genetic rescue has long been recognized as a means of mitigating inbreeding and loss of adaptive potential in small, fragmented populations. Yet in practice, it is often postponed or avoided because the risks - particularly outbreeding depression - are difficult to assess and have historically been evaluated using indirect proxies rather than direct evidence. This disconnect between evolutionary theory and management practice has fostered a conservative bias that can delay intervention even when populations show clear signs of genetic erosion.<br /><br />I present a genomics-informed decision framework that reframes genetic rescue as a testable evolutionary hypothesis rather than a precautionary gamble. Building on and extending existing guidelines, the framework integrates genomic estimates of inbreeding, demographic history, gene flow, and signals of local adaptation to explicitly evaluate both the need for genetic rescue and the risk of maladaptive outcomes. A key advance is the inclusion of genomic inbreeding, quantified using runs of homozygosity, as an explicit entry point, ensuring that intervention is focused on populations where genetic processes likely constrain viability.<br /><br />The framework is illustrated using a fragmented butterfly metapopulation, where recent divergence, historical connectivity, and weak evidence for local adaptation together indicate low risk of outbreeding depression despite substantial genetic structure. Importantly, the framework emphasizes genomic inferences that are feasible and widely applicable across conservation contexts, including non-model systems. More broadly, this work demonstrates how population genomics can replace proxy-based rules with direct evolutionary inference, enabling more confident, timely, and transparent management decisions for vulnerable populations in a rapidly changing world.<br />

## Detecting Vulnerability Early: Genomic Signals from Fragmented Landscapes

**Presenter:** Nathalie Puggaard Elkær Andersen Ibsen, *Århus University*

**Abstract:** Even widespread and historically abundant species may be more vulnerable than commonly assumed. Several of the butterfly (Lepidoptera) species we study show genomic signatures consistent with early-stage genomic erosion, despite retaining relatively high nucleotide diversity. All species exhibit drastic recent declines in effective population size (Ne). This apparent mismatch may reflect a “drift debt”, where genetic drift acts more slowly in historically large populations with high standing genetic diversity, causing a lag in the loss of genetic diversity. Functional connectivity, i.e. the interaction between habitat connectivity and innate dispersal ability, plays a key role in mediating population genomic responses to habitat fragmentation. In this context, we examined seven species spanning a gradient of dispersal ability, Aglais urticae, Pieris napi, Gonepteryx rhamni, Maniola jurtina, Thymelicus lineola, Lycaena virgaureae, and Coenonympha tullia, across the highly fragmented Danish landscape. We assessed genetic diversity, population structure, inbreeding, genetic load, and historical Ne trajectories using genomic data. The population genomic patterns differ strongly among species and are shaped by dispersal ability. Species with low dispersal ability exhibit relatively higher levels of inbreeding and stronger population structure than highly dispersive species, indicating increased population isolation and reduced gene flow. By integrating life-history traits with genomic and spatial data, this study identifies early genomic indicators of population vulnerability and highlights the importance of functional connectivity and demographic history in shaping contemporary and future evolutionary resilience in fragmented landscapes.

## Costs and benefits of genetic rescue of the critically endangered helmeted honeyeater from another subspecies

**Presenter:** Alexandra Pavlova, *Monash University*

**Bio:** I am passionate about using genomics to help managers design optimal interventions to improve health and persistence of wildlife populations, species and communities.

**Abstract:** Small, isolated populations rapidly lose genetic diversity, reducing adaptive capacity and increasing inbreeding and fitness loss. Genetic rescue—adding individuals from more diverse populations—can restore fitness. When sources of gene flow are ecologically similar and recently diverged from a recipient population, outbreeding depression and disruption of local adaptation—the common risks of gene flow—are unlikely. But when only more distantly related or differently adapted sources are available, adaptive genetic management can be used, with gene flow adjusted in response to measured fitness outcomes.<br />The critically endangered Australian helmeted honeyeater, a small and isolated subspecies of the yellow‑tufted honeyeater, provides a rare case study of genetic rescue using migrants from a neighboring subspecies. We first conducted captive trials to test whether admixed pairs had higher reproductive performance than pure helmeted honeyeater pairs. Mixed pairs bred more readily, produced more nestlings, and showed less male‑biased chick sex-ratio, indicating strong fitness benefits of admixture.<br />We then assessed whether the benefits persisted under the harsher conditions of the wild by monitoring field fitness across six breeding seasons. We examined whether variation in genetic diversity and inbreeding predicted survival and breeding outcomes. Preliminary analyses showed that more genetically diverse captive‑bred chicks had higher survival in the wild, and that less-inbred wild birds were more likely to breed. These results support continued genetic augmentation and meta‑population genetic management across remnant, reintroduced, and captive populations. More broadly, our results demonstrate that carefully monitored genetic rescue—even using moderately diverged sources—can substantially improve fitness in small, isolated populations.

## Environmental effects on the origin and establishment of Arabidopsis arenosa 


**Presenter:** Yu Cheng, *Department of Botany, Faculty of Science, Charles University*

**Abstract:** The effect of the environment on the establishment of polyploidy has been a long-term mystery, and it has been hypothesized that polyploids are better adapted to temperature extremes than their diploid progenitors. Given the increasing challenges that plants and animals face due to global warming, this study explores polyploids' molecular basis of their adaptation to thermal and precipitation fluctuations, using Arabidopsis arenosa from the family Brassicaceae as the model system. This species crosses multiple climatic zones along its distribution, and its various intraspecific lineages are adapted to diverse environmental conditions, including northern latitudes.<br /><br />In this study, I utilized the first pan-genome of A. arenosa and genotype–environment association analysis with 250 genome-sequenced diploid and 473 tetraploid populations (total 723 individuals) to investigate the impact of harsh environments on the adaptation of A. arenosa, namely selected genes. Also, genetic offset was used to predict maladaptation comparing diploids with tetraploids.<br /><br />Based on the findings from this analysis, significant differences in single nucleotide polymorphisms strongly associated with temperature and precipitation were observed between diploids and tetraploids. Our findings indicate that diploid climate adaptation is primarily associated with metabolic, stress-response and developmental pathways, including carbohydrate and lipid metabolism and hormone signaling. In contrast, autotetraploid adaptation is enriched for regulatory, epigenetic and cellular homeostasis functions, suggesting greater buffering capacity under environmental change. This may explain why tetraploids exhibit robust adaptation across broader environmental regimes, as reflected by lower genetic offset.
