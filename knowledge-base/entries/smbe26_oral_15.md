## Genomic legacy of near extinction and intensive management in the pink pigeon

**Presenter:** Carolina Pacheco, *Lund University*

**Abstract:** Intensive management can rescue population size yet fail to rescue genetic diversity. The pink pigeon (Nesoenas mayeri), endemic to Mauritius, declined to 9-10 known wild birds in the 1990s but recovered to ~600 in the following decades through captive breeding, reintroductions, predator and disease control and supplementary feeding. Given the well-documented demographic trajectory and decades of monitoring, this species provides a powerful system for quantifying how intensive management shapes genomic trajectories in recovered populations. We generated 150 whole-genomes spanning 180 years, capturing before and after the population nadir. Using this time series, we quantified genome-wide and immune gene diversity, inbreeding, putative load, kinship structure, and demographic history.<br />We document a ~20% loss of genetic diversity between historical and 1990s individuals, accompanied by rising inbreeding, evidenced by an increase of medium- to long-length runs of homozygosity. Genetic diversity continued to decline during the period of most rapid demographic recovery, with no evidence of rebound between 1990s and contemporary individuals. Kinship analyses show strong early-recovery reproductive skew, with contemporary birds descending from only a few lineages, which could help explain the continued loss of diversity despite rising population size. Moreover, SLiM simulations show that gains from future genetic rescue will be limited, as the captive population has also lost substantial diversity.<br />Our results illustrate the challenges of restoring genomic diversity in severely bottlenecked populations and highlight the importance of evolutionary-informed management strategies that account for effective population size, lineage representation, and the timing and sources of reintroduction events in genetically depleted populations.

## Phenotypic plasticity shapes genetic variation and patterns of inbreeding and extinction risk

**Presenter:** Aleix Palahí i Torres, *Uppsala University*

**Abstract:** Phenotypic plasticity is a major mechanism of adaptation to changing environments. When it alters an individuals’ dispersal capacity it can modulate gene flow, shaping connectivity between populations and genetic diversity within them. The water strider Aquarius najas exemplifies this dynamic. Across Europe, it presents a latitudinal cline in wing frequency: Southern populations are wing-polymorphic and can disperse, while Scandinavian populations are wingless and sedentary, making it an ideal system to study how plasticity-mediated dispersal shapes genomic vulnerability at large geographical scales.<br /><br />We sequenced >700 individuals and discovered extreme inbreeding in Northern populations, being characterized by low genetic diversity and strong isolation-by-distance, with no gene flow between rivers. Contrastingly, Southern polymorphic populations have ten-fold higher diversity and low differentiation between distant sites from different water basins, indicating flight-mediated dispersal. While ecological and genetic models partially explain the cline in diversity through Southern glacial refugia followed by successive Northern bottlenecks, the connectivity between distant polymorphic populations highlights the direct effect of wing polyphenism on diversity.<br /><br />This study integrates population genomics, demographic inference and ecological models to reveal a highly fragile species facing different threats at each distribution edge. Southern populations, while genetically rich, face increasing habitat instability due to climate change-induced droughs; Northern populations are genetically eroded and isolated due to a complete loss of dispersal capacity. Our findings demonstrate how plasticity modulates dispersal patterns and therefore acts as an important mechanism that shapes genetic diversity and thus alters the equilbrium between mutation, drift, gene flow and selection across a latitudinal gradient.<br />

## Pedigree‑based analysis reveals rapid de novo insertion and purging of retroviral integrations in the koala genome

**Presenter:** Guilherme B. Neumann

**Abstract:** Retroviruses that colonize the germline become heritable genetic elements and can drive rapid genomic change. The koala (Phascolarctos cinereus) is experiencing recent germline colonization events: KoRV first invaded the koala genome ~312,191 years ago, phaCin‑β ~1.6 million years ago, whereas phaCin‑β‑like colonizations are much older (~6.9 Mya). We analyzed integration‑site segregation and diversity of endogenous KoRV, phaCin‑β and phaCin‑β‑like elements in 111 pedigreed koalas from the San Diego Zoo Wildlife Alliance and seven European zoos. Using multigenerational pedigrees and individual health records, we document rapid evolutionary dynamics—ongoing generation of novel heritable insertions, differential persistence among loci, and recurrent loss of integrations, notably from proto‑oncogenes. Of 714 detected integrations, many do not persist in the extant population, and among 55 parent–offspring triads we found 21 integrations present in offspring but absent in both parents, consistent with recent germline insertion or early somatic‑to‑germline transmission. We also link specific integrations to phenotypes (leukemia, fertility, longevity) to estimate genetic risk scores and develop a longevity breeding index to reduce neoplasia risk in captive populations. Together, these results highlight the recent and ongoing impact of retroviral colonization on koala genome evolution and offer actionable metrics for conservation breeding.

## Parallel adaptation and genomic vulnerability of bottlenose dolphins in a changing ocean

**Presenter:** Svenja Marfurt, *University of Zurich*

**Abstract:** The pace and magnitude of anthropogenic change are unprecedented, making biodiversity conservation one of the defining challenges of our time. Marine ecosystems are especially vulnerable, yet adaptive responses in marine organisms remain poorly understood. Using one of the largest whole-genome sequencing datasets generated for a marine mammal, we applied a seascape genomics framework to Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphins (Tursiops aduncus) distributed across the environmentally heterogeneous Australian coastline. Genome-wide variation strongly reflected geography, consistent with a northern origin followed by two independent north-to-south colonisation pathways along the east and west coasts. In contrast, putatively adaptive SNPs grouped individuals by tropical versus temperate waters irrespective of geographic distance or coastline, providing rare evidence for parallel adaptation to tropical conditions on both coasts. This pattern was likely facilitated by selection on standing genetic variation retained from a tropical northern origin and highlights the importance of considering demographic history when interpreting signatures of selection. Together, these results suggest tropical-temperate, ecotype-like differentiation across Australian waters. Extending this framework into a predictive context, we estimated genomic offset (GO) under moderate (RCP4.5) and high (RCP8.5) emission scenarios. Southeastern Australia consistently showed the highest offsets, whereas the more climatically stable southwest may represent a potential refugium. High GO values indicated that some populations would require substantial genomic change to remain adapted, posing a particular challenge given dolphins’ long generation times. By uncovering environment-associated adaptive genetic variation and identifying areas of predicted genomic vulnerability, our work highlights the importance of genomics-informed conservation strategies in a fast-changing ocean.

## When Social Structure Shapes Genomes: Modeling Demography and Genomic Signals in Reef Fishes

**Presenter:** Diego Hartasánchez, *Cinvestav*

**Abstract:** Understanding how population dynamics impact genomic signals is a major challenge for population and conservation genomics. Many reef fishes exhibit complex social systems, such as size- or rank-based dominance, socially controlled reproduction, and sex change, yet most population genetic models and empirical sampling designs typically assume random mating and representative sampling. <br /><br />Here, we present a spatially explicit, non–Wright–Fisher SLiM model developed to investigate the joint demographic and population-genetic dynamics of reef fishes under realistic social organization. The model explicitly couples social rank, reproductive access, sex-specific life histories, and site attachment across discrete habitat patches. Individual fate (growth, survival, sex, and reproduction) is determined by local social context, generating emergent deviations in reproductive success and non-random genetic contributions across generations. This framework enables the exploration of how social regulation alters effective population size, genetic diversity, and allele-frequency trajectories under stable and perturbed demographic conditions.<br /><br />Beyond characterizing the genetic consequences of social structure itself, the model can also evaluate sampling scheme biases. We contrast population-genetic inferences derived from random sampling of individuals with those based on breeders only, rank-restricted samples, or uneven spatial sampling schemes. This allows direct quantification of how commonly used empirical sampling strategies may misestimate diversity, relatedness, or demographic history when social structure is ignored.<br /><br />By linking demography, social behavior, and genetics in a unified simulation framework, we characterize how social systems can shape evolutionary responses and provide a controlled testbed for assessing the robustness of genomic indicators used in reef fish management.<br /><br />

## Tracing the Genomic History of Spotted Owls and Their Relatives

**Presenter:** Murillo Rodrigues, *Oregon Health & Science University*

**Abstract:** Spotted owls (Strix occidentalis) comprise three subspecies that inhabit mountain forests in western North America. Over the past one hundred years, spotted owls have experienced extensive habitat loss, particularly in the Pacific Northwest, where they have become a symbol for the preservation of old-growth forest ecosystems. These threats have presumably intensified with the recent expansion of barred owls (their sister species) into spotted owl territories. To investigate the ancient and recent demographic history of spotted owls and their close relatives, we obtained and analyzed over 350 owl genomes from across the species’ range, including 14 mexican spotted owls, a subspecies for which genomic data has been virtually non-existent. We found substantial population structure across subspecies, particularly between Northern/California spotted owls and Mexican spotted owls, which diverged around a hundred thousand years ago. Using SFS- and genealogy-based methods, we found signs of admixture between Northern and California, but no evidence for admixture between California and Mexican spotted owls. We sampled several spotted-barred owl hybrids across the West Coast, most of which are genetically F1s or backcrosses, pointing to a strong reproductive barrier after the first generations. We also looked for evidence of ancient admixture between the Mexican spotted owls and barred owls, as they may have coexisted longer than previously assumed. Our study provides insights into the recent and ancient demography of a flagship species that has been threatened by anthropogenic change, with important implications for ongoing conservation debates.

## Persistence and Resilience: Tracking the Genomes of North American Brown Bears 

**Presenter:** Joanna Kelley, *University Of California Santa Cruz*

**Abstract:** The brown bear (Ursus arctos) has a Holarctic distribution; however, it has experienced severe range contractions and fragmentation over the past century and a half, primarily due to human persecution and habitat loss. The complex history of brown bears in North America is not yet fully resolved. To address this, we sequenced 268 brown bear genomes, spanning all populations in the contiguous US and most of their contemporary range in Alaska and Canada. Our results show continent-wide population declines, signatures of recent and severe inbreeding in some isolated populations, but minimal genetic load. All populations exhibited a marked and rapid decline beginning approximately 250 years ago. However, we see limited accumulation of deleterious and loss-of-function alleles following population decline in North American brown bears. The results also reveal evidence of selection on genes associated with hibernation and dietary phenotypes. We conclude with discussions potential implications for the species’ future persistence.

## The genomic consequences of introgression for an island-limited rail

**Presenter:** Kees Wanders, *University of Copenhagen*

**Abstract:** Small island-limited populations accumulate genetic load, reducing population fitness. Gene flow from more abundant populations has the potential to ‘rescue’ this effect, by increasing genetic diversity and providing new opportunities for selection against locally fixed harmful alleles. However, harmful recessive polymorphisms are far more abundant in large populations, and their introgression into a small island population could also increase genetic load. The long-term consequences of gene flow from abundant source populations to small sink populations are therefore unclear.<br />The Henderson crake, Zapornia atra, shows all the hallmarks of island life – increased body mass, flightlessness, a small census population size and reduced heterozygosity. The species is limited to a 37km2 island in the Eastern South Pacific, whilst its more widespread (flying) relative, the spotless crake, Zapornia tabuensis, occupies a vast range across Oceania and the South Pacific. Here we use whole-genome-sequencing data from modern samples and museum skins to investigate the demographic history of these species. We show that the Henderson crake has received migrants from the nearby (Eastern Pacific) race of spotless crakes, and that this gene flow occurred more recently than the bottleneck associated with island colonisation (<200kya), but far enough in the past for large admixture tracts to be broken down (>1kya). We then investigate the long-term consequences of gene flow by identifying small regions of the genome with strong signatures of introgression, and testing whether such regions carry an increase or a decrease in genetic load.

## Leveraging standardised frameworks for genetic monitoring of populations

**Presenter:** Chris Barratt, *Wageningen University And Research*

**Abstract:** Rapid environmental change, compounded by anthropogenic activity, is imposing novel selection pressures on biodiversity. The ability of species to persist under these conditions depends on both the magnitude of environmental change and the genetic diversity (neutral and adaptive) present within intraspecific populations. Despite substantial advances in genomics and spatial informatics, standardized tools for assessing population vulnerability across taxonomic groups and geographic regions remain limited. Here, we briefly outline Life on the Edge (https://cdbarratt.github.io/Life_on_the_edge.github.io/), an integrative framework that combines population genomics with spatial modelling to quantify predicted climate exposure, neutral and adaptive genetic diversity, landscape connectivity, and population vulnerability under future global change scenarios. As an empirical demonstration using novel genomic datasets, we apply multiple components of this toolbox to several economically important aquaculture species in the Netherlands (Turbot, Zander, Eel, Mussel, Flat oyster, Pacific oyster), enabling the identification of genetically diverse populations with high conservation value. Our study highlights the utility of integrative genomic–spatial frameworks for evaluating genetic diversity and population vulnerability in applied and conservation-relevant contexts.

## Predicting Mammal-Wide Variant Pathogenicity, Uncertainty, Gene Essentiality, and Genetic Load

**Presenter:** Paulina Nuñez Valencia, *Center For Genomic Regulation (CRG)*

**Abstract:** Biodiversity loss demands tools that translate the surge of genomes into conservation insight.  We present a mammal-wide deep learning framework that predicts the impact of missense variants, quantifies uncertainty, summarizes gene essentiality, and estimates individual genetic load by integrating over the most probable sequence carried by each individual. Our approach couples protein language models with calibration to the observed spectrum of mammalian amino-acid variation. This allows placing variants on a continuous pathogenicity spectrum for ranking beyond binary labels, paired with an uncertainty estimate that allows users to filter low-confidence calls.In benchmarks spanning human, mouse, and dog, calibrated scores discriminate pathogenic from benign variants, classify disease versus healthy cohorts, and prioritize variants with severity-consistent ranking. Across four threatened mammals—black-footed ferret, Iberian lynx, vaquita, and clouded leopard—we map deleterious burden, nominate candidate genes concordant with reported health issues, identify variants for targeting in breeding programs, and recover signatures of demography and management. Together, these scores turn raw genomes into actionable hypotheses for monitoring, assisted breeding, and reintroduction planning.

## Investigating Parallel Adaptation to Altitude and Response to Climate Change in Neotropical Butterflies

**Presenter:** Henry North, *University Of Cambridge*

**Abstract:** Neotropical butterflies offer an exceptional system to study repeatability of evolution across lineages facing similar selective pressures. Here, we investigate parallel adaptation to altitude across 12 species of the Heliconiini and Ithomiini clades to assess the extent to which adaptation is repeatable in this system. Historical constraints often limit repeatability, with the tension extending to the genetic level. Although some loci repeatedly underlie phenotypic evolution, forming “genetic hotspots” or showing gene reuse, the predictability of evolution at the genetic level varies across taxa. This predictability tends to decline with increased divergence, e.g., adaptation at the global level. However, local adaptation to replicated climatic gradients provides a clear mean of testing repeatability. Many species within our focus clades occur across a shared, extreme altitudinal gradient and are particularly well-suited for repeatability studies at the local level. <br /><br />Members of these clades demonstrate strong turnover in species composition and abundance across the Andean slope. This reflects climatic clines. Together, these communities form natural “replay experiments,” enabling tests of whether similar genomic hotspots are repeatedly observed in adaptation to altitude and, hence, temperature gradients. Adaptive divergence has been previously demonstrated in Heliconius erato and melpomene across low- to highland populations. Utilising methods in evolutionary genomics, we extend this to study adaptive potential in response to environmental challenges in other members of these clades. The results will prove crucial in understanding Lepidopteran response to global warming, and more broadly, climate change.

## Restoring Vulnerable Populations via Genomic Rescue

**Presenter:** Laura Bertola, *National Centre for Biological Sciences*

**Abstract:** Human-driven environmental change has left many wildlife species fragmented into small, isolated populations. Such populations are especially vulnerable to extinction, not only due to demographic stochasticity and environmental pressures, but also because of genetic factors: loss of diversity, increased inbreeding, and the accumulation of deleterious mutations. While the principles of drift, inbreeding, and mutation load are well established in molecular evolution, their combined role in determining extinction risk is less well understood.<br /><br />Conservation interventions increasingly draw on genetic tools, including genomic rescue. Current practice often emphasizes demographic recovery or increasing diversity. Yet even when these targets are met, genetic load may persist, accumulate further and eventually become expressed, especially when both source and target populations share deleterious alleles. Thus, interventions aimed at increasing diversity can inadvertently cause harm if the underlying evolutionary processes and resultant genetic patterns are not carefully understood.<br /><br />We have developed a molecular-evolutionary framework for genomic rescue, which integrates demographic history, standing genetic diversity, mutation load, and local adaptation to inform conservation strategies. We will highlight theoretical insights and showcase a high-profile real-world example, the melanistic tigers of Simlipal, where genomic data have informed decisions about translocations and population reinforcement.<br /><br />By bringing together theoretical, empirical, and applied perspectives, we aim to connect molecular evolutionary principles with urgent conservation challenges. We argue that a genomic framework for population rescue will not only advance fundamental understanding of the vulnerability of small populations, but also improve the long-term outcomes of real-world interventions.<br />

## Using population structure and admixture analyses to inform management decisions of native caribou in Greenland

**Presenter:** Nuno Filipe Gomes Martins, *Department Of Biology, University of Copenhagen*

**Abstract:** Caribou have long held fundamental ecological and cultural importance in Greenland as a key local sustenance resource. Over past centuries, caribou numbers in Greenland have fluctuated markedly, and the species has entirely disappeared from the eastern and northern coasts. In the 1950s, domesticated reindeer from Norway were introduced into West Greenland to increase caribou numbers and were later translocated to several other west‑coast locations. Despite the local importance of this species, no broad analysis of genetic diversity and population structure has been performed. Here, we investigate the contemporary evolutionary history of Greenlandic caribou by analysing more than 100 whole genomes from present‑day samples collected across their current range to assess population structure and the extent of admixture with domesticated reindeer. <br />We find clear population structure within Greenland (FST above ~0.05), with the strongest differentiation between the northernmost distribution in West Greenland, Inglefield Land, and all other regions (FST > 0.3). We also find substantial recent admixture with domesticated reindeer in several regions.<br />These findings have major implications for management, particularly regarding the threatened genetic integrity of native caribou, the delimitation of management units, and decisions about reindeer relocations within or import to Greenland. Management advice based on our results will aim to improve long‑term sustainable harvest and we hope our analyses can thereby help secure the future survival of native Greenlandic caribou.

## Structural variant load under demographic change: How a small fly can provide big insights

**Presenter:** Jana Wold, *Universite De Rennes*

**Abstract:** The democratization of whole-genome sequencing platforms has better informed conservation management strategies by providing high-resolution data on population diversity and structure. However, there is a tension between management strategies that aim to maintain the maximum level of genomic diversity as a means of preserving adaptive potential and the possibility of retaining, or introducing, deleterious alleles in small populations. To date, SNPs have been the genomic marker of choice for estimating key metrics of genomic health, including the prevalence of deleterious alleles (i.e., mutation load). Yet another class of genomic variants, structural variants (SVs), have been shown to impact a higher proportion of the genome and intersect genes and gene regions more often than SNPs. In addition, SVs can alter mutation and recombination rates and are generally more deleterious than SNPs. While a growing body of literature is identifying characteristics of SVs that increase the likelihood of deleteriousness, the interaction of recombination and selection on SV diversity over time under various demographies is largely unexplored in threatened species. We implement a two-pronged approach of evolutionary models and evolve and resequence experiments in the seaweed fly (Coelopa frigida), a globally-populous species with well-described SV diversity, to predict and test the effects of demography on SNP- and SV-load. This highly manipulable model system allows us to assess the impact of SNP- and SV-load on fitness over successive generations and presents an opportunity to identify frameworks for mitigating the consequences of deleterious variants, while maximizing genome-wide diversity in species of conservation concern.

## Population Genomics of the Rice’s Whale Reveals Long-term Isolation, Admixture, and Genetic Distinctiveness 

**Presenter:** Diana Aguilar Gomez, *Ucla*

**Abstract:** Rice’s whale (Balaenoptera ricei) is a newly described baleen whale species that was long considered to be a Bryde’s whale because the species are difficult to differentiate using external morphological characters. The current abundance estimate for Rice’s whales is approximately 51 (95 % CI: 20–130) individuals, making it one of the most endangered baleen whales worldwide. Using whole-genome sequences from 25 individuals, we reconstruct the evolutionary and demographic history of Rice’s whale and assess its genetic health. Genomic analyses reinforce its distinctiveness from Bryde’s whales, and support that Rice’s whale has persisted as a single, small, and geographically restricted population in the northern Gulf of Mexico for tens of thousands of years. Despite having a long-term small effective population size, Rice’s whale genomes do not show signatures of recent inbreeding. Runs of homozygosity (ROH) account for a substantial proportion of the genome. However, long ROH are rare, indicating that genome-wide homozygosity largely reflects historically small population size rather than recent inbreeding. We detect clear genomic evidence for a pulse of admixture approximately 500 years ago from a Bryde’s whale–like lineage. This admixture event increased heterozygosity in Rice’s whale genomes but had little effect on deleterious variation. Our simulations indicate that a recent population collapse to as few as 100 breeding individuals places the species at high risk of future inbreeding unless population growth occurs. However, the effect of these recent demographic events on the genetic load, or overall population fitness, is more modest.

## Heterosis vs. adaptation: genomic insights from a 25-year mixed-source plant translocation program

**Presenter:** Olivier Brisset, *Muséum National D'histoire Naturelle*

**Abstract:** Mixed-source translocation can reduce immediate extinction risk in endangered populations, but their long-term consequences remain debated. Arenaria grandiflora L. (Caryophyllaceae) experienced a drastic decline in the Fontainebleau Forest (Ile-de-France) in the late 20th century, likely due to inbreeding depression. To save it from extinction, local and non-local (Chinon) plants were multiplied in vitro and introduced in three unoccupied sites in Fontainebleau in 1999. Yearly monitoring of plant's fitness coupled with analyses based on microsatellites revealed better fitness of admixed individuals. To gain more insights into the genetic consequences of the translocation, we whole genome sequenced a time series consisting of : i) individuals from the reintroduced populations (2007 and 2023) ii) part of the original clones; iii) samples from the 19th century from Fontainebleau (16 individuals) and Chinon (14 individuals) stored in the MNHN herbarium. Reintroduced populations exhibited increased genome-wide diversity and reduced inbreeding (shorter runs of homozygosity) relative to pre-translocation populations. Extant individuals showed wide variation in ancestry proportions, with non-local ancestry increasing more than expected under neutrality between 2007 and 2023, consistent with previous microsatellite-based results. To disentangle the roles of heterosis and local adaptation, we are estimating the variation in mutation load within the temporal series and searching for genomic signatures of the potential advantage of non-local alleles. Finally, we are conducting forward-in-time simulations using SLiM to predict the future evolutionary trajectory of the reintroduced populations and inform conservation management. This study provides a rare opportunity to assess the real-time genomic consequences of a mixed-source translocation program.
