## What drives genome expansions in the fungal kingdom? From populations to deep timescales

**Presenter:** Daniel Croll, *University of Neuchatel*

**Abstract:** The mechanisms underpinning variation in genome size across eukaryotes have attracted significant attention ever since the discovery of the C-value paradox and the extent of non-coding DNA in most genomes. Fungal genome sizes exhibit more than 100-fold variation, largely driven by the expansion of repetitive sequences such as transposable elements (TEs). Hence, the fungal kingdom provides an exciting study system to address fundamental questions of genome evolution. Variation in genome structure (i.e. plasticity) extends to the intraspecific level in most species studied to date and enables investigation of proximate drivers such as active TEs, recombination hotspots and genomic instability. In this talk, I will address how capturing the interplay of TE dynamics, suppression and adaptive evolution provides a powerful framework to understand genome evolution at short evolutionary timescales. Activation of TEs can generate beneficial genetic variants in pathogenic fungi, initiate chromosomal degeneration and trigger incipient genome expansions. Furthermore, I will show how the evolution of lifestyles and genomic defense against TEs allows us to reconstruct genome dynamics over deep timescales. Taken together, the fungal kingdom provides unique opportunities to connect population-level processes creating genomic variability to how genome structures emerged across a eukaryotic kingdom.

## An in-vivo approach to population 3D genomics: age and sex affect 3D genome in a healthy controlled cohort of chickens.

**Presenter:** Juan Antonio Rodríguez, *Globe Institute, University Of Copenhagen*

**Abstract:** Linear DNA sequence variation has been extensively studied at the population level, yet three-dimensional (3D) folding of the genome and its diversity across individuals in healthy populations remains largely unexplored. Likewise, while epigenomic features are known to be fluid and dynamic in response to developmental and environmental cues, a systematic population-level assessment of 3D genome variation is still lacking.<br /><br />Here, we investigate population-level variation in 3D genome organization using a well-controlled cohort of Ross chickens (Gallus gallus). We generated Hi-C data from liver samples of 16 individuals (8 males, 8 females), with balanced sampling at two life stages (day 7, day 35). For each individual, we also obtained RNA and whole-genome DNA sequencing data. We quantified 3D genome differences across multiple levels, including interchromosomal contacts, chromatin compartments, looping, and TADs. Hi-C data showed stark clustering by age and sex explaining a large proportion of variance. Interchromosomal contacts, as well as long-range compartmentalization and decay profiles also followed sex-specific trajectories between days 7 and 35. Moreover, integrating Hi-C with RNA-seq data highlighted several sex-specific differentially expressed genes involved in liver metabolism and chromatin structure remodeling, potentially explaining the observed sex-specific 3D structural differences.<br />Our results highlight how often-overlooked variables such as age and sex may condition 3D genome organization in a tissue-specific manner, revealed by unexpected sex-specific plasticity even over short developmental timescales. This study provides a framework for future analyses of population-level 3D genomic variation and underscores the risks of interpreting 3D structural features without accounting for common population variation.

## Beyond the textbook centromere: genome evolution across monocentric–holocentric transitions in Drosera

**Presenter:** Aaryan Bhatia, *Max Planck Institute For Plant Breeding Research*

**Abstract:** Textbook models of eukaryotic chromosomes are built around the monocentric chromosome, where centromere function is confined to a single region. However, holocentric chromosomes—where centromeric activity is distributed chromsome-wide, have evolved repeatedly and independently across eukaryotes, including insects and plants. Despite this striking convergence, the mechanisms enabling transitions between centromeric architectures, and their consequences for genome organization, remain poorly understood. <br /><br />The carnivorous plant genus Drosera provides a powerful system to address these questions. Within a single genus, Drosera includes both monocentric and holocentric species, with evidence for multiple independent transitions, allowing centromere evolution to be studied at an unusually fine scale.To investigate the genomic basis and evolutionary consequences of these transitions, we generated chromosome-level assemblies representing both architectures. <br /><br />Using a whole-genome comparative framework, we associate centromeric transitions with their potential causes and effects across multiple scales, including fine-scale patterns of gene gain/loss, enrichment and depletion of functional categories, and genus-wide comparisons of chromosomal fission, fusion, and rearrangement. We further characterize differences in genome organization, with emphasis on conserved genes involved in meiosis and kinetochore assembly. <br /><br />We find early evidence for loss of the centromere deposition chaperone KNL2 alongside extensive chromosomal rearrangements consistent with holocentric organization at the base of Drosera. In addition, we identify multiple independent hexaploidy events within the genus, accompanied by recurrent reuse of chromosomal rearrangement sites.<br /><br />Together, these results establish Drosera as a tractable evolutionary model for dissecting the rules, constraints, and consequences of centromere architecture transitions, with implications for chromosome biology and genome evolution.

## An increase in chromosome copy number drives rapid adaptation in a cold-adapted bacterium

**Presenter:** Macarena Toll-Riera, *Institut de Biologia Evolutiva (IBE-CSIC)*

**Abstract:** Large genome rearrangements, such as alterations in the number of chromosomes (aneuploidy) and copy number variations, are a common mechanism for rapidly responding to stress in eukaryotes. Whether this is also the case in prokaryotes is not known, but at least one-tenth of bacterial species have genomes with more than one chromosome. To investigate bacterial adaptation to stress, we used the cold-adapted bacterium Pseudoalteromonas haloplanktis TAC125, which has two chromosomes (chromosome I, chromosome II). We adapted twelve populations of P. haloplanktis to severe nutrient stress for 200 generations. We sequenced 56 endpoint clones and identified two main evolutionary paths underpinning adaptation to the stress conditions: an increase in the number of copies of chromosome II, and mutations affecting outer membrane proteins. The increase in chromosome II copy number was very frequent and emerged independently in all populations, suggesting that it is adaptive. Chromosome II codes for the genes responsible for transporting and metabolizing D-gluconate, which is the only carbon source present in the medium. We hypothesize that an increased number of copies of chromosome II is beneficial because it increases the expression of genes involved in D-gluconate metabolism. Our results suggest that genome plasticity provides a rapid mechanism for bacterial adaptation to stress.

## Genome plasticity through a large chromosomal inversion structures phenotypic and regulatory responses to environmental variation

**Presenter:** Ana Lindeza, *University Of Helsinki*

**Abstract:** Structural variation represents a powerful, and often underexplored, source of genome plasticity with the potential to reorganize genomic architecture and shape how organisms respond to environmental variation. Among such variants, large chromosomal inversions can act by suppressing recombination and coupling linked genes and regulatory elements, facilitating both phenotypic and regulatory responses to selection. However, it remains unclear how these inversions shape phenotypic and regulatory differences across environments. Here, we investigate the evolutionary consequences of a large chromosomal inversion on chromosome Omy5 in Rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) using a long-term common-garden experiment. This inversion, harboring approximately 1200 protein-coding genes, has been associated with alternative life-histories strategies, metabolism and circadian regulation, making it a strong candidate to explain both phenotypic and regulatory responses to environmental variation. Over two years, we followed 12 full-sib families reared under control and food-restricted conditions to test how inversion genotypes shape phenotypic expression and genotype-by-environment interactions (G×E) in fitness-related traits. We find that inversion genotypes, particularly individuals carrying the double inversion, show pronounced G×E effects, including consistent divergence in migration-related traits and reduced growth, under resource limitation. By integrating long-term phenotyping with transcriptomic profiling, preliminary RNA-seq results reveal important regulatory differences between inversion genotypes, with dozens of differentially expressed genes within the inverted genomic region itself. Together, our findings highlight how genome plasticity arising from structural rearrangements can generate environmentally dependent phenotypes by integrating genomic architecture and regulatory variation, with implications for species adaptive potencial and resiliance under climate change.<br />

## Chromosomal loss trajectories following whole-genome duplication in cancer

**Presenter:** Máire Ní Leathlobhair, *Trinity College Dublin*

**Abstract:** From plants surviving climate shifts to tumours evading physiological constraints, whole genome duplication (WGD) exemplifies extreme genome plasticity. In species evolution, ancient polyploidy events have been followed by rediploidization, the transition of a polyploid genome (usually tetraploid) toward a more stable diploid state through selective loss and reorganization of duplicated genes over millions of years. Whether analogous depolyploidization occurs during somatic evolution in cancer has not been systematically explored.<br /><br />We developed a computational framework exploiting copy number features to detect and quantify depolyploidization in tumour genomes. Analysis of large cancer cohorts reveals progressive decline in balanced tetraploid genome proportions following genome doubling across most cancer types. Chromosomal loss rates vary substantially: tumour types with frequent WGD exhibit faster breakdown of tetraploidy, while rare-WGD tumours retain balanced states longer. Extensive chromosome loss leading to hypodiploidy is widespread and clinically relevant, typically associated with elevated chromosomal instability, chromothripsis, and poor prognosis. However, complete depolyploidization to a diploid or pseudo-diploid state, though rare, is detectable across most cancer types.<br /><br />Critically, depolyploidized tumours display genomic signatures distinguishing them from stable diploids. Despite standard ploidy analyses grouping them together, depolyploidized genomes exhibit fundamentally different chromosomal architectures, elevated instability, and distinct loss-of-heterozygosity signatures. As ploidy decreases following WGD, tumours show increased replication and proteotoxic stress, and altered dependencies on ploidy-specific essential genes.<br /><br />These analyses connect organismal and somatic evolution, exploring shared principles governing genome plasticity and highlighting the potential value of comparative frameworks for understanding what drives and constrains genome evolution following polyploidy.

## An epigenetically induced hypermutator state reshapes genome architecture through elevated structural variation

**Presenter:** Ivona Glavincheska, *ETH Zurich*

**Abstract:** Hypermutator states can alter evolutionary dynamics by increasing heritable variation, yet the mechanisms that cause elevated mutation rates beyond canonical defects in DNA repair remain poorly characterized. Cytosine DNA methylation (5mC) is a conserved mechanism of transposable element (TE) repression and therefore represents an epigenetic control point for mutation rates. In principle, disruption of 5mC-mediated TE silencing would increase the frequency of TE-associated genetic variation, but direct experimental evidence linking methylation loss to elevated rates of structural genome change is limited. Natural polymorphism in 5mC in the fungal pathogen Zymoseptoria tritici, provides a tractable system to explore this question. Loss-of-function of the DNA methyltransferase DIM2 has led to near-complete loss of 5mC, raising the question of how variation in epigenetic TE control influences genome stability and evolutionary trajectories. Here, we combine experimental evolution with long-read and epigenomic sequencing to profile parental and experimentally evolved lines that differ in DIM2 activity. Building on our recent finding that TE-enriched spatial domains drive long-range interactions in the Z. tritici 3D genome, we also assess whether TE-mediated structural variation affects higher-order genome architecture¹. This work demonstrates that loss of 5mC is associated with a hypermutator state characterized by elevated rates of structural genome variation and enables a multi-scale inference of de novo genetic and epigenetic changes in a fungal pathogen.<br /><br />¹ Glavincheska, I. & Lorrain, C. Three-dimensional genome architecture connects chromatin structure and function in a major wheat pathogen. BMC Biology 23, 353 (2025).

## Copy That: Exceptional Retrocopy Abundance in Sloths and Xenarthrans


**Presenter:** Marcela Uliano Da Silva, *Wellcome Sanger Institute*

**Abstract:** Xenarthrans—sloths, anteaters, and armadillos—exhibit some of the most extreme morphological and physiological specialisations among mammals, including the exceptionally low metabolic rates that characterise sloths. Yet the genomic mechanisms underlying these distinctive traits remain largely unresolved. Genome plasticity driven by transposable elements can be a powerful source of evolutionary innovation, but its role in shaping long-term physiological specialisation is poorly understood. Using chromosome-level genomes including our new two-toed sloth (Choloepus didactylus) and the southern anteater (Tamandua tetradactyla) assemblies, we investigated how retrotransposon-mediated gene duplication has remodelled xenarthran genome architecture and adaptation. Comparative analyses across Xenarthra and 41 mammals reveal that xenarthran harbour the highest retrocopy burden reported to date, with pronounced lineage-specific dynamics. Anteaters and armadillos are characterised by predominantly ancient LINE-1 repertoires and older, species-specific retrocopy insertions. In contrast, sloth genomes retain abundant young LINE-1 elements and thousands of young retrocopies, together with a large shared cohort that originated from a burst of retroduplication in the lineage leading to their last common ancestor approximately 30 mya. Transcriptomes show that nearly half of retrocopies in C. didactylus are expressed, substantially exceeding levels observed in armadillos. Evolutionary analyses identify 38 sloth-only retrocopies with strong signatures of functional domestication, many derived from parental genes involved in mitochondrial function, energy metabolism, and cellular respiration. Together, these results implicate retrotransposition as a major driver of genome plasticity in Xenarthra and suggest that the extensive retention and functionalisation of retrocopies in sloths may have contributed to the molecular mechanisms underlying their uniquely slow and energy-constrained physiology.

## A theoretical perspective on the two-speed genome model

**Presenter:** Julien Dutheil, *Max Planck Institute For Evolutionary Biology*

**Abstract:** Whether self-replicating elements are associated with adaptive processes in their host genome has long been debated. In fungal pathogen genomics, the hypothesis is known as the "two-speed genome", as several species exhibit a genome compartmentalization in which virulence-related protein-encoding genes are associated with an increased density of transposable elements (TEs).<br />The excess of TEs around genes that are typically under strong selection is generally justified by a TE-induced elevated mutation rate, which may foster rapid adaptation. However, another argument is that TE-accumulations result from a locally reduced population size and are, therefore, a consequence rather than a driver of recurrent positive selection.<br />To disentangle these hypotheses, we study a two-locus model with a distance modifier and assess the impact of the selection regime on the modifier’s evolution by changing the distribution of fitness effects at the two loci. In the basic model, the modifier only affects the genetic distance. We then extend the model to allow the modifier to jointly influence the mutation rate at the flanking loci and the recombination rate between them.<br />Our results demonstrate that a simple model in which TEs have no impact on flanking loci is sufficient to explain their differential accumulation around loci undergoing recurrent positive selection. Adding the effect of TEs on the recombination rate, however, generates complex feedback loops in their spreading probability that depend on the population size and the average recombination probability. We posit that these complex interactions with life-history traits explain the broad diversity of observed genome architectures.<br />

## Construction of unbiased pangenomes to better characterize standing variation and structural rearrangements between closely related butterfly morphs

**Presenter:** Ava Mackay-Smith, *Duke University*

**Abstract:** Heliconius butterflies have a rich history of ecological and genetic exploration due to their wing pattern mimicry and dramatic geographic diversity. However, the contribution of structural and standing genetic variation on the maintenance and integrity of mimicking patterns remains murky for many members of this clade. This is further complicated by the remarkable ability of Heliconius butterflies to stably hybridize with other Heliconius that do not participate in the same mimicry ring, which would otherwise be expected to carry a major evolutionary cost to produce intermediate, non-mimicking, phenotypes. These outbred populations also have higher heterozygosity than many other sequenced species, meaning that the issue of missing or hidden genetic variation in Heliconius could be a major concern if our biological inferences are based on single-individual reference genomes or previously sequenced sister species. To address this bias, we assemble reference-free pangenomes from multiple individuals with long-read sequencing to better characterize the landscape of standing variation that occurs between two recently diverged, phenotypically distinct, yet persistently hybridizing subspecies of Heliconius erato. Further, we construct a third pangenome to characterize the landscape of admixture in a neighboring Heliconius that is hypothesized to be of hybrid origin between the two H. erato subspecies, but participates in the same mimicry ring as only one of the putative ancestral subspecies. This resource will allow us to explore a variety of questions, particularly the diversity and relative contribution of structural variation to the persistence of hybridization between divergent phenotypes of Heliconius.

## Alu-associated structural variation contributes to species-specific differences in genome organization across primate evolution 

**Presenter:** Shu Zhang, *Gladstone Institutes*

**Abstract:** Structural variants (SVs) account for a large fraction of genomic differences between humans and other primates, yet their contributions to species-specific genome organization and function remain incompletely understood. Many SVs arise from transposable elements, which can both insert themselves and facilitate recombination-mediated rearrangements. Among these, Alu retrotransposons are particularly abundant and have been implicated in shaping 3D genome architecture, including chromatin compartmentalization and enhancer–promoter interactions. Because Alus have evolved dynamically across the primate lineage, we hypothesized that Alu-associated SVs contribute to species-specific differences in genome folding and potentially drive divergence in regulatory programs between humans and other primates.<br /><br />To test this hypothesis, we analyzed over one million SVs identified from long-read primate genome assemblies using a machine learning framework. We retrained Akita, a deep learning model that predicts 3D genome folding from DNA sequence, on the T2T primate assemblies, leveraging natural sequence variation to improve species-specific predictions. We then modeled both lineage-specific and shared SVs in silico and predicted their effects on genome organization across homologous regions of the primate genomes. We find that SVs proximal to Alu elements are, on average, more disruptive to genome folding than SVs lacking nearby Alus, and we identify cases in which Alu-associated rearrangements are predicted to create or remove species-specific structural features. Together, these results implicate Alus as a driver of 3D genome evolution in primates and provide a computational framework for linking SVs to the evolution of regulatory architectures and phenotypic divergence.
