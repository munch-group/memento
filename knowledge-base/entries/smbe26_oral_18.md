## Macro-evolutionary perspectives on a large chromosomal rearrangement   underlying alternative life-histories in seaweed flies

**Presenter:** Dominique Hicks, *CNRS*

**Abstract:** Discrete alternative phenotypes, such as morphotypes and ecotypes, are often underpinned by complex genomic architectures termed supergenes. How supergenes evolve over long evolutionary timescales remains an open question. They often occur in highly plastic regions and involve large-scale structural rearrangements, including successive inversions or translocations. At the evolutionary level, they can involve parallel evolution, introgression, and trans-species balancing selection. This highlights the importance of studying supergene evolution in multi-species systems with large geographical distributions. <br />In the seaweed fly Coelopa frigida, a 25-Mb supergene formed by nested chromosomal inversions is associated with polymorphism in several life-history traits. Indeed, males harbouring one arrangement have a larger size, longer development time, and higher reproductive success than males with the other arrangement. Interestingly, similar male size polymorphisms were qualitatively reported in other Coelopidae species across the globe, suggesting that this chromosomal rearrangement may be shared across species boundaries. <br />To address this hypothesis, we quantified development time in five Coelopa species, and size in 13 Coelopidae species, using machine learning-assisted high-throughput phenotyping. This supported the presence of phenotypic polymorphism comparable to C. frigida in some coelopid species – but not all.  Then, for 17 species, we implemented a species vs. gene tree approach with a combination of short- and long-read data to investigate the supergene’s phylogenetic origin and evolution. Using genome assemblies, we also studied inter-species synteny to retrace the structural dynamics in this genomic region.  Our results provide valuable information on multi-species evolution of phenotypic polymorphism and structural rearrangements associated with an adaptive supergene.

## Mobile chromosomes as drivers of clonal pathogen evolution

**Presenter:** Cristina Barragan, *University Of Kiel*

**Abstract:** Crop disease pandemics are frequently driven by genetically uniform, asexually reproducing plant pathogen lineages. Despite limited genetic diversity and the absence of sexual recombination, these clonal populations repeatedly adapt to new hosts, environments, and control measures, raising fundamental questions about the origins of their evolutionary potential.<br /><br />Here, we demonstrate that horizontal chromosome transfer provides a mechanism for rapid genetic innovation in pandemic clonal lineages of the blast fungus Magnaporthe oryzae. Through comparative genomic analyses of field-collected isolates, we identify horizontally transferred accessory mini-chromosomes shared between pathogen populations infecting cultivated cereals and wild grasses. Furthermore, population-scale phylogenetic analyses reveal that horizontal mini-chromosome transfer is a recurrent evolutionary process actively shaping pathogen genomes across diverse agricultural and natural ecosystems.<br /><br />Initial functional characterization indicates that horizontally acquired chromosomes influence both virulence phenotypes and reproductive modes, directly linking chromosome transfer to key adaptive traits in pathogen biology. Together, these findings establish horizontal chromosome transfer as a driver of rapid adaptation in clonal pathogen lineages, with direct implications for predicting pathogen evolution and developing strategies for sustainable crop protection in an era of rapidly evolving agricultural threats.

## Transposable Elements in Ant Macroevolution

**Presenter:** Lukas Schrader, *University Of Münster*

**Abstract:** Transposable elements (TEs) are key contributors to genome evolution, yet their macroevolutionary role in the adaptive radiation of the ants remains poorly understood. We analyzed 163 ant genomes spanning 12 of 16 extant subfamilies to characterize TE impacts over the past 100 million years. We identify convergent TE bursts in ancestors of the most speciose ant clades, preceding rapid diversification following the Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event approximately 66 million years ago. We demonstrate that TEs associate with expansions of olfactory receptors and other key gene families, and reveal striking compartmentalization of ant genomes into faster-evolving TE-rich and more conserved TE-poor regions. Our findings demonstrate the broad-scale impact of TEs on the structural evolution of ant genomes in the wake of ecological disruptions.

## Genome reorganisation and expansion shape 3D genome architecture and define a distinct regulatory landscape in coleoid cephalopods


**Presenter:** Thea Rogers, *University Of Vienna*

**Abstract:** Our understanding of how genomic changes translate into organismal novelties is often confounded by complex and multi-layered genome architecture, and the deep evolutionary time-scales over which genomic innovations can accumulate. Coleoid cephalopods (squid, octopus and cuttlefish) offer a unique system in which to study the impact of macroevolutionary genomic processes on phenotypic complexity. A large-scale chromosomal rearrangement in the coleoid ancestor (~450 MYA) resulted in an extensively modified karyotype. Following this shared genomic shift, octopodiformes (octopuses) and decapodiformes (squid and cuttlefish) followed distinct evolutionary trajectories, shaped by lineage-specific chromosomal changes including fusions, translocations, and repetitive element expansions. These processes are hypothesised to underpin the evolution of coleoid innovations, including their large and elaborately structured nervous system, novel organs, and complex behaviours. Yet, how exactly these processes have impacted regulatory architecture remains poorly understood. To address this, we integrate Micro-C, RNA-seq, and ATAC-seq data across several coleoid species, developmental stages, and tissues, and examine syntenic genes, conserved non-coding elements, and topological interactions. We find that compartments tend to be conserved, yet exhibit divergent evolutionary paths depending on their ancestral context. Conversely, hundreds of putative regulatory chromatin loops are species-specific and often show stage- or tissue-specific activity with complex expression dynamics. Furthermore, CRISPR knock-out of an enhancer within a conserved loop highlights its function in neural development. We propose that differential levels of relaxed evolutionary constraints on the coleoid 3D genome enables species-specific patterns of chromosomal rearrangement and expansion, facilitating  the emergence of novel regulatory configurations underlying complex traits in the coleoids.

## The contribution of epigenetic regulation to antimicrobial resistance phenotypes in Escherichia coli from antibiotic-free dairy farms

**Presenter:** Adamandia Kapopoulou, *University Of Bern*

**Abstract:** Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) poses a major threat to human and animal health. Although commonly associated with antimicrobial use, resistant bacteria are increasingly detected even in the absence of direct antibiotic exposure. In a previous study, we investigated the genomic basis of AMR in Escherichia coli isolated from heifers raised on farms reporting no antimicrobial use. Despite the overall low prevalence of AMR, resistant and multidrug-resistant E. coli persisted across farms, seasons, and sequence types, a pattern not explained by resistance gene presence alone. To investigate additional mechanisms underlying this persistence, we tested whether epigenetic regulation contributes to resistance phenotypes. Using long-read sequencing, we analysed DNA methylation patterns in promoter regions. Differential methylation was enriched in promoters of multidrug efflux systems and envelope stress regulators, as well as resistance-associated genes. Notably, the mdtM promoter showed high GATC methylation exclusively in multidrug-resistant isolates carrying TEM-1 and floR, which were confined to a clonal lineage sampled repeatedly from the same animal across seasons. Our results show that identical genetic resistance determinants can be associated with distinct antimicrobial susceptibility phenotypes, consistent with variation in promoter methylation states. We identify associations between Dam/Dcm-mediated DNA methylation and resistance-associated gene expression, indicating that epigenetic modification contributes to phenotypic heterogeneity in AMR. These findings support the hypothesis that DNA methylation can function as a reversible, environmentally responsive regulatory layer, enabling E. coli to maintain resistance phenotypes in the absence of direct antibiotic selection and shaping AMR persistence in antibiotic-free environments.

## Programmed DNA elimination as a source of genomic plasticity in free-living nematodes

**Presenter:** Hend Abu-Elmakarem, *Wellcome Sanger Institute*

**Abstract:** In most organisms, all cells contain the same genetic material; however, some species break that rule through a process called Programmed DNA Elimination (PDE). In that process, parts of the DNA in the somatic cells are deleted during early embryogenesis, resulting in differences between the genomes of germline and somatic cells. <br />PDE was first discovered in parasitic nematodes in the 1800s. It has since been observed in a variety of eukaryotes: songbirds, fungus gnats, hagfish, among others. Despite being absent from the free-living model nematode Caenorhabditis elegans, PDE has been recently discovered in free-living nematodes from the genera Oscheius, Mesorhabditis and Caenorhabditis (suborder Rhabditina). In addition, the sites of DNA elimination were found to be highly precise and contained a short palindromic motif that likely recruits a nuclease to induce double-strand breaks, which are subsequently healed by de novo telomere addition.<br />These recent discoveries raise questions about how widespread the phenomenon is across Rhabditina and how the features of PDE differ across taxa. Here, we analyse the genomes of over 50 species and find PDE to be widespread across free-living members of Rhabditina and we discover that motifs differ in sequence and structure between clades. The amount of eliminated DNA also seems to vary and most of the eliminated genes are multi-copy, but some are highly conserved genes with important functions specific to germline development. PDE is a striking example of genomic plasticity within a single organism, and our work shows that it is much more widespread than previously thought. <br />

## Extensive sex chromosome rearrangements in cichlid fish

**Presenter:** Astrid Böhne, *Leibniz Institute For The Analysis Of Biodiversity Change, Museum Koenig Bonn*

**Abstract:** Discoveries of sex chromosomes across the tree of life have seen a tremendous uprise with the advance of sequencing data, yet our knowledge on mechanisms of sex chromosome emergence and consequences of sex chromosome turnover is limited. <br />The cichlid fish radiation of East African Lake Tanganyika offers an unmatched system to study sex chromosome evolution based on a solid understanding of species relationships comprising an entire radiation and comprehensive genome sequencing data. We previously detected an unprecedented high rate of sex chromosome turnover with non-random sex chromosome recruitment. We now show that intraspecies variability also exists in sex chromosome presence. Further, within a particular lineage of the radiation, one chromosome has evolved three times as sex chromosome lending support to the limited options theory of sex chromosome emergence. By reconstructing haplotype resolved genome assemblies, we find that novel sex chromosomes are often formed through chromosomal fusion. However, we also show that fusions and other chromosomal rearrangements are not limited to sex chromosomes suggesting that large-scale genomic changes are coupled not only to sex determination but ultimately might be linked to speciation in Lake Tanganyika cichlids. Sex chromosome phased assemblies reveal that differently sized inversions are the concurrent driver for recombination arrest. Sequence content in inversions differs in transposable element content compared to surrounding genome sequence potentially impacting gene regulation. By combining sequencing data with cytogenetics we show further support for massive chromosomal rearrangements, demonstrate the formation of neo-sex chromosomes and a rare case of heteromorphic sex chromosomes in cichlids.<br />

## When plasmids settle down: domestication and chromid-like evolution in Pantoea

**Presenter:** Devani Romero Picazo, *Institute Of General Microbiology, CAU Kiel*

**Abstract:** Plasmids play a central role in prokaryotic evolution by promoting genetic innovation and ecological adaptation. While typically perceived as mobile elements, some plasmids evolve stable inheritance within their host lineage and become integral components of their host genome. Here, we investigate the evolutionary trajectories of two large plasmids (LPP-1 and LPP-2) in the plant-associated genus Pantoea. Using comparative and phylogenomic analyses, we identify single events of plasmid acquisition followed by vertical inheritance, which may be explained due to the synchronisation of replication between plasmids and chromosome. Moreover, LPP-1 and LPP-2 encode core genes, whose chromosomal or plasmid localisation varies across species. Phylogenomic analyses reveal a deep evolutionary split between plasmid and chromosomal homologs, reflecting limited gene transfer between replicons. We infer that plasmid acquisition introduced genetic redundancy, followed by lineage-specific waves of gene loss. Together, stable vertical inheritance, synchronised replication and conserved core gene content are hallmarks of plasmid domestication in Pantoea LPP-1 and LPP-2, that may precede the emergence of chromids/secondary chromosomes. Our findings demonstrate that LPP domestication coincided with species divergence and likely contributed to ecological specialisation in Pantoea. Our study provides a model for how plasmids transition into stable, adaptive replicons during bacterial genome evolution.<br />

## Structural variants and their contribution to successful and unsuccessful colonizations

**Presenter:** Beatrice Sammarco, *University Of Ferrara*

**Abstract:** In the current context of rapid environmental change and increasing anthropogenic impacts, understanding the evolutionary mechanisms underlying the uneven distribution of biodiversity is crucial for predicting species redistribution. While some populations struggle to survive or adapt, others persist or expand beyond their native ranges. Among the genetic factors potentially influencing these contrasting outcomes, structural variations (SVs) may play a key role in shaping population fitness. Here, we investigated the effects of SVs on colonization outcomes in the marine gastropod Littorina saxatilis, a species that provides a natural experiment of repeated colonization events, including both historical and recent, successful and failed range expansions. Using high-coverage (50×) whole-genomes from 149 individuals sampled across the species distribution range, we identified SVs from paired-end short reads using a robust and widely adopted bioinformatic pipeline which includes a manual curation step. We then compared SV abundance and allele-frequency distribution in populations that underwent successful and unsuccessful colonization events to assess the potential role of chromosomal rearrangements in the colonization outcome. Finally, we used SVs to estimate genetic load quantifying deletions (DELs) in coding sequences, as potentially deleterious mutations that can affect gene expression and functioning. Overall, our results highlight the complex role of SVs during population range shift dynamics. These findings improve our understanding of the colonization process and the evolutionary dynamics underlying range shifts, shedding light on the genomic mechanisms distinguishing populations that persist from those that fail under rapid environmental change.

## Telomere-Specific Transposable Elements and Host Fitness in Drosophila

**Presenter:** Morteza Bajgiran, *University Of Regensburg*

**Abstract:** Unlike most eukaryotes, Drosophila lacks telomerase and canonical telomeric repeats. Nevertheless, they avoid chromosome shortening caused by the end-replication problem by relying on three transposable elements, HeT-A, TART, and TAHRE, which are located at chromosome ends where they exclusively transpose and form head-to-tail arrays (HTT arrays). These elements are believed to contribute to telomere maintenance, but the extent of their functional similarity to conventional telomeres remains unresolved.<br />While the regulation of these arrays and their recruitment of capping proteins suggest host domestication, the frequent evolutionary turnover of telomere-specific TEs across lineages suggests a more complex, potentially selfish dynamic. Consequently, the evolutionary forces shaping these HTT elements remain unclear.<br />Here, we investigate whether HTT elements are co-opted for host benefit or persist as selfish genetic elements by linking host fitness to telomere length across the life-course. Using nine lines from the Drosophila Genetic Reference Panel (DGRP), we leverage intra-line genetic uniformity to overcome the challenges of destructive sampling. We estimate telomere length via quantitative PCR (qPCR) at multiple time points and correlate these measurements with fitness traits across lines. By determining if telomere length positively impacts fitness or remains selectively neutral, this study clarifies the co-evolutionary interaction between HTT elements and the host genome, providing deeper insight into the evolution of non-canonical telomeres.<br /><br />Keywords: Drosophila, Telomere, Transposable elements, Host-parasite coevolution

## Modeling the Evolutionary Advantage of Plasmid Persistence Strategies

**Presenter:** Mario Santer, *Kiel University*

**Abstract:** The evolutionary success of plasmids and other extrachromosomal elements depends on their ability to persist across host generations despite imperfect inheritance. While individual plasmid persistence mechanisms—such as active partitioning or toxin–antitoxin (TA) systems—are well described, their combined evolutionary consequences remain poorly understood. Here, we use a mathematical modeling framework, grounded in empirically measured plasmid loss dynamics, to investigate how different persistence strategies shape plasmid fitness over evolutionary timescales. Our model integrates intracellular competition between plasmid genotypes with population-level inheritance dynamics, allowing us to quantify the long-term selective advantages of plasmid persistence mechanisms. We find that combining active partitioning with post-segregational killing via TA systems consistently maximizes plasmid fitness, outperforming either strategy alone. These results follow from the synergistic reduction in segregational loss and the elimination of plasmid-free cells, which together increase long-term persistence in host lineages. Experimental competition assays between engineered plasmid variants closely match model predictions, validating the inferred evolutionary trajectories. Extending these insights to natural systems, comparative genomic analyses indicate that partitioning and TA systems are core features of large plasmids in enterobacteria, as predicted by the model. We further identify and experimentally validate a previously unrecognized type I TA system in IncX3 plasmids. By integrating mechanistic segregation dynamics with evolutionary competition at the population level, our work reveals general principles shaping the architecture of extrachromosomal elements and underscores the evolutionary advantage of combined persistence strategies. This synthesis offers a quantitative framework for understanding plasmid evolution within the broader landscape of genome plasticity and mobile genetic elements.

## Genome-Wide Association Analysis reveals insights into Escherichia coli adaptation to specific clinical niches

**Presenter:** Riccardo Nodari, *University of Milan*

**Abstract:** Escherichia coli is a genetically and ecologically diverse species whose evolutionary success is driven by extensive genome plasticity and frequent horizontal gene transfer. This diversity enables rapid adaptation to contrasting niches, ranging from gut commensalism to invasive extraintestinal disease. This shift in pathogenic potential is largely driven by the accessory genome, which encodes virulence and resistance factors that promote survival in diverse environments.<br />Here, we applied a phylogenetically stratified genome-wide association study (GWAS) based on gene presence-absence across a large and ecologically heterogeneous collection of E. coli genomes isolated from adult and neonatal bloodstream, urinary tract infections, hospital settings, and non-clinical environments. By explicitly controlling for population structure, we identified lineage-independent genetic associations, allowing us to infer signatures of niche-specific selection acting on the accessory genome.<br />Bloodstream and urinary isolates were significantly enriched in genes involved in metal acquisition, adhesion, biofilm formation, and stress responses, highlighting shared adaptations for survival in hostile host environments. Neonatal bloodstream isolates showed a strong association with the yersiniabactin iron-scavenging system. In addition, antibiotic resistance genes and mobile genetic elements were significantly overrepresented in hospital and urine derived isolates, underscoring the role of clinical settings as reservoirs for antimicrobial resistance.<br />These findings show that E. coli adapts to distinct ecological and clinical niches through the acquisition and maintenance of functionally relevant genes independent of phylogenetic background. Source-specific genetic traits identified here provide valuable insights into E. coli pathogenesis, and offer potential biomarkers for diagnostics, surveillance, and targeted intervention strategies.

## Transposable element islands at the interface of 3D genome architecture and karyotype evolution in ants

**Presenter:** Janina Rinke, *University Of Muenster*

**Abstract:** Karyotype diversification is a common phenomenon in evolution, yet how the underlying chromosomal rearrangements emerge often remains unclear. Here, we generated chromosome-level assemblies for ten species of Cardiocondyla ants and the outgroup Nesomyrmex wilda to investigate the genomic basis of karyotype diversification. Cardiocondyla species possess the smallest ant genomes reported yet exhibit extraordinary chromosome number variation (1n=8-32). Transposable Elements (TEs) in Cardiocondyla genomes are highly compartmentalized into dense TE Islands. These islands coincide with pericentromeric regions, are hotspots of structural variation, and frequently overlap chromosomal breakpoints, implicating them as anchors of Robertsonian fusions and rapid centromere turnover. 3D genome analyses revealed spatial clustering of centromeres embedded in TE Islands, providing a structural context facilitating repeat-mediated chromosomal rearrangements. Our results identify TE Islands as focal regions that both promote and record structural genome evolution, revealing a mechanistic link between TE compartmentalization, 3D genome organization, rapid karyotype diversification and genome compaction.

## Exploring architectural diversity in fungal 3D genomes to uncover new perspectives on eukaryotic chromatin organization

**Presenter:** Cecile Lorrain, *ETH Zurich*

**Abstract:** Fungi represent some of the most phylogenetically and ecologically diverse eukaryotes, yet remain underexplored models for studying chromatin architecture and its evolutionary dynamics. While recent advances in genome conformation technologies have uncovered fundamental principles of three-dimensional genome organization across life, the mechanisms governing chromatin domain formation in fungi remain largely elusive. In contrast to bilaterians, where chromatin domains are shaped by architectural proteins, or plants, where they emerge primarily from chromatin state and compartmentalization, it remains unknown whether fungi follow either of these models or represent a distinct mode of genome organization altogether. To address this gap, we performed a comparative meta-analysis of high-resolution Hi-C datasets from a diversity of fungal species. We aim to identify conserved and lineage-specific patterns of chromatin interaction domains, with a focus on boundary features such as transcriptional activity, repetitive elements, and candidate binding motifs. By integrating chromatin interaction data with comparative genomics and motif discovery approaches, we explore whether domain boundaries in fungi reflect passive structural compartmentalization or active maintenance by architectural factors. This work aims to provide a foundation to identify novel chromatin domains-associated elements, informs on how genome architecture evolves across eukaryotes, and opens new avenues to understand the functional consequences of three-dimensional genome organization in non-model systems.

## Long-read sequencing reveals the genomic architecture of alternative reproductive tactics in swordtail fishes

**Presenter:** Gabriel Preising, *Stanford University*

**Abstract:** Alternative reproductive tactics exist across the tree of life. In such systems, individuals within the sex under stronger sexual selection can evolve diverse reproductive strategies, including coercive mating or female mimicry. These polymorphic strategies are driven by environmental and genetic variables to varying degrees. When reproductive tactics are strongly genetically linked, loci underlying this variation are predicted to accumulate in regions with reduced recombination rates, creating discrete phenotypic distributions for alternative morphs. Here, we characterize the genomic architecture of alternative reproductive tactics within swordtail fishes (Xiphophorus). Males of different swordtail species can be categorized as having large courting males, small coercive males, or being polymorphic for both male reproductive morphs. Using long-read sequencing, we generated genome assemblies for species exhibiting each of these scenarios. Within one polymorphic species, X. multilineatus, we performed a genome-wide association study for reproductive morph and identified a large, structurally variable region on the Y-chromosome that correlates with reproductive tactic. This region contains expansions of multiple genes, including the melanocortin-4 receptor (mc4r) which regulates feeding behavior in vertebrates. We explored the evolutionary history of this region and showed that across species, small coercive males exhibit marked reductions in mc4r copy number, while large courting males show high variance in mc4r copy number. The non-recombining region of the Y-chromosome containing these structural variants likely acted as a refuge for tactic-specific loci to accumulate, explaining their persistence over time. These results highlight structural variation of the Y-chromosome as a mechanism for driving the evolution of reproductive strategies.
