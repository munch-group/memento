## Leveraging public data for comparative evolutionary genomics of arthropod biodiversity

**Presenter:** Robert Waterhouse, SIB Swiss Institute of Bioinformatics

**Abstract:**

The countless adaptations observed across insects and other arthropods mean that they are ideal for investigating how conservation or divergence of functional genomic elements give rise to the splendour of animal biology. The rapid accumulation of high-quality public genomic resources for species from across the arthropod phylogeny supports new avenues of research investigating biological innovations. Comparative evolutionary and functional genomics approaches exploiting these data are beginning to trace how changes in gene repertoires – gene family expansions and contractions as well as gene gains, losses, and emergences – are associated with variable traits such as diet and digestion or immunity and moulting. Leveraging such large-scale public datasets to quantify genetic and genomic changes requires methodological advances in genomics analysis and visualisation tools. In parallel, aiming to understand how such changes relate to phenotypic differences requires new literature mining approaches to liberate knowledge from publications at scale.

## Rare variation in malaria parasites biases population-genetic inference

**Presenter:** Amy Goldberg, UCLA

**Abstract:**

Understanding how pathogens evolve is fundamental to disease control and is a basic question in evolutionary biology, yet pathogens with complex life cycles violate assumptions of classic evolutionary models. Genetic analyses of the malaria parasite \textit{Plasmodium falciparum} have shown multiple surprising patterns. For example, empirical analyses produce effective population size estimates that vary by orders of magnitude depending on the method and an excess of genes with elevated nonsynonymous variation (measured as $\pi_N/\pi_S$). Here, reanalyzing genomic data from 18 worldwide populations from the MalariaGEN repository and PlasmoDB, I show that these observations directly follow from distributions of genetic variation enriched for rare variants. Multiple potential mechanisms may increase the proportion of rare variants, including host expansions, lifecycle dynamics, population structure, or selection.  The observed genealogies are more consistent with a multiple-merger coalescent than a Kingman coalescent, causing common summary statistics to be biased in predictable directions. In particular, the abundance of rare variants interacts with the mathematical properties of ratio statistics to systematically inflate gene-level $\pi_N/\pi_S$ estimates even in the absence of selection. Notably, filtering this rare variation reveals previously masked candidates for selection, including well-characterized antigens such as merozoite surface proteins. This framework provides a  foundation for interpreting genomic data in pathogens with high variance in reproductive success.

## Integration and comparison of public single-cell RNA-Seq data for animals in the Bgee database and the scFAIR consortium

**Presenter:** Frederic Bastian, SIB Swiss Institute of Bioinformatics - University Of Lausanne

**Speaker Bio:** Co-lead for the gene expression database Bgee (https://www.bgee.org/), and the scFAIR consortium (https://www.sc-fair.org/).

**Abstract:**

Single-cell transcriptomics has become a widely used technology to study heterogeneous and dynamic biological systems. The complexity of these datasets makes them particularly challenging to find, access, and reuse; and to be integrated and compared over multiple experiments. In primary repositories such as the Sequence Read Archive (SRA), cell type information is mostly unstructured, and critical information for allowing reanalyses is often missing.<br />The Bgee database (https://www.bgee.org/) aims at standardizing scRNA-seq dataset annotations across experiments and species for improving data reproducibility and integration. It also aims at making the resulting expression data comparable between experiments and species. Moreover, Bgee is one the groups leading the scFAIR consortium (https://www.sc-fair.org/), to coordinate curation work and agree on best practices among major single-cell international resources (e.g., CELLxGENE, EBI Single Cell Expression Atlas). As a result, Bgee is recognized as a Global Core Biodata Resource and as an ELIXIR Core Data Resource.<br /><br />In this presentation, we will describe the curation guidelines and best practices applied at Bgee and scFAIR in order to reuse public single-cell datasets. We will present the original statistical methods applied in Bgee to make these data comparable across experiments and species, and how to leverage ontology structures to reach common cell type information across species for gene expression comparisons. This work allows researchers to access highly structured FAIR data for reuse in their own analyses, and to provide simplified and easy-to-use gene expression information in cell types, comparable between species.<br />

## Completing the Tree of Life with High-throughput Phylogenetics

**Presenter:** John Allard, Temple University

**Abstract:**

Understanding evolutionary relationships among species is central to evolutionary biology and functional genomics. Despite abundant sequence data, species representation in existing molecular tree aggregation databases remains limited because sequence curation for molecular phylogenetics is labor-intensive. Some efforts to achieve species completeness in large phylogenies have used data-free imputation approaches, which cannot recover true topological relationships or divergence times and may mislead macroevolutionary analyses. We introduce an end-to-end automated platform for high-throughput phylogenetics and divergence time estimation. Using this platform, we performed de novo tree inference to produce three new ultra-large, time-calibrated phylogenies of Vertebrates (>50,000 species), the insect order Diptera (>18,500 species), and the plant clade Monocots (>26,000 species). These yield increases of over 50% in species representation for each group compared with the entire aggregated time-calibrated molecular phylogenetics literature in the Timetree of Life 5 database. We validate the accuracy of our trees by comparing them with gold-standard phylogenomics studies. We also show that greater species completeness improves macroevolutionary metrics compared with preexisting trees and enhances genotype-phenotype predictive modeling of the convergent acquisition of C4 photosynthesis in monocots. Our freely available cross-platform software not only automatically generates a phylogeny for a user-specified target taxonomic group but also provides marker alignments that will be useful to researchers who wish to perform manual curation. Generation of de novo phylogenies across the tree of life is now feasible and can be continuously updated to incorporate the latest data.

## Expanding the Mammalverse of Genomes

**Presenter:** Jonathan Hughes, Arizona State University

**Abstract:**

The Mammalverse is comprised of the ecologies and genomes of the approximately 6,800 extant and recently extinct mammal species. Improvements in sequencing technologies and assembly techniques have led to a dramatic rise in the availability and quality of mammal genomes in recent years. However, the gaps in genomic sampling of taxa in different geographies and ecologies are poorly characterized. Aligning NCBI data to the Mammal Diversity Database taxonomy, we find that only ~10% of mammals have had their genomes sequenced to date, many of which are low quality (two-thirds with scaffold N50 < 1 Mb). We also find biases regarding which mammals have genome data available, including sparse sampling of rodents, shrews, and bats globally. We review the current landscape of the Mammalverse of Genomes, including how disparity in genome availability and quality constrains our ability to study patterns of trait evolution, genome organisation, and repetitive sequence diversification. We also highlight the need for associating genome assemblies with preserved voucher specimens to future-proof these data to ongoing taxonomic changes. Each new assembly provides crucial insights into the evolution and diversity of mammals and their genomes across the last 180 million years, so planning with a view of the Mammalverse is well warranted.

## Mining Public Metagenomic Archives Reveals Deep-Time Evolution and Hidden Diversity of the Human Oral Microbiome

**Presenter:** Mohamed Sarhan

**Abstract:**

The human oral microbiome reflects long-term interactions between host biology, diet, hygiene, and environment, yet its deep evolutionary history remains incompletely understood. In our study we aimed at reconstructing temporal changes in oral microbial communities from Neanderthals to modern humans using ancient dental calculus metagenomic datasets publicly available in different public repositories. A total of 840 ancient DNA datasets were analyzed across multiple host species, geographic regions, and historical periods. Community-level analyses revealed clear temporal structuring of the human oral microbiome. Ancient samples exhibit substantially higher levels of uncharacterized diversity, with Neanderthals showing a median of ~70.5% unknown taxa, compared to ~15% in modern humans. Compositional analyses identify significant shifts across historical periods (PERMANOVA R² = 0.2225, p = 0.001), displaying highly significant temporal variation. Supervised machine-learning models demonstrated that microbial composition contains a measurable temporal signal, achieving 80% accuracy in classifying samples by historical period. Genome-resolved metagenomics generated 6,638 genome bins, dereplicated into 850 non-redundant metagenome-assembled genomes (MAGs). Only 40% of which could be assigned at the species level, leaving nearly 60% completely unclassified. Notably, approximately one third of these unclassified MAGs belonged to the Candidate Phyla Radiation (CPR), with a strong enrichment of Patescibacteriota, particularly Saccharibacteria. The prominence of these ultra-small and poorly characterized lineages underscores ancient dental calculus as a reservoir of previously unexplored oral microbial diversity. Overall, this work demonstrates the importance of mining the public datasets and highlights the value of open research in answering seminal questions related to human oral microbiome evolution over millennia.

## FAIRness of polymorphism data matters for plant demographic inference

**Presenter:** Maxence Brault, CNRS, ECOBIO (Ecosystems, Biodiversity, Evolution), Rennes, France

**Abstract:**

Many long-standing questions in population genomics  (e.g. Lewontin’s paradox, the prominence of adaptive evolution at the molecular level) can now be addressed through comparative analyses and by leveraging the vast amount of genomic data being generated. In the context of questioning the utility of producing such a large amount of genomic data, whether for ecological or economic reasons, we argue that data publication should be standardized to ensure long-term reusability. Based on a literature review and key examples, we emphasize that despite the growing volume of available data, the lack of methodological documentation and the absence of metadata make most published polymorphism datasets uncomparable, preventing the calculation of meaningful statistics. We stress that the standard Variant Calling Format (VCF) alone is insufficient to compute absolute statistics such as pairwise nucleotide diversity (π) or Watterson’s θ. From a comparative analysis on plant demographies, we underscore the need for a systematic reporting of standardized statistics, coupled with transparent documentation of data processing steps, to ensure the reproducibility and comparability but also to isolate systematic bias from real biological signal. Finally, we propose guidelines and best practices to provide sufficient information to allow the proper calculation of standard statistics while accounting for sources of bias and misestimation frequently observed in the literature.

## Developing open tools and datasets to uncover the role of whole genome duplications in early vertebrate evolution

**Presenter:** Lukasz Niezabitowski, Trinity College Dublin

**Abstract:**

Whole genome duplications (WGDs) profoundly shaped early vertebrate evolution, most famously the 2 rounds (2R) that occurred at the base of the lineage. We now know that the first event (dubbed 1R) is shared by all vertebrate genomes and was shortly followed by two independent events in jawed and jawless vertebrates. The set of paralogs retained from these events—'ohnologs'—have had lasting impacts on genome structure and function, including disease-gene enrichment. Despite this, we still lack a gold standard ohnologs database. Here, we have harnessed ancestral genome reconstructions, together with phylogenetic and synteny information, to produce a robust ohnolog dataset available at ohnologs.com. For the first time, we have been able to resolve the 1R versus 2R origins of a large number of cases, as well as including previously hard-to-detect ohnologs. We find that 1R-retained ohnologs are involved in cellular transport, while those retained after both 1R and 2R are biased towards signalling functions, especially neuronal signalling. These findings suggest that after an initial adaptation to the cellular burden of polyploidy, WGD expanded the opportunities for intercellular communication. Furthermore, using our dataset, we uncover that rediploidisation, or return to diploidy, was both asynchronous and temporally protracted after 1R, suggesting that some loci began to diverge in sequence and function millions of years after duplication. Rather than a single 'big bang', 1R may have provided vertebrate genomes with a steady stream of innovation. In the spirit of open science, our tools and datasets are publicly available, documented, and accessible for future work.

## RepeatOBserver: Tandem repeat visualisation and putative centromere detection in 2000 genomes from across the tree of life

**Presenter:** Cassandra Elphinstone, University of Oslo

**Abstract:**

Tandem repeats play an important role in centromere structure, subtelomeric regions, DNA methylation, recombination and the regulation of gene activity. Although chromosome-scale genome assemblies are increasingly available for a wide range of non-model species, there is still little information on species specific repetitive elements and rarely experimentally verified centromere locations. The ability to predict putative centromeres and detect imperfect repeats from just the genome sequence alone can help us better annotate these genomes.<br /><br />RepeatOBserver is a new bioinformatics tool for visualizing chromosome-scale repeat patterns and identifying putative centromere locations and sizes in chromosome-scale genome assemblies (https://github.com/celphin/RepeatOBserverV1). RepeatOBserver detects a broad spectrum of repeats ranging from 3 to 20,000 bp in length, without requiring prior knowledge of repeat sequences or parameter optimization. In addition to centromere prediction, the tool enables visualization of potential inversion boundaries, retrotransposon clusters, and gene copy number variation. <br /><br />By analyzing 107 chromosomes with known centromere positions, we observe that centromeres consistently occur in regions exhibiting the lowest diversity of repeat types. Using this information, we apply a genomic Shannon diversity index to predict centromere locations in an additional 2,000 chromosome-scale genome assemblies. We will discuss patterns observed in putative centromeres across 2,000 genomes spanning the tree of life, as well as other genomic features that can be revealed using RepeatOBserver.<br />

## What do bats and belugas have in common? Comparative genomics reveals convergent anti-deafness adaptations in echolocating Cetacea and Chiroptera

**Presenter:** Liliana M Dávalos, Consortium for Inter-Disciplinary Environmental Research and Institute for Advanced Computational Science, Stony Brook U

**Abstract:**

Echolocation has evolved independently in bats (Chiroptera: Yangochiroptera + Rhinolophoidea) and toothed whales (Odontoceti). While echolocation is an indispensable adaptation for navigation and foraging in these taxa, constant exposure to intense noise paired with cellular senescence are known to cause the death of cochlear sensory hair cells (HCs) and subsequent hearing loss in mammals. Selection to maintain functional hearing by delaying cellular senescence into old age in echolocating mammals is therefore likely to be strong, but evidence for convergence in senescence-associated molecular pathways among these taxa remains limited. We conducted a comparative genomics study across 101 highly contiguous mammal genomes to test for convergence in positively selected genes and functional pathways among echolocating bats and toothed whales. Using an orthologous gene set several times larger than previously studied, we found positive selection in genes not previously investigated in these clades whose functions include spatial reasoning and cognition, cochlear cell survival and repair, and tumor suppression. We also detected positive selection in all three echolocating lineages for genes essential for the structural integrity of cochlear HC stereocilia bundles, whose breakage directly leads to hearing loss in mammals. Finally, we found evidence of convergent selection in senescence-associated functional pathways, including immune and DNA damage pattern recognition, mitochondrial homeostasis, and cellular response to hypoxia and cytotoxic reactive oxygen species. Our study aligns with previous findings supporting parallel evolution in hearing-associated genes among distantly related echolocating mammals and expands these findings to include convergence in other cellular processes relevant to maintaining crucial sensory phenotypes. <br /><br />

## Understanding the diversity of adhesive proteins in sea stars (Echinodermata, Asteroidea)

**Presenter:** Leandro Smacchia, University of Mons / University of Liège

**Abstract:**

Marine invertebrates produce strong adhesive secretions to attach to the sea floor, particularly in intertidal environments characterized by strong current forces. Beyond substrate attachment, marine adhesive secretions may also serve for food capture, locomotion, building or protection purposes. Because biological adhesives consist mainly of proteins, ‘omics technologies have been used extensively to characterize adhesive protein (AP) repertoires from diverse marine species. The wealth of public genomic data has also been increasing over the years. In sea stars, abundant data is now available, allowing to investigate AP diversity and evolution.<br />Our study covers and integrates three aspects of adhesion evolution in sea stars: the general asteroid phylogeny, the AP repertoire diversity, and the morphological and ecological traits relevant to adhesion. First, 26 publicly available genomes were gathered and annotated, using EviAnn. An evolutionary framework of the asteroid clade has been established using phylogenomic approaches (orthology inference, single-gene trees and supertree building and, alternatively, supermatrices), indicating basal Velatida, followed by a dichotomy between Forcipulatacea and other asteroid orders. Hierarchical orthologous groups were then mined to retrieve homologs to the adhesive proteins from two reference species, Asterias rubens and Asterina gibbosa. Corroborated by extensive HMMER searches, our analyses show that some APs are species-specific or lineage-specific, whereas others are ubiquitous among sea stars. In parallel, morphological and ecological metadata, such as habitat, role of adhesion, podia morphotype and adhesive cell types were mapped onto the evolutionary framework. Finally, AP phylogenies were reconciled with the species tree and ancestral gene repertoires were inferred.

## Characterizing HIF-pathway alteration across two early diverging metazoan lineages (Ctenophora, Porifora). 

**Presenter:** Zach Opoka, University Of Kansas

**Abstract:**

Ctenophores and sponges are some of the earliest diverging metazoans, evolving in very low oxygen conditions 800 million years ago. Although there was little oxygen present, they still seemed to have parts of the pathway associated with oxygen homeostasis - the Hypoxia Inducible Factor (HIF). This pathway is considered highly conserved across all of metazoa, with only a few recent exceptions. Previous work has shown that the main HIF transcription factor (HIFa) is present in sponges and ctenophores; yet it is the canonical domain associated with oxygen sensing (oxygen degradation domain; ODD). Additionally, they are missing the primary repression genes in the HIF pathway (EGLN). This leaves the question of whether HIFa evolved de novo or if it was co-opted from existing machinery. Also, it is unknown if these differences in the basal metazoan pathway were present in prior lineages (choanoflagellates). Using publicly available genomes and transcriptomes, I analyzed a wider breadth of basal metazoans, as well as the closest outgroup, for the presence of various elements of the HIF-pathway: 19 species of ctenophores, 15 species of sponges and 19 choanoflagellate species. Ultimately, we confirm the presence of a “HIFa”-like in sponges and ctenophores with no similar member in choanoflagellates. Further investigation is needed to determine if “HIFa” in ctenophores and sponges have oxygen related functions. Investigating HIFa in ctenophores and sponges will elucidate what the ancestral function of this gene is and ultimately help us understand how animals adapted to the rise in oxygen concentrations during early animal evolution.

## Genomic fossils reveal hidden evolutionary processes underlying phylogenetic conflicts in avian radiations

**Presenter:** Yulong Xie, Zhejiang University

**Abstract:**

Phylogenetic conflicts in rapid radiations are often dismissed as stochastic noise from incomplete lineage sorting (ILS), yet emerging evidence suggests that some discordance patterns may preserve signals of specific evolutionary events obscured by recombination across most of the genome. Here we conduct the first genome-wide survey of long syntenic phylogenetic conflict (LSPC) across birds, analyzing 63,430 intergenic locus trees from published 363 bird genomes to systematically identify genomic regions where alternative topologies persist over megabase scales. We develop a quantitative framework partitioning phylogenetic discordance into ILS-driven and non-ILS-driven components, revealing fundamental differences between the K-Pg boundary Neoaves radiation (dominated by neutral ILS) and the Passeriformes radiation (shaped by selection). We identify 15 nodes with significant LSPC and classify them into four mechanistic types: localized signals from ancestral structural rearrangements, scattered signals from selection-mediated retention of introgressed haplotypes, genome-wide patterns from ancient gene flow, and chaotic signals from GC-biased gene conversion. We demonstrate that ancient introgression generated extended syntenic blocks selectively retained due to functional gene clusters, for example HOXD families in Columbiformes and ubiquitin ligase complex genes in ancestral Telluraves. Our results establish that LSPC regions function as "genomic fossils" recording evolutionary events otherwise erased by recombination, providing a framework for revealing hidden processes underlying phylogenetic controversies in rapid radiations across the tree of life.
