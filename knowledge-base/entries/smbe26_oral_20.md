## Paralog interference contributes to the preservation of genetic redundancy

**Presenter:** Christian Landry, Université Laval

**Abstract:**

Models of gene duplication often assume that loss-of-function mutations neutrally promote the return to the ancestral singleton state. They thus ignore the potential functional interference between duplicated proteins stemming from their physical interactions. Here, we show that for heteromerizing paralogs, such interference potentiates negative selection on loss-of-function mutations. This effect maintains genetic redundancy over longer timescales depending on the rate and severity of loss-of-function mutations. We experimentally estimate that around 6% of deleterious amino acid substitutions in a representative tetrameric protein interfere with a second copy. Interfering substitutions typically disrupt either catalysis or the final step of protein complex assembly, with varying degrees of severity. Our work shows that paralog interference renders the negative effects of loss-of-function mutations visible to purifying selection, contributing to the preservation of genetic redundancy.<br /><br />

## The Gene Regulatory Evolution of the Human Skeleton

**Presenter:** Nadav Mishol, Weizmann Institute of Science

**Abstract:**

Skeletal modifications were central to deep human evolution, facilitating upright locomotion and large brains, and influencing childbirth and our distinctive limbs. Despite their importance, the regulatory networks underlying human skeletal divergence remain poorly characterized. Here, we systematically map human-specific skeletal gene regulation. Using massively parallel reporter assays (MPRAs) in chondrocytes, we synthesized and assayed 561,410 fixed human-derived single-nucleotide variants in promoters and enhancers, identifying 15,077 loci with human-specific regulatory activity. We then generated human-ape hybrid cells and differentiated them into osteochondral progenitors. The combination of these complementary approaches enabled a multi-level investigation of the mechanisms underlying cis-regulation, from the single regulatory element through genes and pathways to phenotypes. Integrating the hybrid system with MPRA measurements yielded a genome-wide atlas of human-specific cis-regulatory expression changes, and the sequence changes that drove them. This atlas reveals extensive divergence affecting molecular networks such as Hedgehog signaling and extracellular matrix biosynthetic pathways, with pronounced downregulation of polysaccharide (glycosaminoglycan) biosynthesis likely shaped by lineage-specific selection. We further validate this reduction in polysaccharide content in human compared with chimpanzee cartilage, and propose that this shift might underlie the particularly elevated susceptibility of humans to degenerative joint diseases. Together, our results reveal coordinated regulatory remodeling of the skeletal extracellular matrix during deep human evolution and provide a comprehensive resource for studying how human skeletal biology was shaped by evolution.

## Mapping the Local Biochemical Landscapes of TEM-1 β-Lactamase

**Presenter:** Jia Zheng, Westlake University

**Abstract:**

Understanding and predicting the effects of mutations on enzyme activity remains challenging due to pleiotropic impacts on multiple biochemical properties. Here, we developed a high-throughput approach that links bacterial growth to the catalytic activity of TEM-1 β-lactamase against ampicillin. By culturing E. coli expressing approximately 120,000 TEM-1 variants across 30 ampicillin concentrations and using deep sequencing to measure growth, we inferred in vivo catalytic velocities under each condition. Fitting these velocities to the Michaelis-Menten equation allowed estimation of Vmax and Km for each variant, enabling simultaneous quantification of fitness and kinetic parameters. Our results reveal widespread pleiotropy, with mutations exerting differential effects on catalytic constant, substrate affinity, and folding stability. The spatial distributions of mutations influencing these properties exhibit partial overlap,and long-range mutational hotspots distant from the active site exert significant effects on kinetic parameters. These findings provide a comprehensive mechanistic map of the TEM-1 fitness landscape, elucidating the complex sequence-structure-function relationships that underlie enzyme evolution and adaptation.

## Cumulative epistatic changes in fitness effects drove contingency and entrenchment in the billion-year evolution of Hsp90

**Presenter:** Ricardo Muñiz-Trejo, University of Chicago

**Abstract:**

Epistatic interactions can change the accessibility of a mutation within a protein by modifying its fitness effects as substitutions occur at other sites. However, no studies have directly evaluated historical changes in fitness effects with sufficient resolution to elucidate their temporal dynamics and genetic mechanisms. Here we experimentally track fluctuations in the fitness effect of every substitution that occurred along a billion-year-old evolutionary trajectory of the essential chaperone Hsp90 from a deep eukaryotic ancestor to modern Saccharomyces cerevisiae. We prepared mutant libraries containing every historical ancestral and derived amino acid states and used a high-precision bulk-competition assay to measure their selection coefficients when introduced into all 27 reconstructed ancestral backgrounds along this trajectory. Most changes in fitness effects were small between consecutive ancestors, but their cumulative change across multiple intervals was sufficient to change accessibility of many historical states at various points along the trajectory. Many substitutions were contingent on the long-term accumulation of multiple permissive substitutions; once these contingently permitted substitutions occurred, they immediately entrenched all their permissive modifiers, restricting their access back to the ancestral residue. Moreover, some permissive substitutions were contingent on prior permissive changes of their own, and some nonspecifically changed the accessibility of many target substitutions in a correlated fashion. Our findings show that the historical changes that occurred during Hsp90 evolution depended not just on their most temporally proximal epistatic modifiers but on a long chain of mutually contingent events that began in the long-distant past.

## Bacterial dynamics of evolution tracked by barcodes over 20,000 generations validate a century of theoretical models of adaptation

**Presenter:** Olivier Tenaillon, Institut Cochin, Inserm, Université Paris Cité

**Abstract:**

In asexual bacterial populations, long-term adaptation relies on the accumulation of multiple beneficial mutations. Understanding the dynamics of this process has been a central question in population genetics for over a century. Researchers have developed a range of models, each proposing different adaptive regimes. These regimes vary according to the rate at which beneficial mutations arise and their selective effects.<br />Using genetic barcodes, we analysed the adaptive dynamics in a long-term evolution experiment spanning more than 20,000 generations of laboratory adaptation. Our findings show that the observed dynamics align with the regimes predicted by a century of theoretical models. Furthermore, by combining microfluidics with deep mutational scans of key adaptive genes, we identify potential mechanisms driving transitions between these regimes.<br />

## Parsing nearly neutral theory at the protein level through experimental variation of effective population size and high-throughput biophysical measurements

**Presenter:** Solomon McShea, University Of California, San Francisco

**Abstract:**

The nearly neutral theory of molecular evolution predicts that the extent to which natural selection can overcome the random action of drift depends on a population’s effective size (Ne). Comparative work has shown that several phenotypes – including genome size and structure, codon usage, and amino acid composition – vary with Ne, suggesting that differences in mutation-selection-drift balance contribute to phenotypic differences between species. To link such macroevolutionary patterns to the microevolutionary dynamics from which they arise, we employ experimental evolution and fitness assays, with rhinovirus and its protease 3CPro as a model system. We measure the effect of Ne on evolutionary trajectories and outcomes by evolving rhinoviral populations at different Ne across 5 orders of magnitude. We then measure fitness for all 3CPro variants from experimental populations and for a library of single, double, and triple mutants, enabling analysis of the effect of Ne on how populations traverse the local fitness landscape, focusing on dynamics like selective sweeps, clonal interference, and epistasis. Thermodynamic stability (ΔGfold) measurements of these 3CPro variants will allow complementary analysis of how Ne affects the evolution of stability, a universal property of proteins known to be under purifying selection and thus likely sensitive to differences in Ne. These ongoing experiments are supported by our recent results from population genetic simulations. By experimentally varying Ne, the property of populations theorized to tune the balance between mutation, drift, and natural selection, this work aims to illuminate population genetic dynamics and molecular mechanisms underlying the nearly neutral theory.

## Tracking the evolution of protein fusion entrenchment via genomic directed evolution 

**Presenter:** Yiqiao Sun, University of Zurich

**Abstract:**

The evolution of multi-domain proteins is often characterized by the fusion of previously autonomously folding domains. While the prevalence of multi-domain proteins is often attributed to adaptive benefits, this architecture can become irreversible simply because separating the domains becomes deleterious—a process termed entrenchment. We hypothesize that protein fusions can become "locked" in a fused state during co-evolution, where mutations accumulate that are harmless in the fused context but disrupt function if the domains are separated. <br /><br />To capture the emergence of dependence on the fused state, we deployed a genomic directed evolution method to evolve the histidine biosynthesis enzymes HisI and HisE. Standard directed evolution relies on plasmids, but their uneven distribution and over-expression of proteins distort selection for dosage-sensitive metabolic enzymes. To avoid these artifacts, we employed a CRISPR-Cas9 editing system to evolve a Listeria innocua hisIE fusion directly within the E. coli His operon, maintaining native expression levels. We generated two libraries: one maintaining the fusion and one with forced fission by reintroducing stop and start codons before each round of selection. We evolve the fusion only, but measured the fitness of fusion and fission at every generation using pacbio. By tracking the fitness of the fusion relative to its non-fused counterpart, we can identify the specific mutations that enlarge the fitness differences between the two states. <br />

## Assessing Growth–Survival Trade-offs in Fluctuating Environments

**Presenter:** Megan Behringer, Vanderbilt University

**Abstract:**

Microbes are evolutionarily robust organisms capable of rapid adaptation to complex stress, enabling them to colonize harsh environments. In nature, microbes are regularly challenged by starvation, a particularly complex stress because resource limitation often co-occurs with changes in pH, osmolarity, and toxin accumulation created by metabolic waste. Often overlooked are the additional complications introduced by eventual resource replenishment, as successful microbes must withstand rapid environmental shifts before swiftly capitalizing on renewed resources to avoid invasion by competing species.<br />Using longitudinal population-genomic analysis of Escherichia coli populations evolved under extreme feast–famine conditions, we found that adaptation is characterized by narrow evolutionary trajectories with high mutational parallelism and reproducible mutational order. Genetic reconstructions reveal that early-arising mutations impose trade-offs for biofilm formation and motility but trade-ups for growth and survival, as these mutations confer positively correlated advantages during both short-term growth and long-term starvation.<br />To expand these investigations and directly quantify how fitness effects vary across distinct periods of the growth cycle, we developed a Bayesian multilevel framework for longitudinal randomly barcoded transposon sequencing (RB-TnSeq). By applying this framework to a 10-day feast–famine starvation regime in Escherichia coli, we can generate a one-dimensional Fisher’s geometric “seascape” model. Ultimately, resulting in a compact, genome-wide description of growth–survival trade-offs, linking short-term competitive fitness effects to long-term adaptive outcomes.<br />

## Experimental reconstruction of the domestication, evolution, and adaptive role of horizontal gene transfer in eukaryotes.

**Presenter:** Florian Mattenberger, Gregor Mendel Institute

**Abstract:**

New genes are crucial for organismal evolution providing new functions key for cellular innovations. Recently, horizontal gene transfer (HGT), defined as the acquisition of genetic material from different species, has been shown to have an important effect on eukaryotic genome evolution. HGT has an unprecedent adaptative potential as it can introduce key functional innovations in the absence of genetic predecessors. However, important constraints impair HGT, making foreign genes unstable and likely to be lost. Overcoming these challenges requires gaining expression in the host genome, eliciting functional effects upon which selection can act, and avoiding conflicts with pre-existing cellular components. Accordingly, only a small fraction of HGTs overcome these challenges but even these few HGTs have the potential to underpin major biological transitions. Despite this, our understanding of eukaryotic HGTs is almost entirely based on observational comparative genomics and phylogenetics which are correlative and can only depict transferred genes at a single point in time, usually after domestication. Here we experimentally mimic the early stages of HGT by randomly introducing a bacterial antibiotic resistance gene, depicted of any regulatory element, into the genome of Chlamydomonas reinhardtii. By identifying hundreds of antibiotic-resistant strains with de novo bacterial gene insertions we are characterizing how bacterial genes can gain expression following integration. By using long-term experimental evolution of resistant strains we are tracking in real time the domestication of HGT. Our results reveal the importance of co-option during foreign gene establishment and highlight important cost-benefit tradeoffs which may dictate HGT retention over evolutionary time.

## Understanding the rules of regulation: Rewiring gene networks with synthetic regulators from random sequence

**Presenter:** Arianne Babina, University of Glasgow

**Abstract:**

Gene regulatory networks are central to organismal adaptation, yet how novel regulatory interactions arise and integrate into existing genetic networks are not fully understood. To address this gap, we developed a synthetic biology framework in Escherichia coli to study the de novo emergence and evolution of novel regulatory interactions from random or nonfunctional sequence space. Using diverse expression libraries of randomly generated DNA sequences and high-throughput selection screens, we investigated how unstructured genetic diversity can be co-opted into functional regulatory elements in vivo, isolating de novo synthetic regulators that rewire genetic networks to restore E. coli growth under non-permissive conditions such as nutrient limitation and antibiotic stress. These synthetic regulators include small proteins and noncoding RNAs that act through RNA–protein and protein–protein interactions to upregulate alternative metabolic pathways, modulate signal transduction cascades, and alter cellular interactions with the environment. Detailed characterization of these selected regulators helps define the determinants necessary for robust, context-dependent regulatory interactions and informs the design of targeted synthetic genetic regulators. By integrating these synthetic library screens with experimental evolution approaches, we can now track in vivo evolutionary trajectories from “gene birth” to better understand the fundamental constraints, sources of robustness, and pathways to innovation in genetic networks. Together, this work establishes random sequence library screens as a powerful tool for dissecting the molecular basis of gene regulatory network evolution and for guiding the rational design of novel synthetic regulators for basic research, biotechnology, and drug development.

## Interplay of gene conversion and nucleotide skews is driving the evolution of macrolide antibiotics

**Presenter:** Aleksandra Nivina, University Of Zurich

**Abstract:**

Like many other antibiotics used in the clinic, macrolides are natural products secreted by members of the bacterial genus Streptomyces. They are biosynthesized by modular polyketide synthases, massive and complex enzymatic machines encoded in biosynthetic gene clusters.<br />Gene conversion is a key evolutionary mechanism to generate the large chemical diversity of naturally occurring macrolide antibiotics. It is a kind of non-reciprocal recombination event that "copy-pastes" stretches of DNA from one homologous region to another. Gene conversion homogenizes sequences within an individual genome but increases sequence diversity in a population. Using a set of synthetic constructs, we demonstrate that gene conversion happens at remarkably high frequencies in Streptomyces. Surprisingly, DNA regions that have undergone gene conversion have a highly biased nucleotide composition, prompting us to name these sequences GRINS - genetic repeats of intense nucleotide skews.<br />Using experimental evolution of genetically modified strains coupled with long-read sequencing and population dynamics modeling, we have captured the molecular processes responsible for the diversification of macrolide antibiotics. Gene conversion can help create combinatorial libraries of polyketide synthases with modified enzymatic functions, while nucleotide skews change the DNA sequence and amino acid composition of these synthases.<br />More generally, our work demonstrates a new mechanism for accelerating molecular innovation and sheds light on the co-evolution of antibiotic biosynthesis and resistance.<br />
