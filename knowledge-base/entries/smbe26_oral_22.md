## Primary cilia regulation linked to evolution of sex determination in vertebrates with and without sex chromosomess

**Presenter:** Nicole Valenzuela, *Iowa State University*

**Abstract:** The full genetic architecture of vertebrate sexual development remains elusive despite extensive fragmentary data documenting evolutionary changes at upstream regulators of sex determination and at downstream mediators of sex differentiation. We report on the first study to build and compare species-specific regulatory networks of gonadal development between closely related vertebrates with contrasting sex-determining mechanisms, using matched time-course sampling, to uncover key steps in the transitions in sex determination by testing for the conservation or divergence of modular components. Namely, we modeled gene-transcription factor interaction networks for two turtle species with and without sex chromosomes (Apalone spinifera – with a ZZ-ZW system of genotypic sex determination or GSD, and Chrysemys picta – with temperature-dependent sex determination or TSD) and tested multiple alternative hypotheses: that transcription factor (TF) hubs and their target genes are conserved between species (null H0); that the same TF hub targets a new set of genes, retaining or not the ancestral function (H1 and variants); that a new TF hub takes over the regulation of ancestral gene targets (H2); or that a complete overhaul took place where ancestral TF hubs and their target genes were replaced (H3). Results implicate primary cilia as integrators of environmental signals underlying TSD, and point to key changes in their sensory machinery that accompanied TSD-GSD transitions, expanding the most recent models of epigenetic regulation and the evolution of plasticity and canalization.

## Drivers of primary sex ratio bias in dwarf willow

**Presenter:** Sophie Karrenberg, *Uppsala University*

**Abstract:** Deviations from Fisher’s equal sex ratio at birth or seed production (primary sex ratio) can be due to meiotic drive or allelic incompatibility. The resulting sex ratio selection is proposed to contribute to the fast evolution of sex chromosomes. Yet, empirical studies on primary sex ratio bias are challenging and only limited data is available, particularly from natural populations. We investigate mechanisms leading to sex ratio bias in the dwarf willow Salix herbacea that has ZW sex determination, strongly female-biased sex ratios at the population level and variable sex ratios among families. We used pooled whole-genome re-sequencing to track allele transmission from parents to female, male and non-flowering offspring in six within-population crosses with a wide range of sex ratios. We further examined the inheritance of sex ratio bias over two generations. Our results suggest a dominant mechanism of maternally controlled sex ratios with biased transmission of autosomal alleles from different chromosomes to the rare sex, as expected under a scenario of W-chromosome drive with autosomal restorers in females. Moreover, we detected evidence for structural variation on the sex chromosomes. Overall, our findings are consistent with an evolutionary arms race situation leading to W-drive with multiple autosomal restorers. Our study is thus supports an important role of sex ratio selection for the dynamic evolution of sex chromosomes.

## Bagworm moths: a new model for studies of genome evolution across variable levels of sexual dimorphism, from mild to extreme.

**Presenter:** Christina Watkins, *The University of Florida*

**Abstract:** Sexual dimorphism is intriguing because each sex within a species shares the same genomic information while manifesting striking, sex-specific phenotypes. Although this topic has received substantial attention, inferences are often limited as most examined systems exhibit mere modifications of a shared body plan. Therefore, systems containing varying degrees of dimorphism are needed to clarify how sex-specific phenotypes arise. One such system is the Bagworm moths (Lepidoptera: Psychidae). In this conserved ZZ/Z0 system, females exhibit a continuum of body plans: fully winged, half-winged, wingless, and vermiform (which lack most typical adult characters). Males retain fully developed wings/ appendages. Paired with peculiar reproductive ecologies, Psychidae provide a powerful model for examining how variation in sexual dimorphism shapes genome evolution.<br />Here, we introduce Psychidae as a comparative system, present preliminary results, and outline efforts to characterize genomic variation using a phylogenetic framework. Leveraging publicly available data and in-house generated chromosome-level assemblies, we explore how shifts in female form relate to genome architecture, including structure, divergence rates, genetic novelty, and heterozygosity. Initial analyses reveal overall highly conserved synteny, with gene movement onto and off the Z chromosome largely confined to vermiform lineages. The Z chromosome also contains proportionally more novel genes than autosomes in highly dimorphic species. Expression patterns indicate increased female-biased novelty in the winged-to-wingless transition, while vermiform evolution involves novel genes expressed in both sexes. Together, this work highlights Psychidae as an integrative system for the examination of sex chromosome dynamics, chromosome rearrangement, and sexual dimorphism within a single evolutionary framework.

## A comparison of selection on autosomes and the young X
chromosome in diploid Mercurialis species


**Presenter:** Suhaas Sehgal, *University Of Lausanne*

**Abstract:** The evolutionary dynamics of proto-X and Y chromosomes during early divergence—when most genes remain functional but recombination is suppressed—have been explored theoretically. A recent model  (the "slow-X" hypothesis) predicted that genes on young X chromosomes should exhibit lower adaptive substitution rates than autosomal genes due to their smaller effective population size and the ‘masking’ of their gene products by their Y-linked allelic homologs in males, while they remain functional. Accordingly, young X-linked genes should tend to accumulate deleterious mutations faster than autosomal genes. We tested these predictions in the dioecious plant Mercurialis annua and its sister species M. huetii, for which we are assembling a haplotype-phased genome. These species have young sex chromosomes with a large non-recombining region on the Y, where most genes are still intact, allowing us to evaluate the slow-X hypothesis. Although autosomal and young X-linked genes show similar divergence between species, X-linked genes segregate a higher proportion of non-synonymous polymorphisms. Selection tests indicate lower adaptive potential on the X chromosomes, and the distribution of fitness effects is skewed toward deleterious mutations with few or no beneficial ones. Overall, these results support the predictions of the slow-X effect.

## Diversity of dosage compensation mechanisms in squamate reptiles

**Presenter:** Michail Rovatsos, *Faculty of Science, Charles University*

**Abstract:** Differentiation of sex chromosomes is typically accompanied by the cessation of recombination and subsequent gene loss from the degenerated sex chromosome (Y or W), resulting in gene dosage imbalances between the sexes. Classical theory, based largely on studies in model organisms, predicts the evolution of mechanisms to compensate for these imbalances. Dosage compensation, or at least dosage balance in the expression of sex-linked genes, has been well documented in lineages with male heterogamety (XX/XY). In contrast, lineages with female heterogamety (ZZ/ZW) are often thought to lack global, chromosome-wide regulatory mechanisms for gene dosage, particularly at the transcriptional level. In this study, we combined high-throughput sequencing with comparative bioinformatics to investigate patterns of transcriptional gene dose regulation in eleven independently evolved sex chromosome systems of squamate reptiles. Our findings provide novel evidence from non-traditional model organisms, revealing the molecular diversity and evolutionary complexity of gene dose regulatory mechanisms across vertebrates.

## Convergent evolution of genetic sex determination among species of stickleback fishes

**Presenter:** Michael White, *University Of Georgia*

**Abstract:** The evolution of sex chromosomes begins with the acquisition of a primary sex determination gene. Although sex differentiation is a remarkably conserved process among vertebrates, the primary sex determination gene that initiates sex differentiation can rapidly evolve across taxa. Stickleback fishes are an excellent model group to understand how newly evolved primary sex determination genes gain this function. The threespine stickleback (Gasterosteaus aculeatus) evolved a Y chromosome approximately 22 million years ago. Within the oldest region of the Y chromosome is a copy of anti-Müllerian hormone (Amh), which duplicated and translocated from autosome eight. We have shown this Y-specific duplication of Amh (Amhy) is both necessary and sufficient for male development. We are now comparing sex determination in the threespine stickleback with a closely related species, the brook stickleback (Culaea inconstans), which has an independently evolved Y chromosome at the early stages of differentiation. We have completed a genome assembly and discovered a 7 kb insertion on the new Y chromosome that contains a convergent duplication of Amh. In contrast to the threespine, the brook stickleback Amhy only has a handful of mutations that differentiate it from the ancestral autosomal paralog, indicating it evolved much more recently. Despite its recent origin, Amhy has already evolved multiple unique isoforms that are not detected from the ancestral autosomal locus or from the convergently evolved Amhy in the threespine stickleback. Both species will therefore offer unique insights into the different ways Amhy regulation can evolve to neofunctionalize as a primary sex determination gene.

## A Tale of Skink Sex Chromosome Evolution

**Presenter:** Paul Waters, *University Of New South Wales*

**Abstract:** Sex chromosomes typically originate from a pair of autosomes after one member acquires a sex‑determining locus across which recombination becomes suppressed with its partner. Following the onset of recombination suppression, the sex‑specific chromosome—here the Y—loses gene function and undergoes structural and sequence degeneration, resulting in a gene poor, repeat rich, small chromosome that recombines with the X in a small pseudoautosomal region (PAR). Here we present near chromosome level genome assemblies for four genera of skinks. Surprisingly, each genus has a unique trajectory of sex chromosome evolution. The genus Bassiana is the only one that has what would be considered the traditional trajectory of sex chromosome evolution described above. The genus Pseudemoia has a small PAR, but in contrast to Bassiana has not wasted away. In the genus Lampropholis, there is no evidence of sex chromosomes, suggesting that the Y has been lost and there is a new genetic or environmental sex determining system. Finally, the genus Tiliqua has a large PAR and small X/Y specific regions. In these regions there is direct evidence of inversions on the Y that supressed recombination with the X. These skinks offer rare insight into different evolutionary trajectories that shape sex chromosomes.

## Causes and consequences of sex-chromosome turnovers in Diptera

**Presenter:** Lorena Layana, *Institute of Science and Technology Austria*

**Abstract:** Sex-chromosome systems are highly variable across animals, but how they transition from one to another is not well understood. Diptera have undergone multiple sex-chromosome turnovers and expansions while maintaining their general chromosomal content, which makes them an ideal clade to study such transitions. We analyzed more than 100 dipteran whole-genome assemblies and identified 4 new lineages that underwent sex-chromosome turnover (in addition to the 5 previously reported). We find that the majority of turnovers happened in the group Schizophora, which tend to have fewer genes on Muller element F. (the chromosome homologous to the ancestral insect X chromosome) than lower dipterans, a factor previously hypothesized to facilitate turnover. Most derived X chromosomes have higher GC content than autosomes, consistent with a high prevalence of male achiasmy in Diptera. In addition, an excess of gene movement out of the X is detected for most of these new X chromosomes, and many of these moved genes have high testis expression in Drosophila, suggesting that out-of-X gene movement contributes to the long-term demasculinization of X chromosomes.

## Evolution of recent autosomic translocations to the X of iconic neotropical bats

**Presenter:** Liliana M Davalos, *Stony Brook University*

**Abstract:** While Eutherian mammal sex chromosomes are highly differentiated, their pseudo-autosomal region maintains recombination in males resulting in distinctive rates of evolution compared to their non-recombining genes. But how this shift in evolutionary rate first occurred is obscured by the age of this event. Here we analyze rates of evolution across the X chromosome of stenodermatine bats, a rapid bat radiation characterized by, among other traits, a large autosomal translocation to the X some 20 million years ago. Compared to other regions of the X, we find no evidence of recombination suppression (i.e., no gene loss) and rates of evolution similar to those of autosomes, consistent with ongoing recombination between this translocation and the stenodermatine -specific Y2. We propose this recent translocation is a sex-linked autosome.

## Alternative splicing and sex-specific adaptation in stick insects

**Presenter:** Iulia Darolti, *University Of Lausanne*

**Abstract:** Males and females are often subject to antagonistic selection pressures over shared traits. While research has focused on the role of gene expression regulation in the evolution of sexual dimorphism and the resolution of sexual conflict, other regulatory mechanisms, such as alternative splicing, remain comparatively understudied. We leverage the multiple, independent transitions from sexual to parthenogenetic reproduction that have occurred in Timema stick insects, together with PacBio long-read sequencing of somatic and reproductive tissues, to study sex differences in gene splicing and expression and to investigate how these change following the loss of sex and sexual conflicts. Alternative splicing is widespread in Timema, with the greatest transcriptional complexity occurring in male gonads. Differentially spliced genes between males and females undergo faster lineage-specific turnover and are evolving under distinct selective constraints compared to sex-biased genes. However, we identify a core set of gonadal-expressed genes with conserved patterns of sex-specific transcript usage across the phylogeny.  Following the transition to asexuality, we consistently detect a decrease in sex-biased isoform abundance. Taken together, our results suggest that relaxed selection in parthenogenetic lineages reshapes the transcriptome not by increasing splicing noise, but through the decay of sex-specialized isoforms.
