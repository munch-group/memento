## From Sterility to Clonality: Linking Rapid Sex-Chromosome Turnover to Asexual Reproduction in a Vertebrate Hybrid Complex

**Presenter:** Lucija Andjel, *Institute Of Animal Physiology And Genetics Of The Czech Academy Of Sciences*

**Abstract:** Interspecific hybridisation often triggers meiotic aberrations and the production of unreduced gametes, leading to asexual clonal reproduction. This outcome is strikingly sex-specific: hybrid males are typically sterile, while females reproduce via altered meiosis (e.g., premeiotic endoreplication). It remains an intriguing question whether this asymmetry stems from genome-wide incompatibilities or chromosome-specific conflicts, especially those involving divergent sex-determination systems. Answering this requires high-quality parental genomic references.<br />We therefore generated chromosome-level genomes for C. elongatoides, C. taenia, and C. tanaitica. Pool-Seq identified nascent, species-specific, sex-determining regions. This revealed rapid sex-chromosome turnover with XY systems on distinct chromosomes (Ch01 in C. elongatoides, putative Ch05 in C. taenia, putative Ch06 in C. tanaitica). In elongatoides-taenia F1 hybrids, species-specific Y-markers usually follow expected inheritance. Yet, we found notable exceptions, including females carrying one of the parental Y-markers and males lacking both—indicating ongoing turnover across lineages.<br />Critically, cytogenetic analysis in hybrid males shows severely disrupted meiotic pairing of these divergent sex chromosomes and autosomes. This failure directly links rapid sex-chromosome evolution to universal hybrid male sterility, creating the genomic conflict necessary for the switch to asexuality in hybrid females.<br />Our results demonstrate that hybrid meiotic behaviour is driven by chromosome-specific incompatibilities, as evidenced by variable bivalent formation, coinciding with rapid turnover of distinct sex chromosomes between species. This positions Cobitis as a valuable vertebrate model for linking parental genome architecture to the hybrid dichotomy of male sterility versus female clonality, uncovering a direct genomic pathway to asexuality.<br /><br />Keywords: loaches, sex determination, chromosome evolution, hybrid sterility, (a)sexual reproduction

## Functional verification of a genetic trigger for female sex determination in the Marsabit clawed frog (Xenopus borealis)

**Presenter:** Ben Evans, *Mcmaster University*

**Abstract:** A central question in biology asks why in some groups, the genetic basis of sexual differentiation evolves so quickly even though disruption of this process has severe risks such as sterility. A challenge to understanding mechanisms of this rapid evolution is a lack of functionally verified genetic triggers for sex determination. To begin to remedy this, we used fieldwork, bioinformatics, cytogenetics, and genetic engineering to isolate and functionally test a candidate sex determining gene in the Marsabit clawed frog X. borealis, which has a species-specific and newly evolved W chromosome. Whole genome re-sequencing data identified several candidate triggers for female sexual differentiation, and RNAseq data indicates that only one of these was expressed specifically in female gonads during sexual differentiation. Using genetic engineering, we knocked out this gene, which is a female-specific duplicate of wnt3.L – hereafter wnt3-W – and induced complete female-to-male sex reversal. The sex reversed individuals have male-typical external and internal morphology, mating behavior, testis histology, and mating calls, and are fertile. These results demonstrate that wnt3-W is the genetic trigger for feminization in X. borealis and bring the number of functionally verified triggers for sex determination in amphibians from one to two. WNT signaling is known to play key roles in ovarian development, but this typically involves wnt gene family members other than wnt3. The discovery of wnt3-W raises the question of whether this female-determining function involves crosstalk with the TGFβ pathway, which plays important roles in triggering and determining sex in several other species.

## Sex chromosome turnover: why some sex chromosomes never settle, and others never leave.

**Presenter:** Paul Saunders, *CEFE - CNRS UMR 5175*

**Abstract:** Sex chromosomes have evolved countless times throughout the tree of life, yet the rate at which new sex chromosomes arise and replace the old ones varies dramatically across lineages. Some clades experience frequent turnover, cycling through largely homomorphic sex chromosomes, while others evolve long-lived and often heteromorphic sex chromosomes that appear to lose the ability to turnover. The diversity in turnover dynamics and the mechanisms leading sex chromosomes to lose this ability are often cited as some of the remaining mysteries in the field, but remain largely unexplored in theoretical models.<br />Using individual-based simulations, we explore how turnover dynamics are shaped by different modes of sex chromosome recombination suppression, by Y degeneration, by the evolution of dosage compensation, and by other processes such as spontaneous sex reversal.<br />Simulations uncover two distinct evolutionary regimes: a “turnover-prone” regime characterised by an endless replacement of sex chromosomes, and a “turnover-proof” regime in which turnovers are effectively halted. Transitions between the two are abrupt rather than gradual, indicating a bistable system. While mutation rates and the mode of recombination suppression modulate turnover frequency within the turnover-prone regime, the emergence of dosage compensation plays the key role in locking sex chromosomes into a stable, turnover-resistant state. Transitions toward turnover resistance are typically unidirectional; however, we identify rare conditions that can reopen a window for turnover, providing potential explanations for empirical cases in which lineages escape old and stable sex chromosome systems.

## Sex-specific modifications of gametogenesis in natural and lab-bred Fundulus spp. hybrids

**Presenter:** Anne-marie Dion-Côté, *Université De Moncton*

**Abstract:** Successful hybridization between species can be limited by a variety of reproductive barriers; the emergence of asexual lineages is an example of a strong post zygotic barrier that is relatively rare in animals. In vertebrates, asexual reproduction typically arises through gametogenic alterations such as premeiotic genome endoreplication or meiotic failure, yet the relative frequency and conditions leading to their emergence remain poorly understood. We investigated natural, all female clonal hybrid lineages and laboratory-bred hybrids of both sexes between two North American killifishes, Fundulus diaphanus and F. heteroclitus, to determine the mechanisms underlying and maintaining the emergence of asexual reproduction and male sterility. By combining cytogenetic, molecular, and gametogenetic analyses, we learned that both natural and lab-bred F1 hybrid females can produce diploid and tetraploid oocytes. In diploid oocytes, we observed both bivalents and univalents. In contrast, tetraploid oocytes formed exclusively bivalents with normal crossover formation, consistent with premeiotic genome endoreplication restoring proper meiotic pairing and facilitating clonal reproduction. Hybrid males do not undergo premeiotic genome duplication and exhibit extensive chromosomal mispairing, leading to aberrant meiotic divisions, elevated apoptosis, and decreased fertility or sterility. Together, these findings highlight sex-specific differences in gametogenesis, paralleling patterns observed in other systems, and emphasize the relative ease with which asexual reproduction can arise in hybrid females, in contrast to the sterility commonly observed in hybrid males.

## Reproductive morph specialisation facilitated by a maternal sex determining region in a fungus gnat (Bradysia coprophila)

**Presenter:** Melany Henot, *University Of Edinburgh*

**Abstract:** Sexual reproduction promotes the evolution of specialised gametes and sexes with divergent reproductive roles. The resulting, cyclical relationship between selection for sexual dimorphism and the structure of sex determining regions shapes sex‑chromosome evolution and helps explain the evolution and stability of mating systems. <br /><br />Black-winged fungus gnats (Family: Sciaridae) are a group of Dipteran insects with maternal genetic sex determination, a unique system where maternal genotype - instead of zygotic genotype - determines zygotic sex. Within this, split-sex ratios have repeatedly evolved, where some females produce all-female broods while others produce all-male broods. In Bradysia coprophila, female reproductive morph is determined by a large X-linked inversion, which is present only in female-producing females. This relatively young (<0.5 Mya), morph-determining and non-recombining region offers an informative parallel to the evolution of sex-determining regions and sex chromosomes. Does this maternally-acting region accumulate deleterious mutations, or facilitate specialisation into female-production? <br /><br />Using B. coprophila as a model, we observe that female morphs diverge significantly in phenotype and gene expression, in functions other than offspring sex determination. Despite signatures of degradation and reduced expression of homologs on the inversion, the fitness of female-producing females is maintained. Combined with analyses of rates of molecular evolution on X-linked genes, this suggests specialisation into distinct reproductive roles, which we hypothesise can partly explain the rapid turnover of mating systems in this family. We speculate on potential drivers for specialisation and show that sex-specific maternal provisioning could be involved.

## Evolutionary and Functional Consequences of W–Autosome Fusions in Heliconius Butterflies

**Presenter:** Nicol Rueda, *Wellcome Sanger Institute*

**Abstract:** Fusions between sex chromosomes and autosomes can resolve sexual conflict, drive local adaptation, and promote reproductive isolation, thus warranting particular attention in evolutionary genomics. Heliconius butterflies typically exhibit a stable karyotype of 21 chromosomes with strong synteny across species; however, three species in the sapho subclade show deviations, with chromosome counts reaching up to 64. We generated 11 chromosome-level reference genomes and gene annotations for six of the seven species and subspecies in this subclade. Our findings confirm three W–autosome fusions we had previously suspected based on whole-genome resequencing and reveal an additional fusion in two subspecies of H. eleuchia. Analysis of the chromosome-level genomes shows that, in three species, the autosomes have undergone extensive fissions, increasing chromosome number. The Z chromosome remains conserved across species. In contrast, W–autosome fusions involve complex structural rearrangements, including translocations, inversions, and further fissions of the neo-W chromosome. Despite these rearrangements, chromatin compartment organization remains largely unchanged. Genes located on the neo-W show signs of gradual degeneration, with 50% genes disrupted or lost on the oldest fusion and 24% on the youngest. We find no evidence that lost genes on the neo-W are compensated through dosage compensation mechanisms. Interestingly, despite the overall degeneration of the W chromosome, some genes—such as the W-specific rhodopsin—remain present even on the older W-autosome fusion. Together, these results reveal the evolutionary, structural, and functional consequences of W–autosome fusions in Lepidoptera over time.

## Repeated losses of self-fertility shaped heterozygosity and polyploidy in yeast evolution 

**Presenter:** Nina Vittorelli, *Sorbonne Université / Collège De France*

**Bio:** PhD student
Working on reproductive systems, genome evolution and yeasts

**Abstract:** Evolutionary transitions in mating strategy have profound consequences for genetic variation and adaptation. In the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae, mating-type switching is a central feature of the life cycle that enables haploid cells to be self-fertile and mate with their own mitotic descendants (homothallism). Yet heterothallic isolates that have lost this ability are found across diverse niches, indicating that this trait is polymorphic. Here, we experimentally characterized loss of mating-type switching in a representative panel of strains. Analysis of 117 telomere-to-telomere genome assemblies revealed multiple independent loss-of-function mutations in the Ho endonuclease gene which is essential for switching. We estimated that at least 13 independent transitions from homothallism to heterothallism have occurred in the species history. Analysis of the HO genotype of 2,910 strains shows that at least 27% are heterothallic. We found that heterothallism is strongly associated with polyploidy and elevated genome-wide heterozygosity, although the strength of these associations varies between populations. Heterothallic isolates are most prevalent in domesticated and clinical clades, consistent with an origin linked to human-associated environments. However, they are also found, though less frequently, in natural niches. Signatures of recombination in HO sequences suggest that outcrossing contributed to the ecological and geographical distribution of the trait. Our findings reveal that mating-type switching has undergone repeated losses in Saccharomyces cerevisiae evolution, with major consequences for genome architecture and ecological diversification.

## Unveiling the Evolutionary Dynamics of the X Chromosome in Aphids

**Presenter:** Gaorui Gong, *INRAE IGEPP*

**Abstract:** Sex chromosomes often display distinct evolutionary dynamics compared to autosomes. In many organisms, the X chromosome evolves faster than autosomes due to hemizygous exposure of recessive alleles and/or reduced effective population size. Disentangling the relative contributions of these two factors is challenging. However, aphids, with their XX/X0 system and cyclical parthenogenesis, offer a compelling model to examine these processes at broad phylogenetic scales.<br />We conducted comparative genomic analyses across 53 aphid species, including four newly assembled high-quality genomes, to test whether faster-X evolution is a general pattern and to explore its underlying mechanisms. Despite remarkable macro-synteny conservation, X chromosomes exhibit significantly higher sequence divergence than autosomes across species, consistent with a pervasive faster-X effect. Strikingly, this elevated divergence correlates strongly with recent transposable element expansion and segmental duplication on the X. We identified abundant lineage-specific gene copies enriched on the X chromosome, many residing in regions shaped by TE activity, suggesting that TEs may contribute to the proliferation of these duplicates. Multi-species transcriptomic analyses further reveal that X chromosomes are consistently enriched for male-biased genes.<br />Our findings demonstrate that faster-X evolution is a wide phenomenon in aphids, associated with TE-related sequence turnover and the accumulation of lineage-specific duplicates on the X. The observed male-biased expression of these copies hints at functional differentiation, though the relative contributions of adaptive versus non-adaptive processes remain to be resolved. This work highlights the X chromosome as a potential hotspot for genomic innovation in aphids.

## Sex chromosome variation across vertebrate genome assemblies

**Presenter:** Melissa Wilson

**Abstract:** The mechanisms of sex determination, the system that determines whether an organism differentiates into male or female, vary extensively across vertebrate lineages. While birds and mammals have largely conserved genetic sex determination systems, other vertebrate clades, including squamate reptiles, turtles, ray-finned fish, and chondrichthyan fish, have multiple independent sex determination systems. This independent evolution made identifying sex chromosomes in whole genome assemblies challenging. Recently, researchers in the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP) have generated multiple haplotype-resolved genome assemblies and annotated sex chromosomes across the vertebrate Tree of Life. Using VGP’s high-quality dataset, we analyzed sex chromosome biology across ~500 MY of vertebrate evolution, examining variation in relative sex chromosome size and repeat density. We defined pseudoautosomal region (PAR) boundaries using sex chromosome alignments to compare sex chromosome structure within and between taxonomic groups. We found that Z and X chromosomes are generally, but not always, larger than W and Y chromosomes, and the size ratio varies extensively within and between vertebrate clades. We further applied multiple approaches to identify previously unannotated sex chromosomes in the VGP genome assemblies, uncovering putatively novel chromosomal sex determination systems. Here we present our summary analyses, including novel approaches for annotating and curating sex chromosomes in haplotype resolved assemblies. <br />

## Sex chromosome evolution mediated by a large inversion and a possible switch of the sex determination gene

**Presenter:** Xiaomeng Mao, *Stockholm University*

**Abstract:** Sex chromosomes often evolve faster than autosomes and commonly degenerate after recombination arrest. However, the underlying evolutionary processes are under persistent debate. In particular, it is unclear whether or not recombination arrest generally evolves in a stepwise manner and how switches in sex determination genes contribute to sex chromosome evolution. Here, we investigate sex chromosome evolution in the dioecious plant genus Salix. <br />We identified Z and W regions (~8 Mb) on chromosome 15 of the dwarf willow Salix herbacea using a new haplotype-resolved assembly. The W region harboured a large (5 Mb) embedded inversion. Analyses of synteny with other Salix species, sequence divergence between sex chromosomes and sequence degeneration suggest that this inversion recently incorporated pseudoautosomal sequence into the W region, extending its length nearly three-fold. The W region exclusively contained seven pairs of inverted partial repeats of the male essential floral identity gene PISTILLATA, suggesting a possible PISTILLATA suppression mechanism by interfering RNA in females. Such PISTILLATA pseudogenes were also found in other Salix species with ZW sex determination, but not in those with XY sex determination. <br />Our study provides rare and compelling support for the long-standing theory of inversions underlying stepwise recombination reduction and raises the hypothesis that the turnover of sex chromosomes in the Salicaceae family might be associated with a switch of the sex determination gene.

## Tetrapolar mating relaxes host boundaries through sexual recombination between wheat- and barley-adapted Puccinia striiformis rust fungi. 

**Presenter:** Julian Rodriguez Algaba, *Aarhus University*

**Abstract:** Reproductive barriers maintain genetic structure in host-specialized plant pathogens, yet it remains unclear whether host specialization alone prevents sexual reproduction when mating mechanisms are conserved. We tested sexual compatibility between the phylogenetically divergent (~8 million years) wheat-adapted Puccinia striiformis f. sp. tritici (Pst) and barley-adapted P. striiformis f. sp. hordei (Psh), which share common barberry (Berberis vulgaris) as an alternate host for sexual reproduction.<br />Chromosome-scale assemblies showed that the homeodomain (HD) and pheromone receptor (PR) mating-type loci reside on different chromosomes (chr2 and chr6, respectively). Replicated reciprocal crosses on barberry produced viable sexual progeny, and locus-specific restriction assays at the HD locus confirmed segregation of parental alleles, providing genetic validation that tetrapolar mating remains functional despite host specialization. Host specificity assays on wheat and barley differentials, near-isogenic lines, and wild relatives confirmed strict parental host specificity, whereas progeny displayed dual-host compatibility and recombinant virulence phenotypes.<br />These results demonstrate that ecological divergence on primary cereal hosts can coexist with sexual compatibility on a shared alternate host, enabling recombination that reshuffles host-associated virulence and relaxes strict host boundaries. More broadly, they highlight how mating-system architecture can preserve opportunities for sexual reproduction and genetic exchange across host specialization boundaries, with implications for host range evolution and sexual diversification in fungal pathogens.<br />

## Sex-chromosome evolution and speciation in the genus Salmo 

**Presenter:** Marte Sodeland, *University Of Agder*

**Abstract:** Species may arise through emergence of genetic incompatibilities that separate lineages of shared ancestry. These incompatibilities frequently arise in tandem with evolution of sex-chromosomes. Here we identify an extensive 20Mb chromosomal region exhibiting suppressed meiotic recombination that flank the sex determining loci in the Brown trout (Salmo trutta). We show that this extensive ‘supergene’ of tight meiotic linkage emerged through a double chromosomal inversion, separating S. trutta from its sister species in the genus Salmo, the Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar). By utilizing whole-genome sequencing we provide evidence that selection contribute to extension of the sex-supergene beyond the boundaries of the originating double inversion, a hypothesized trajectory in sex-chromosome evolution. Within S. trutta gradual repression of recombination between the sex chromosomes has resulted in formation of three evolutionary strata on the emerging Y chromosome that show different levels of divergence from the ancestral X chromosome. <br />A role of sex-chromosome evolution in speciation within the genus Salmo is supported by identification of incompatibility loci in derived evolutionary strata on the Y chromosome. We show that introgression between local populations is constrained by these incompatibility loci. Our study provides a novel case of sex-chromosome differentiation within the Salmonidae, and, demonstrate that sex-chromosome evolution may contribute to speciation in this highly diversified family of fishes.

## Neo-sex chromosome evolution and reproductive isolation in hybridizing gazelle lineages

**Presenter:** Thomas Bøggild, *University Of Copenhagen*

**Abstract:** The Grant’s gazelle complex in eastern Africa harbours rare autosome-sex chromosome translocations that have generated neo-sex chromosomes. We have sequenced 33 whole genomes from two principal lineages; Peter’s gazelle and Grant’s gazelle as well as a hybrid population, and performed population genetic analyses to understand how these neo-sex chromosomes influence variation within and between lineages. Using a novel method we find that two regions on the neo-sex chromosomes, one ≈51 megabase (Mb) segment derived from ancestral chromosome 16 and another ≈12.5 Mb segment from ancestral chromosome 5, have stopped recombining and show relaxation of purifying selection in their neo-Y-linked homologs, both hallmarks of sex chromosome degeneration. The hybrid population carries slightly more genomic ancestry from Peter’s gazelle than Grant’s gazelle, with admixture being both ancient and recent. Parental ancestry is unevenly distributed across the genome, with extensive “deserts” devoid of Grant’s gazelle ancestry. These non-recombining regions may constitute major barriers to gene flow: hybrid individuals from Mkomazi are fixed for Peter’s gazelle alleles in the neo-Y linked homologs in both regions, and nearly fixed for Peter’s gazelle in one of the corresponding neo-X regions. These results suggest that neo-sex chromosomes are helping to reinforce genetic barriers between these nascent species, providing an example of their role in an ongoing mammalian speciation process.<br />In addition, ongoing comparative analyses of similar chromosomal rearrangements in the tribe of Tragelaphines, could also help showcase the role of the neo-sex chromosomes on a broader scale between the different species of Tragelaphines which we have sampled.

## Experimental evidence that balancing selection maintains sexually antagonistic genetic variation in a polygenic trait

**Presenter:** Martyna Zwoinska, *Uppsala University*

**Abstract:** Genetic variation in fitness-related traits often persists despite ongoing selection, which is sometimes attributed to balancing selection. Sexually antagonistic selection (SAS), in which alleles have opposing fitness effects in males and females, has been proposed as one mechanism maintaining such polymorphism. However, how SAS influences genome-wide evolutionary responses during selection on polygenic traits remains unclear. Here, we address this question using an Evolve-and-Resequence experiment in the seed beetle Callosobruchus maculatus, manipulating the strength of sexual antagonism over body size and tracking genomic change.<br /><br />We evolved replicated populations for ten generations under three regimes: sex-limited selection acting in a single sex, SAS (simultaneously favouring larger females and smaller males), and a relaxed-selection control. All regimes produced consistent phenotypic responses. Sex-limited selection resulted in the strongest divergence in body size, whereas SAS led to the greatest increase in sexual body size dimorphism.<br /><br />Genomic responses contrasted markedly among regimes. Under SAS, allele-frequency changes were limited and genome-wide divergence remained low, despite ongoing phenotypic evolution. In contrast, relaxing sexual antagonism by selecting on only one sex produced large, repeatable allele-frequency shifts across populations. Loci underlying these responses were enriched for intermediate-frequency alleles and elevated Tajima’s D in the ancestral population, consistent with long-term balancing selection. Functional annotation implicated developmental processes, particularly genes involved in BMP signalling, which diverged under sex-limited selection but showed constrained allele-frequency change under SAS.<br /><br />Together, our results show that SAS can restrict short-term genomic divergence and preserve adaptive standing variation, reconciling persistent genetic diversity with ongoing phenotypic evolution.

## How and why genes survive on the human Y chromosome: Insights from telomere-to-telomere ape assemblies, long-read transcriptomes, and protein modeling

**Presenter:** Kateryna Makova, *Penn State University*

**Abstract:** The mammalian Y chromosome poses an evolutionary paradox: how do genes persist on this largely non-recombining chromosome prone to degeneration? Here, we address this question by integrating telomere-to-telomere Y chromosome assemblies from great apes with long-read transcriptomics and protein structure modeling. <br />Focusing on the seven canonical Y ampliconic gene (YAG) families (BPY2, CDY, DAZ, HSFY, RBMY, TSPY, and VCY), we resolve their full copy number, genomic organization, and evolutionary history at single-nucleotide resolution. We show that most YAGs are embedded within palindromes and tandem arrays that facilitate intrachromosomal gene conversion, driving concerted evolution and high sequence identity among copies, particularly those located on opposite arms of the same palindrome. Despite rapid structural lineage-specific expansions and contractions in copy number, our analyses reveal pervasive purifying selection on coding sequences, especially for CDY, HSFY, RBMY, and the RNA-recognition motifs of DAZ. Thus, structural dynamism coexists with strong functional constraint.<br />Using PacBio Iso-Seq from testis across great apes, we reconstruct isoform-resolved transcriptomes and demonstrate that gene survival is not merely genomic but functional: conserved structural isoforms—most strikingly for TSPY—are recurrently expressed across species, often supported by multiple sequence variants. Protein structure prediction further reveals that highly ordered domains are preserved despite extensive sequence and copy number variation.<br />In summary, Y-linked gene survival is enabled by palindrome-mediated homogenization, purifying selection, isoform conservation, and protein structural constraint. These findings illuminate the evolutionary scenario that preserves fertility-related genes on a non-recombining chromosome and offer new insight into the long-term persistence of the human Y chromosome.<br />

## Sex chromosome turnover has dramatically different consequences between closely related cichlid fishes

**Presenter:** Sophie Smith, *Leibniz Institute For The Analysis Of Biodiversity Change*

**Abstract:** Differentiating between drivers and consequences of sex chromosome turnover remains difficult, however studies of young, homomorphic sex chromosomes have greatly enhanced our ability to understand this dynamic. The Lake Tanganyika radiation of cichlid fishes shows a remarkably high rate of sex chromosome turnover amongst closely related species, providing a prime resource to investigate this enigma. Within one tribe of this radiation, Cyprichromini, our phylogenetic reconstruction revealed two turnovers involving the same homologous chromosome pair (LG05): a turnover from an XY to a ZW system; and a turnover involving a chromosome fusion, and an additional turnover to a different chromosome pair (LG15). Illustrating these turnovers, we generated haplotype-resolved whole genome assemblies of four species of this tribe, and combined this with cytogenetic investigations to precisely characterise the sex chromosomes, and understand what has driven their transitions. We find that, despite sharing LG05 as (at least part of) their sex chromosome, patterns of inversions, rearrangements and accumulation of deleterious mutations have followed independent evolutionary trajectories in three species of Cyprichromini. One of these species has undergone multiple chromosomal fusions, including a sex-specific neo-sex chromosome fusion, resulting in a Z₁Z₂W system. Finally, we show that a fourth species, Paracyprichromis nigripinnis (LG15 XY), has a degenerated Y chromosome, rare among cichlids, and remarkable considering the young age of this sex chromosome. The species-specific characteristics of sex chromosome evolution among these very closely related species shows how various factors can influence the evolution of sex chromosomes and why comparative studies are needed to understand this.
