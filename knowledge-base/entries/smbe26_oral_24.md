## Biological Sequence Language Models for Prediction and Design

**Presenter:** Yun Song, *University of California, Berkeley*

**Abstract:** Accurately predicting the functional effects of genetic variants is central to modern biology, with applications ranging from disease diagnosis to understanding complex trait architecture and engineering novel biomolecules. In this talk, I will present my lab's recent work on developing evolution-informed language models for biological sequences that capture the complex constraints shaping natural variation. I will showcase two frameworks: one that learns from genomic evolutionary patterns to predict non-coding variant effects across the human genome, and another that explicitly models evolutionary trajectories to enable realistic sequence generation. Through applications in human genetics and protein engineering, I will demonstrate how grounding AI models in evolutionary principles yields both improved predictive accuracy and biologically meaningful sequence generation, offering new tools for variant interpretation and biomolecular design.

## From Likelihood to Fitness: Accounting for Phylogeny to Improve Variant Effect Prediction

**Presenter:** Mafalda Dias, *Crg*

**Abstract:** Generative models trained on natural sequences are increasingly used to predict the effects of genetic variation, enabling progress in therapeutic design, disease diagnosis and risk prediction, and synthetic biology. In the zero-shot setting, variant impact is estimated by comparing the likelihoods of sequences, under the assumption that likelihood serves as a proxy for fitness. However, this assumption often breaks down in practice: sequence likelihood reflects not only evolutionary fitness constraints, but also phylogenetic structure and sampling biases, especially as model capacity increases. In this talk, I will present our recent advances toward meeting these challenges. I will introduce Likelihood-Fitness Bridging (LFB), a simple and general strategy that improves variant effect prediction by averaging model scores across sequences subject to similar selective pressures. Assuming an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck model of evolution, LFB can be viewed as a way to marginalize the effects of genetic drift, although its benefits appear to extend more broadly. LFB consistently improves predictive performance across model families and sizes, and notably, it reverses the performance plateau observed in larger protein language models. These results suggest that accounting for phylogenetic and sampling biases is essential to realizing the full potential of large sequence models in variant effect prediction.

## Learning protein interaction origination and evolution with sequence-based machine learning

**Presenter:** Li Zhao, *The Rockefeller University*

**Abstract:** Proteins functions through dynamic and evolutionarily constrained interaction networks, yet the molecular basis of many protein–protein interactions (PPIs) remain poorly characterized. Sequence-based structural models such as ESMFold and AlphaFold2-Multimer leverage evolutionary signals in protein sequences to infer structural and functional interactions from mechanistic to network scales. Here, we apply AlphaFold2 Multimer to systematically predict 30,000 PPIs in Drosophila by integrating physical interaction datasets with functional association networks that reflect shared evolutionary and biological constraints. We find that incorporating functional associations substantially improves the confidence, coverage, and interpretability of predicted protein complexes. Structural analyses further reveal that rapidly evolving and weakly conserved intrinsically disordered regions play a pervasive role in mediating high-confidence interactions across the network, highlighting how functional constraints can preserve interaction capacity despite sequence divergence.<br /><br />Beyond large-scale network inference, we show that these models can illuminate the evolutionary origins of specific molecular interactions. Using ancestral sequence reconstruction together with AlphaFold2 and molecular dynamics simulations, we demonstrate that a recently evolved ligand–receptor interaction, the Drosophila sex peptide–sex peptide receptor pair, can be structurally supported by ancestral receptor states predating the ligand’s emergence. These analyses reveal that only a small number of amino acid changes are sufficient to enable novel interactions and that new ligand specificity can arise through the co-option of preexisting interaction interfaces. Together, our results demonstrate how deep learning models can bridge interaction networks to individual binding interfaces, providing mechanistic insight into how evolutionary forces shape protein interactions and cellular function.<br />

## From Sequence to Expression Across Evolution: Promise and Limits of AI Genome Models

**Presenter:** Maria Chikina, *University of Pittsburgh at Pittsburgh*

**Abstract:** Deep sequence-to-function models are rapidly transforming our ability to predict regulatory phenotypes directly from genome sequence. With the parallel emergence of highly predictive foundation models and high-quality, contiguous vertebrate genomes, a central question comes into focus: beyond coding sequence, can genome-wide regulatory output -gene expression- be broadly inferred across mammalian and vertebrate lineages to enable comparative evolutionary studies?<br />We address this question by evaluating AlphaGenome in an end-to-end pipeline across five additional vertebrates (rhesus macaque, rabbit, rat, opossum, and chicken), spanning substantial evolutionary distances. For each species, rich gene expression compendia across diverse tissues and developmental stages enable context-aware evaluation across ~20,000 protein-coding genes, directly probing both phylogenetic generalization and tissue specificity. <br />AlphaGenome recovers broad expression landscapes across taxa, achieving robust correlations even in distant lineages, with the lowest performance observed in chicken brain (Pearson ≈ 0.6 averaged across developmental stages). Analysis across six tissues (brain, heart, kidney, liver, ovary, and testis) shows that performance is driven primarily by global expression structure, while tissue-specific regulatory effects are captured more weakly.<br />Together, these results highlight both the promise and shortcomings of AI-driven genome interpretation. While sequence models can be adapted to non-model organisms to recover coarse expression patterns, they remain limited in capturing context-specific regulation. Our findings motivate a shift from accuracy benchmarks toward evolutionary stress tests that aim to disentangle model limitations from genuine regulatory divergence, clarifying which failures reflect incomplete learning and which arise from lineage-specific regulatory evolution.

## Nonlinearity in Complex Trait Genomics: Limits of Linear GWAS and Interpretable Deep Learning for Locus Discovery

**Presenter:** Burak Yelmen, *University of Tartu*

**Abstract:** Conventional genome-wide association studies (GWAS) typically rely on additivity and linearity assumptions. These assumptions can be problematic for polygenic traits shaped by complex evolutionary forces, which can give rise to genetic architectures with multiple interacting components. In this series of studies, we first derive analytically how unmodeled genotype–genotype interactions can bias GWAS test statistics. Using phenotype simulations based on Estonian Biobank genotypes, we validate the mathematical model and show that conventional linear models can yield biased test statistics and false-positive associations, especially in large-scale GWAS.<br /><br />Motivated by these limitations, we present an artificial neural network (ANN)–based alternative to linear GWAS to model genotype–phenotype associations without linearity assumptions. We train ANNs to predict complex traits from genotype data and introduce a model-agnostic framework to compute feature importance scores and identify potentially associated loci (PAL). Across simulations spanning multiple parameter settings, we show that PAL can be detected with good precision when stringent selection criteria are used. Applying this framework to the schizophrenia cohort in the Estonian Biobank, we identify multiple loci that overlap with previously reported loci for schizophrenia and bipolar disorder. We further perform enrichment analyses which demonstrate that genes mapped to PAL predominantly highlight terms related to brain morphology and function.<br /><br />With advancements in model optimization and uncertainty quantification, ANN-based frameworks have the potential to enhance GWAS by improving locus discovery under non-additive genetic architectures and by serving as initial screening tools for downstream functional studies.

## A new genomic foundation model and a sequence-only gene prediction model for plant evolutionary biology

**Presenter:** Matt Pennell, *Cornell University*

**Abstract:** Functional interpretation remains a fundamental challenge in plant biology and genomic foundation models are a promising technology for overcoming this. In this talk, I will present PlantCAD2, a long-context DNA foundation model pre-trained on 65 angiosperm genomes. PlantCAD2 efficiently captures evolutionary conservation and regulatory architecture with 676 million parameters, outperforming substantially larger models in zero-shot tasks and achieving state-of-the-art performance in predicting chromatin accessibility and gene expression across diverse species. Along with the model, we developed a new suite of cross-species benchmarks for evaluating genomic foundation models in plant evolutionary biology and agricultural applications. Building on PlantCAD2, we developed GeneCAD, a new gene prediction approach that predicts complete gene models directly from DNA without requiring species-matched transcriptomes or proteomes, improving transcript-level accuracy by 8–10% on average over current methods across phylogenetically diverse angiosperms, including polyploids. I will then illustrate the power of this method by introducing a phylogenetic comparative study of genome content and structure across angiosperms. Beyond these applications, I will close by discussing the ways in which genomic foundation models mirror traditional phylogenetic comparative methods. I will show how this connection can improve foundation model training efficiency and provide new insights into how population genetic processes shape interspecific divergence.

## Functional impact prediction for single nucleotide variants in Drosophila melanogaster with FlyCADD

**Presenter:** Katja M Hoedjes, *Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam*

**Abstract:** A wealth of genomic, evolutionary, and functional information, including high-quality reference genomes, dense population genomic data, and detailed functional annotations is the result of extensive study over decades of model organisms, such as Drosophila melanogaster. Despite this rich knowledge base, understanding how individual genetic variants, particularly single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), contribute to phenotypic differences remains a major challenge. While SNPs play critical roles in complex, polygenic traits, their functional effects are often difficult to infer from traditional gene-level analyses alone.<br /><br />Here, we present FlyCADD, a machine-learning-based functional impact prediction framework for single nucleotide variants in Drosophila melanogaster. Based on the established Combined Annotation-Dependent Depletion (CADD) approach, FlyCADD integrates over 650 genomic and evolutionary features at nucleotide resolution. This information, encompassing conservation scores, regulatory elements, GC content, and DNA secondary structure, is combined into a single metric that reflects the predicted impact of a variant on evolutionary fitness. FlyCADD provides genome-wide impact scores for all possible single nucleotide variants in D. melanogaster.<br /><br />We demonstrate how FlyCADD can be used to prioritize phenotype-associated variants, evaluate naturally occurring polymorphisms in population genomic data, and guide experimental design for CRISPR–Cas9 genome editing. The development of a CADD framework for Drosophila melanogaster is particularly impactful because it unites impact prediction with a well-established functional genetics toolbox. The combination of FlyCADD with extensive resources and precise genome editing in Drosophila provides a powerful platform for functional genetics and evolutionary genomics, advancing our ability to link genomic variation at single nucleotide resolution to phenotypic outcomes.<br /><br />

## Comparing archaic and modern human genomes using deleteriousness predictions from a DNA language model

**Presenter:** Danat Yermakovich, *Institute of Genomics, University Of Tartu*

**Abstract:** For more than 200,000 years, archaic and modern humans inhabited different regions of the world, evolving in unique ways that led to the emergence of distinct biological traits. The genetic basis of these traits can be studied using the genomes of these humans. However, most of our knowledge about the phenotypic consequences of genetic variants is derived from studies of present-day human variation, which limits and biases comparisons between modern and archaic humans towards modern human biology.<br />In this study, we aim to identify biological differences between modern and archaic humans based on differences in deleteriousness using variant effect predictions from a DNA language model (GPN-MSA), trained without accounting for present-day human genetic variation. Leveraging the predictions, we found that genome-wide levels of deleteriousness of derived variants differed between modern and archaic human genomes, being more deleterious in archaic humans, and the difference among them was partly linked to the proportion of homozygosity and age. Regions with large differences in deleteriousness showed an enrichment for keratin filaments. Such regions also overlapped genomic segments devoid of archaic introgression in modern humans, including genes such as FOXP2, IMMP2L and PRR20. Furthermore, when comparing archaic introgressed haplotypes to their non-introgressed counterparts among present-day humans, we found that introgressed DNA has a higher level of deleteriousness, consistent with the general pattern between archaic and modern humans. Overall, our study highlights the potential of large-scale neural network models as another promising source of information for studying human evolutionary history.<br />

## The Evolutionary Ecology of Color Vision in Krill: Comparative Genomics, Machine Learning and Analysis of Standing Genetic Variation in Opsins

**Presenter:** Andreas Wallberg, *Uppsala University*

**Abstract:** Do species exposed to similar light environments evolve comparable genetic solutions, and can machine learning (ML) help us understand how adaptive traits evolve? We address these questions through a comparative analysis of opsin genes in krill, a keystone group of marine macrozooplankton. Opsins mediate visual light sensitivity and frequently show adaptive tuning to environmental light conditions.<br /><br />Building on the recent genome assembly of the Northern krill, which revealed a markedly expanded opsin repertoire and adaptive divergence in opsin alleles between populations, we extend analyses across more than 30 ecologically diverse krill species. Using transcriptomic data, we identified over 200 krill opsins and predicted their spectral sensitivity (λmax) with OPTICS, a machine-learning framework trained on the Visual Physiology Opsin Database (VPOD). OPTICS integrates multiple regression-based ML models to infer phenotypes from protein sequences while capturing epistasis and identifying key amino acid tuning sites.<br /><br />Our results show that krill possess unusually large and diverse opsin repertoires, with parallel and pronounced expansions of blue-sensitive opsins in polar species, likely enhancing vision in seasonal and dim deep-water environments. Gene duplication and spectral tuning appear to have been major drivers of opsin evolution in krill. Lastly, we apply the framework to assess how much of the standing genetic variation in populations may significantly impact opsin function and have the potential to be adaptive under ocean change.<br /><br />Overall, this study demonstrates how ML-enabled phenotype prediction can uncover repeatable molecular evolution and provides a generalizable framework for studying sensory adaptation and adaptive potential in non-model organisms.
