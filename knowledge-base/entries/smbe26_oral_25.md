## Diversity of recombination landscapes across taxa: from genetic control to genomic architecture

**Presenter:** Aurora Ruiz-Herrera, *Universitat Autonoma de Barcelona (UAB)*

**Abstract:** Meiotic recombination is a fundamental process for sexual reproduction and a major source of genetic diversity. Understanding the mechanisms by which genetic variation is generated and maintained across phylogenetic groups is central to biodiversity and evolution. In this talk, I will synthesize key aspects of meiotic recombination, from chromosome organization to the molecular and evolutionary forces shaping recombination landscapes. I will present an integrated view of genome architecture and the mechanisms underlying the establishment, regulation, and genomic distribution of recombination hotspots, with particular emphasis on the factors that govern the formation and repair of the large number of programmed DNA double-strand breaks generated during early meiosis. In parallel, I will discuss the genetic, mechanistic, and evolutionary determinants of recombination landscapes across taxa, illustrating how multiple layers of regulation converge to shape crossover patterns. These layers include selective forces acting on DNA sequence composition, modulated by genome reshuffling and lineage-specific evolutionary histories, as well as constraints imposed by higher-order chromosome organization during meiosis. Together, these factors highlight meiotic recombination as a dynamic and versatile toolbox driving genome evolution.

## Rapidly Evolving Epigenetic Controlling Factors of Meiotic Recombination

**Presenter:** Linda Odenthal-Hesse, *Max Planck Institute For Evolutionary Biology*

**Abstract:** Meiotic recombination is essential for gamete formation, with errors leading to miscarriage and infertility. In most vertebrates, recombination is non-random and specified by the rapidly evolving histone methyltransferase PRDM9, which marks chromatin accessibility and defines recombination hotspots. Here, we present a second rapidly evolving epigenetic control system that acts with PRDM9 to regulate meiotic recombination and fertility.<br />By isolating Prdm9 alleles from hundreds of wild mouse samples worldwide, we identified over 40 novel alleles. We integrated alleles reported in GenBank and characterized more than 250 distinct Prdm9 alleles using phylogenetic reconstruction methods tailored to the Prdm9 coding minisatellite. We observed lineage-specific evolutionary dynamics across mice.<br />Prdm9 is also a hybrid sterility gene that acts together with the Hstx2 locus. In collaboration with the Forejt laboratory, we resolved the Hstx2 locus and revealed a microRNA-mediated pachytene checkpoint in which copy-number variation modulates fertility and genome-wide recombination rates. Comparative genomics across sterile and fertile mouse strains has now revealed those mature miRNAs that are functionally divergent between the sterility and fertility alleles of Hstx2. Targets were in silico predicted and validated by differential expression levels in testicular cells of sterile and fertile mice.<br />Finally, we show that Prdm9 and Hstx2 alleles both affect crossover frequency and fine-scale recombination resolution across intersubspecific and intrasubspecific hotspots in fertile mice. We tested three hotspots activated by different Prdm9 alleles, including a human transgene. Together, our results link these two rapidly evolving loci to their roles in recombination, reproductive isolation, and infertility.

## Tuning meiosis in different habitats: lessons from Arabidopsis arenosa

**Presenter:** Marinela Dukic, *ETH Zürich*

**Abstract:** Meiotic crossover recombination exchanges genetic information between homologous chromosomes and is essential for meiotic stability and evolution. Crossover number and location influence chromosome segregation accuracy, which is critical for individual fertility. At the population level, crossovers promote genetic diversity and adaptive potential in challenging environments. Although tightly regulated and broadly conserved, crossover rates vary across taxonomic levels and environmental conditions, but the evolutionary significance and molecular mechanisms of this variation remain largely unknown.<br />We investigated temperature-induced crossover rate variation in two genetic lineages of Arabidopsis arenosa from thermally distinct habitats. Crossover response curves across 14-34°C differed between populations, with the gradient of responses reflecting their geography. Cytological analysis revealed that the population from the warmer habitat exhibits more distally positioned crossovers, which correlated with reduced chromosome missegregation at high temperatures. Complementary fertility assays following heat treatment provided additional insight into the relationship between crossover recombination, meiotic stability, and fitness.<br />Furthermore, we used a genome-scan approach to identify genetic variation and candidate genes that may be governing the observed differences in recombination plasticity and meiotic stability between these two lineages of A. arenosa. This combination of approaches provides a unique opportunity to address the adaptive value of preferential crossover positioning, contributing to the debate about the nature of selection that shapes the variation in crossover patterning. We are continuing to investigate mechanisms by which environmental challenges may affect crossover distribution and meiotic stability, and how meiotic processes evolve as organisms adapt to different habitats.<br />

## Self-fertilization can reverse the selection coefficient on a recombination landscape modifier during experimental evolution

**Presenter:** Tom Parée, *New York University*

**Abstract:** Recombination rates are well-known to correlate with self-fertilization rates in plants. Theoretical work has highlighted mechanisms predicting increased selection on recombination modifiers under higher self-fertilization rates. However, these models assume uniform recombination landscapes, whereas in nature they are highly heterogeneous. Here, we investigate the evolution of recombination landscapes under varying self-fertilization rates using experimental evolution in Caenorhabditis elegans populations segregating a rec-1 polymorphism. Mutation at the rec-1 gene homogenizes the otherwise heterogeneous C. elegans’ recombination landscape. Strikingly, predominant self-fertilization not only increases the strength of selection acting on the rec-1 modifier, but also reverse its direction. Simulations mirror the experimental results and show that they can arise from the persistence of associations between the modifier and the genetic combinations it generates at distant loci. Under outcrossing, the modifier is only briefly associated with most genetic combinations it creates; only locally generated combinations are in sufficiently strong linkage to allow indirect selection of the modifier through hitchhiking. Conversely, increased linkage under predominant self-fertilization enables the modifier to form persistent associations with genetic combinations across the genome. When a mismatch exists between local and genome-wide effects of a modifier on selection efficiency—as is the case for rec-1—a reversal of selection under predominant selfing becomes possible. This mechanism should apply to trans-acting modifiers but not cis-acting ones, which lack genome-wide effects. By distinguishing between these two classes, we present a model that explains the evolution of increased recombination in self-fertilizing species and predicts differences in recombination architecture compared to outcrossing relatives

## Heterogeneous evolution of effective recombination landscapes across percomorph fishes

**Presenter:** Marion Talbi, *Institute of Ecology and Evolution, University of Bern, Switzerland*

**Abstract:** Recombination shapes how alleles are combined across the genome and plays a central role in adaptation and speciation. While recombination rates are known to vary and evolve, how recombination landscapes diverge through time and which processes drive this divergence remain poorly understood. Here, we use the Population Recombination Divergence Index (PRDI), an approach that controls for key confounders of LD-based inference, to quantify divergence in realized recombination landscapes in seven population comparisons across four percomorph fish lineages (cichlids, sticklebacks, killifish, and clownfish). We find that recombination landscapes evolve rapidly across this broad taxonomic set. Across comparisons, recombination landscape divergence generally increases with genetic differentiation and divergence time, but the rate at which divergence accumulates varies markedly among population pairs. To interpret this heterogeneity, we propose a conceptual framework in which divergent selection and gene flow interact to shape recombination landscape divergence. Although our dataset is limited, we observe a positive association between gene flow and the relative increase in recombination divergence per unit of genetic differentiation. Finally, we introduce a local version of PRDI (l_PRDI) to characterize genomic heterogeneity in recombination divergence, to examine how it relates to genomic differentiation, and how this relationship is modulated by gene flow.  Together, our work provides new insight into the dynamics of recombination landscape evolution, highlights the importance of recombination divergence heterogeneity, and motivates theoretical and empirical efforts to integrate evolution of recombination into studies of adaptation and speciation.

## A holocentric pangenome links karyotype evolution to meiotic recombination

**Presenter:** André Marques, *Max Planck Institute For Plant Breeding Research*

**Abstract:** Chromosomal fissions, fusions and whole-genome duplications propel genome evolution, yet their impact on meiotic recombination is obscured by the centromere constraint, since in monocentric species most large rearrangements are lethal. Holocentric organisms, which distribute kinetochore activity along the entire chromosome, overcome this barrier and therefore offer a unique window onto the interplay between karyotype change and crossover control. We assembled chromosome-scale genomes for 20 holocentric Rhynchospora species (including 56 haplotypes), representing all major clades of the genus, featuring satellite-based holocentromeres, and integrated single-gamete crossover maps, high-resolution meiotic synapsis immunocytochemistry and Hi-C chromatin architecture. Breakpoint analysis shows that holocentromeric Tyba satellite arrays are recurrent hotspots for both chromosome fusions and fissions, contributing to the genus’s extraordinary chromosome number variation from 2n = 4 to 36. Crossover landscapes group into two apparent modes: strongly distal-biased versus irregularly distributed, which is correlated with divergent patterns of synapsis elongation. Moreover, crossover number scales with chromosome count and meiotic axis length. In contrast, crossover density per megabase is inversely related to chromosome length and to chromatin-loop size. We propose that chromosome fissions create karyotypes with smaller chromosomes folded into shorter loops, thereby increasing the axial substrate accessible for double-strand break formation and elevating recombination frequency. Together, our results provide a structural link between large-scale structural chromosome evolution and meiotic recombination through coupled changes in chromosome number, size, loop geometry, and synapsis dynamics.

## Fine-scale mapping of meiotic recombination in Saccharomyces cerevisiae unveils shifts in genetic exchanges driven by DNA heterologies

**Presenter:** Irene Tiemann-Boege, *Johannes Kepler University*

**Abstract:** Meiotic recombination is a driver of genetic diversity, yet its fine-scale mapping remains technically challenging and is limited by marker density and availability of gametes. HapDS is a deep sequencing approach that reconstructs haplotypes from hundreds of thousands of short chromatid fragments while preserving their molecular identities. This large number of haplotypes (~300 base pairs long) enables the detection of rare recombinants and the accurate estimation of very low recombination frequencies. Using HapDS in Saccharomyces cerevisiae we mapped genetic exchanges at four or 16 different genomic regions each spanning 1.5 kilobases in a hybrid cross (SK1BY) and in a SK1 strain with multiple informative sites introduced by recombineering. We observed clear differences in recombination between sexual and asexual divisions and mapped recombination with resolution primarily determined by marker density rather than sequencing depth, albeit the short haplotype length precluded the distinction between crossovers and non-crossovers. Further, recombination activity closely matched programmed DNA double-strand break (DSB) sites at kilobase resolution. However, at the base-pair level, HapDS revealed sharp recombination peaks adjacent to gaps coinciding with DNA palindromes, hairpins, or large sequence heterologies. We hypothesize that these DNA sequence features are important modifiers of genetic exchanges influenced by alternative DSB repair in place at secondary DNA structures, which could also be a hub for de novo mutations.

## Loci under balancing selection can change the recombination landscape around them

**Presenter:** Lou Guyot, *University Paris-saclay*

**Abstract:** Loci under strong balancing selection, such as sex-determining, mating-type, and self-incompatibility loci, are frequently flanked by regions of suppressed recombination. The reasons why recombination suppression evolves around these loci, thus changing the recombination landscape, remain poorly understood. Here, we propose that one reason may be that loci under balancing selection facilitate the emergence of pseudo-overdominance, a phenomenon under which linked recessive deleterious mutations in repulsion mimic overdominance. Pseudo-overdominance arises when linkage disequilibrium gradually builds up between recessive deleterious mutations, often due to strong genetic linkage and genetic drift. Once complementary haplotypes form, homozygous and recombinant offspring are selected against because they carry homozygous recessive deleterious mutations. The observed recombination patterns in genomes then mimic recombination suppression. Using individual-based simulations, we demonstrate here that the presence of loci under balancing selection eases the establishment of pseudo-overdominance, by facilitating the maintenance of recessive deleterious mutations and linkage disequilibrium in their flanking regions. We further show that, under some conditions, such resulting pseudo-overdominant zones can ultimately lead to selection for genuine crossing-over suppression around permanently heterozygous loci, for example by selection for inversions, preventing the creation of unfit homozygous recombinant offspring. These results suggest new avenues for understanding the evolution of recombination landscapes around loci under balancing selection, in sex chromosomes and supergenes.

## Turtles reveal novel insights into PRDM9’s role in recombination rate landscape evolution

**Presenter:** Laurie Stevison, *Auburn University*

**Abstract:** Functional PRDM9 drives rapid recombination evolution across vertebrates, while its absence, as shown in yeast, birds and dogs, slows evolution. This reduced turnover is due to ‘default’ hotspots near gene promoters, which are more stable over time. Recent work in snakes challenged this dichotomous framework that intact PRDM9 recruits hotspots away from promoters. This duality is also prevalent across mammals, but functional PRDM9 still directs the majority of hotspots. Here, we constructed a novel LD-based fine-scale recombination map in the diamond-backed terrapin. Similar to snakes, we uncovered more balanced hotspot usage between PRDM9 and default mechanisms in turtles, with default hotspots more common on microchromosomes and PRDM9 hotspots more common on macrochromosomes. Our results suggest this balanced hotspot usage is shared across reptiles, having evolved at least ~250-300 mya. We complement our recombination analysis with a PRDM9 survey targeting reptiles, specifically squamates and testudines, and identify at least 5 novel losses. Our findings increase the number of independent losses across vertebrates by 50%, and suggest these clades are particularly prone to loss, including several quite recent. When combined with documented PRDM9 loss in Archosauria (birds, crocodiles) this suggests a possible connection between PRDM9 loss and its role in suppressing default hotspots. Finally, expanding genome coverage in underrepresented, species-rich clades, such as reptiles, enabled this work and promises further evolutionary insights. Future work on these and other clades could yield novel insights on the function of PRDM9, as well as its role in fine-tuning the evolutionary rate of the recombination landscape.
