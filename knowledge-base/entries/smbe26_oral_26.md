## Devils, Disease and Defence: the immune system strikes back

**Presenter:** Kathy Belov, The University Of Sydney

**Abstract:**

Tasmanian devils face one of the most extraordinary disease challenges in wildlife: a contagious cancer transmitted between individuals as living tissue. Devil facial tumour disease (DFTD) has caused severe population declines and provides a rare opportunity to observe rapid evolution in a natural system. Because tumour transmission depends on immune similarity between hosts, the devil major histocompatibility complex class I (MHCI) genes are a central battleground in this host–tumour conflict.<br />Using long‑read MHCI sequencing and immune response data from wild devils, we show that DFTD is reshaping the MHCI system in rapid and highly directional ways. Devils whose MHCI profiles are most dissimilar to the tumour are more likely to mount immune recognition, and these immune‑associated genotypes are increasing in frequency. Strikingly, a deletion that disables an entire MHCI gene (Saha UA) is rising in frequency across diseased populations, providing the first clear evidence in a wild vertebrate that an infectious disease can drive the loss of an MHC gene. <br />These findings challenge the long‑standing assumption that greater MHC diversity universally enhances disease resistance. Under the unusual selective pressures imposed by a transmissible cancer, dissimilarity to the tumour—rather than diversity per se—confers a fitness advantage. However, this adaptation may come at a cost: reduced MHCI diversity could limit the population’s ability to respond to future pathogens.<br />Together, our results reveal a rapidly moving evolutionary arms race between devils and their tumours and underscore the need to balance natural adaptation with conservation strategies that maintain long‑term immunogenetic resilience.<br />

## Understanding the evolutionary dynamics of host and pathogen through comparative transcriptomics

**Presenter:** Dafna Tussia-Cohen, Tel Aviv University

**Abstract:**

As natural reservoirs for emerging pathogens, bats represent a critical model for understanding host-pathogen co-evolution. Bats have adapted to pathogens through diverse mechanisms, including resistance—rapid pathogen elimination—and tolerance—limiting tissue damage following infection. In the Egyptian fruit bat (an important model in comparative immunology), several mechanisms conferring disease tolerance were discovered, but resistance mechanisms remain poorly understood. Previous studies suggested that elevated basal expression of innate immune genes may increase resistance. Here, we test whether such transcriptional patterns occur in Egyptian fruit bat tissues through single-cell and spatial transcriptomics of gut, lung, and blood cells, comparing gene expression between bat, mouse, and human. Despite numerous interferon loss and expansion events in the bat genome, interferon expression patterns are remarkably similar to mouse. In contrast, central complement system genes are highly and uniquely expressed in key regions of bat lung and gut epithelium, unlike in human and mouse. Interestingly, this unique expression in bat gut is strongest in the crypt, where developmental programs are highly conserved. Complement genes also evolve rapidly in their coding sequences across the bat lineage, and the bat complement system displays strong hemolytic activity. Together, these results indicate distinctive transcriptional divergence of the complement system, which may underpin bat resistance. Our findings demonstrate how comparative single-cell transcriptomics can reveal species-specific mechanisms of pathogen resistance and highlight the power of integrative methodologies to uncover how immune systems diversify under different evolutionary pressures—insights essential for predicting host responses to novel or shifting pathogen landscapes.

## Divergent cis-regulatory haplotypes at Tlr2 are associated with immune responsiveness

**Presenter:** Lars Råberg, Lund University

**Abstract:**

Positive and balancing selection on pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) is widely thought to target ligand-binding domains and affect the specificity of recognition of different pathogens. Well-established examples of selection for PRR specificity are, however, rare. Studies of a wild rodent (the bank vole, Clethrionomys glareolus) have shown that Tlr2—a lipoprotein-binding PRR—is highly polymorphic with divergent haplotypes and signatures of balancing selection, and that Tlr2 genotype is associated with susceptibility to Borrelia afzelii infection in the wild. To investigate if Tlr2 is an example of selection for ligand-binding specificity, we here perform integrated population genetic and functional analyses. Ex vivo infection experiments show that the protective Tlr2 haplotype produces a stronger proinflammatory response to B. afzelii compared to the haplotype associated with susceptibility. Tlr2 genotype has a similar, albeit not statistically significant, effect on responsiveness to the phylogenetically distant pathogen Streptococcus pyogenes. We find that the strongest signature of balancing selection is 4.6 kb upstream of the Tlr2 coding sequence, near a putative enhancer, and that Tlr2 exhibits allele-specific expression such that the protective haplotype is more expressed. Collectively these results indicate that balancing selection has primarily acted on cis-regulatory variation affecting the general responsiveness via Tlr2-signaling rather than on polymorphisms affecting Tlr2 ligand-binding specificity.

## Evolutionary diversification of horizontally acquired pathogen-defence genes in bdelloid rotifers

**Presenter:** Tim Barraclough, University Of Oxford

**Abstract:**

Most animals rely on sexual reproduction to generate genetic variability that helps them keep pace in arms races with virulent pathogens. Bdelloid rotifers are microscopic animals that lack observed males and would be theorised to be vulnerable to coevolving pathogens due to the absence (or extreme rarity) of sex. We recently discovered that bdelloids have acquired large numbers of biosynthetic genes by horizontal gene transfer from microbes, which they deploy to resist their specialist fungal pathogens (https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-49919-1). Highly unusual for animals, these non-ribosomal peptide and polyketide synthetase genes are predicted to produce antifungal compounds that substantially augment the normal repertoire of animal immune defences. However, one-off acquisition of such genes does not necessarily bring continued success against pathogens, since those pathogens may evolve resistance to bdelloids' novel antimicrobials. This talk illustrates how bdelloids generate variability in their unusual immune defences, using comparative genomics of closely related bdelloid species and time-series data from a wild bdelloid population sampled over ten years.

## VIEWING IMMUNITY IN THE CONTEXT OF AGEING AND EVOLUTION

**Presenter:** Imroze Khan, Ashoka University

**Abstract:**

Evolutionary theory predicts that the strength of natural selection wanes in late life and becomes negligible beyond the reproductive window. During this period, harmful mutations can accumulate, accelerating ageing, characterised by an overactive immune system that causes immunopathology, along with higher morbidity and death rates. The question is: why doesn’t natural selection eliminate these harmful age-specific maladaptive traits of immunity? To answer this question, we used experimentally evolving populations of Drosophila melanogaster with divergent reproductive schedules and ageing rates, exploring whether the rate of immune ageing coevolves with the rate of reproductive senescence. Does immune senescence appear earlier in early-reproducing and early ageing populations? Our findings support this prediction, showing that populations achieved their highest post-infection health near their respective reproductive peak, followed by a steady decline. In a following transcriptomics study, we found both qualitative and quantitative declines in immune function beyond reproductive ages. Maintaining optimal immune regulation is crucial for health after infection, but responses often become less specific with ageing, as shown by increased expression of Toll-pathway-induced antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) and melanisation cascade targeting Gram-negative bacteria such as Providencia rettgeri. Simultaneously, there is a decline in effectors like IMD-regulated AMPs, which previously provided specific protection against the pathogen at younger ages. This decline in immune efficiency may lead to higher mortality after infection in older flies. Our findings thus offer a rare evolutionary framework for understanding why immunity and post-infection health decline with age, paving the way for detailed molecular studies of the regulatory networks involved.

## Evolutionary Signatures of Host–Pathogen Interactions Revealed by Polygenic Adaptation in Ancient Genomes

**Presenter:** Eduardo Amorim, Arizona State University

**Abstract:**

Pathogens have been among the most persistent and powerful selective forces shaping human evolution, influencing immune diversity, physiology, and population-level responses to disease. Yet the genomic mechanisms by which host populations adapt to pathogen pressure, particularly through polygenic traits, remain incompletely understood. Here, we use ancient DNA (aDNA) data to investigate how past epidemics have reshaped human susceptibility and resistance to infectious diseases across millennia. Using GWAS results for over 1,000 immune- and infection-related traits, we calculated polygenic risk scores (PRS) for more than 3,000 publicly available ancient human genomes from Eurasia spanning prehistory to the historical era. This framework allowed us to track changes in immune-related polygenic traits through time. We test the hypothesis that major shifts in pathogen landscapes imposed selective pressure on standing genetic variation, leaving detectable signatures at the polygenic level in ancient genomes. We identify statistically significant shifts in PRS distributions associated with three major epidemiological transitions: the Antonine Plague, the Justinian Plague, and historical (putative) measles outbreaks. The implicated biological pathways extend beyond canonical immune genes and include lipid metabolism, megakaryocyte development, and hormonal signaling, highlighting the systemic nature of host defense and the infection response. Together, our results demonstrate how integrating aDNA data with polygenic frameworks can reveal evolutionary interactions between hosts and pathogens, providing insights into immune diversity and resilience in changing ecological and epidemiological contexts. We discuss these findings in light of methodological limitations, including the portability of PRS across time and populations.

## The evolutionary dynamics between viral mimics and host proteinss14

**Presenter:** Rotem Fuchs, Tel Aviv University

**Abstract:**

Viral proteins interact with host proteins to hijack cellular pathways important for viral replication. Viral mimics are proteins whose structural similarity to host-mimicked proteins allows them to interact with mutual host targets. This mimicry poses an evolutionary challenge for the host – how to avoid mimics without compromising essential interactions with host-mimicked proteins. Despite the prevalence of mimicry, the evolutionary dynamics between host and viral mimics remain largely unknown. We address this by integrating structural modelling, host-virus interaction networks and comprehensive evolutionary analyses of host and viral proteins. We show that host proteins targeted by both mimics and host-mimicked proteins are highly conserved, and that this is related to functional constraints imposed on host proteins. Host interface residues that interact with both mimics and host-mimicked proteins evolve slowly, while residues that exclusively interact with mimics evolve significantly faster. Surprisingly, viral mimics do not evolve rapidly, instead displaying complex evolutionary patterns. Our analysis reveals host’s limited capacity to escape mimicry and viral evolution to exploit this, and highlights how constraints lead to unexpectedly slow evolution of host-virus interaction networks.

## The population-level mutation dynamics of SARS-CoV-2 spike under immune selection

**Presenter:** Marina Escalera Zamudio, University College London

**Abstract:**

Recent advances in genomics, accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic, have enabled evolutionary analyses at unprecedented scale. In particular, the public availability of more than 15 million high-quality SARS-CoV-2 genomes provides a unique opportunity to explore how host-mediated immune selection has shaped viral evolution across time.<br /><br />We investigate the mutation dynamics of SARS-CoV-2 evolving under immune-driven host selection. Drawing from ~27,500 non-synonymous mutations on the spike protein detected between 2020 and 2025, we developed an analytical framework that combines large-scale viral genomics with structural biology and immunological annotations. We further use machine learning to assess the relative contributions of phenotypic and biophysical features to mutation “success” using allele-frequency trajectories as a proxy for fitness. Our lineage-agnostic approach applies a sliding temporal window to pinpoint mutation frequencies expected to impact B- and T-cell immunogenicity, whilst directly quantifying the biophysical trade-offs shaping mutation success.<br /><br />For B-cell epitope sites, mutations rising in frequency increasingly occur at immunodominant regions of spike, consistent with antigenic drift. We observe a non-monotonic time-dependent pattern, strongly pronounced during the emergence of new variants. However, many mutations fail to persist, reflecting the biophysical trade-offs constraining viable adaptive pathways. In contrast, time-resolved mutation mapping onto T-cell epitope sites shows apparent structure but no sustained enrichment beyond random expectations, indicating that virus evolution is unlikely to be driven by selective pressure exerted by T-cell immunity.<br /><br />Our results highlight how phenotypic and biophysical properties that shape viral evolution can be quantified, raising the possibility of forecasting mutation trajectories to anticipate immune escape.<br />

## Intraspecific diversity shapes functional variability of bats innate immunity

**Presenter:** Emeline Esnouf, MIVEGEC

**Abstract:**

Host-virus-environment interactions are critical drivers of immune evolution, yet their role in shaping intraspecific diversity in innate immunity remains poorly understood, particularly in reservoir hosts. Here, we investigated the contemporary evolution of innate immunity in six divergent bat species sampled across contrasted environments (metropolitan France, Gabon, French Guiana). By combining population-level sampling (n>15 /species), transcriptome-wide data, evolutionary analyses, and in vitro assays, we reveal, for the first time, pervasive intraspecific variation in key components of bat innate immunity.<br />Notably, two rapidly evolving and broad-spectrum antiviral effectors in bats, exhibit accumulation of nonsynonymous polymorphisms and indels that alter key domains for viral dsRNA recognition and enzymatic activity. Moreover, these changes map to positively selected coding sites targeted by viral antagonism. Importantly, we uncover marked contrasts between environments. In several tropical species, but not in metropolitan species, antiviral variants show significant signatures of balancing and positive selection, consistent with the adaptive maintenance of allelic diversity under heterogeneous and intense viral pressures. These patterns point to distinct selective regimes shaping immune evolution at the population level.<br />To assess the functional impact of this diversity, we are experimentally testing whether naturally occurring allelic variants differ in their antiviral activity during infection with bat-associated zoonotic viruses, including poxviruses, rotaviruses and Tonate virus. <br />Overall, our findings show that bat innate immune evolution is an ongoing, population-level process driven by contemporary viral pressures and environmental context, stressing the importance of population-based immunogenomics for understanding host-virus co-evolution and viral circulation in wildlife reservoirs.

## The specialist – generalist trade-off for infection ability of a parasite

**Presenter:** Dieter Ebert, University of Basel, Zoologie

**Abstract:**

In highly specific host–parasite systems, infection depends on parasite effector genes that match particular host genotypes. Although accumulating many effector variants should in principle allow parasites to infect many hosts, parasites rarely become broad generalists. We tested whether this limitation reflects a cost of generalism. Using the bacterium Pasteuria ramosa, a virulent parasite of Daphnia, we focused on parasite attachment, an essential first step of infection. Attachment is mediated by highly specific adhesion genes, which we identified by genome-wide association mapping. We predicted that parasites able to infect many host genotypes pay a cost in reduced attachment strength. Across parasite isolates, we measured adhesion strength and quantified both the number of host genotypes infected and the number of attachment sites bound. Isolates with strong adhesion were restricted to few host genotypes or few attachment sites, whereas isolates with weak adhesion infected more host genotypes and bound more sites. This pattern is consistent with a specialist–generalist trade-off. We further provide a molecular genetic model explaining how this trade-off arises. Together, our results offer a mechanistic explanation for why parasites do not evolve into unconstrained generalists.

## Integrating protein structure and population genomic data to detect diversifying selection related to immunity

**Presenter:** Leonie J. Lorenz, EMBL-EBI

**Abstract:**

As pathogens adapt to host immune systems, this can leave traces of diversifying selection in their genomes. Diversifying selection can be detected by calculating the ratio of nonsynonymous to synonymous substitutions, dN/dS. Classical methods for estimating dN/dS rely on phylogenetic trees, which limits scalability and can give misleading results for bacterial species as trees generally cannot be corrected to exclude recombination. We have developed a new tool called TOMBOMBADIL (Tree-free Omega Mapping By Observing Mutations of Bases and Amino acids Distributed Inside Loci), which computes dN/dS directly from codon counts from alignments by comparing to expected frequencies from the coalescent. We implemented TOMBOMBADIL as an end-to-end differentiable model in both Stan and JAX, allowing us to estimate dN/dS values across genes for alignments with potentially millions of samples. TOMBOMBADIL includes different models of evolution (NY98, GTR) and different assumptions of how codons evolve. We validated our method by comparing it to established, tree-based dN/dS methods and on well-known examples of bacterial genes under positive selection, including the outer membrane protein PorB of Neisseria meningitidis. We have added regression terms between protein features and selection, which will help map sites of positive selection to predictions of protein structures, leveraging the relationship of functional evolution and structure. We have analysed a large-scale dataset comprising bacterial outer membrane proteins, allowing us to study diversifying selection related to host-pathogen interactions. In summary, TOMBOMBADIL allows fast detection of positive selection in bacterial datasets using a tree-free dN/dS approach and machine learning methods.
