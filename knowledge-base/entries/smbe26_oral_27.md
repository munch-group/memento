## Evolutionary rescue under extreme selection: Linking adaptation and demography in agricultural weeds

**Presenter:** Julia Kreiner, University Of Chicago

**Abstract:**

Agricultural weeds provide natural experiments in evolutionary rescue, with populations persisting despite recurrent herbicide applications that impose near-lethal selection and drive the repeated evolution of resistance. Using temporal genomics in the common waterhemp (Amaranthus tuberculatus), we estimated extreme selection coefficients (0.05 < s < 0.3) on target-site resistance SNPs using ancient DNA from herbarium specimens dating back 70 years to the introduction of herbicides across North America. However, these long-term selection estimates aggregate dynamics across heterogeneous landscapes and decades of changing herbicide use. We are refining this resolution through exhaustive herbarium genomic sequencing to understand how evolutionary responses vary with herbicide use over the last 30 years.<br /><br />These historical patterns reveal strong selection and species-level expansion, but do populations actually experience the demographic bottlenecks and recovery characteristic of evolutionary rescue? To test this, we established on-field farmer-conducted evolve-and-resequence experiments with known herbicide use histories, sequencing populations monthly throughout the growing season. Genome-wide diversity patterns will reveal whether populations experience bottlenecks followed by recovery, and how these dynamics depend on herbicide use history and standing variation for resistance.<br /><br />Beyond demographic dynamics, long-read sequencing efforts are revealing hidden complexity in the genetic basis of resistance, including independent origins of high copy number extrachromosomal DNA cassettes and tandem gene amplifications that co-occur within single populations. By integrating selection estimates with demographic inference across temporal scales and resolving the genetic architecture of adaptation, this work characterizes the genomic signatures of evolutionary rescue under extreme selection.<br />

## Genomic and Phenotypic Differentiation of Cydia pomonella Populations Under Varied Pest Management Regimes in France

**Presenter:** Hyerin An, DGIMI, INRAE, University of Montpellier

**Abstract:**

Understanding pest adaptation to anthropogenic pressures is crucial when resistance enables population persistence under intensive management. In apple orchards, pheromone-based mating disruption (MD) has been widely adopted, releasing synthetic sex pheromones to prevent mate finding and reduce reproduction. However, declining MD efficacy has often led to increased chemical insecticide use, raising concerns about resistance evolution. Cydia pomonella, the codling moth, and a major apple pest, provides an ideal system to investigate the genetic basis of such unwanted evolutionary rescue. We first assessed phenotypic responses to MD. Flight tunnel experiments revealed altered orientation and landing behaviors in MD-exposed populations, while electroantennography showed significantly stronger antennal responses to synthetic pheromone, indicating changes in sensory perception and behavior that may facilitate persistence under pheromone saturation. To determine whether these phenotypic differences reflect underlying genetic divergence, we performed whole-genome resequencing of 95 individuals from three regions with contrasting management histories, revealing strong population structure with no admixture between distant populations and gene flow limited to neighboring populations. In the MD and insecticide–exposed population, three genomic regions exhibited reduced nucleotide diversity and elevated Fst and Dxy, consistent with high composite likelihood ratios from selective sweep analyses. Additional flight tunnel and Y-tube assays, GC–MS analyses of female pheromone blends, and transcriptomic comparisons of female pheromone glands and male antennae are ongoing. Together, these results suggest that resistance to MD and chemical insecticides likely involves polygenic adaptation and behavioral or regulatory plasticity, highlighting the complex genetic architecture underlying evolutionary rescue.

## Unwanted evolutionary rescue: bidirectional adaptive introgression of pesticide resistance genes between native and invasive Helicoverpa crop pests

**Presenter:** Henry North, University Of Cambridge

**Abstract:**

Human activity alters selection pressures and redistributes biodiversity, creating opportunities for hybridization through secondary contact. It is now well-established that ancient hybridization plays an important role in adaptive radiation, but its role in recent anthropogenic adaptation remains unclear. We investigated this in a study of hybridizing native and invasive Helicoverpa moths in Brazil: native H. zea, which has recently evolved Bt resistance and is better adapted to maize as a host plant, and H. armigera, which is resistant to pyrethroid pesticides and is better adapted to soybean as a host plant. Using a decade-long time series of 975 genomes from both species, we demonstrate rapid, bidirectional adaptive introgression of structural variants that cause resistance to two distinct pesticides. In one direction, a pyrethroid resistance gene from H. armigera (CYP337B3) is now almost fixed in native H. zea. This gene arose in H. armigera by a small deletion combining the exons of adjacent genes to form a de novo detoxification enzyme. In the other direction, ~30% of invasive H. armigera carry a H. zea-derived trypsin repeat cluster. Using controlled crosses, we show that this trypsin repeat cluster confers resistance to Bt (Cry1Ac) soy in the H. armigera genomic background. Thus, hybridization has combined H. armigera adaptation to soy with H. zea Cry1Ac resistance to facilitate exploitation of a vast anthropogenic landscape: Brazilian Bt soy monoculture, which exceeds the area of Germany in size. We therefore show that evolutionary rescue can drive rapid anthropogenic adaptation via combinatorial evolution.

## Convergent and divergent genetic routes to persistence under thermal stress in the yeast genus Saccharomyces

**Presenter:** Jennifer Molinet, Universidad Autónoma De Chile

**Abstract:**

Thermal adaptation is a central determinant of species distributions and evolutionary rescue under climate warming, yet the extent to which molecular responses are conserved across species remains unclear. Using the budding yeast genus Saccharomyces as a comparative evolutionary model, we combined long-term experimental evolution with whole-genome and functional analyses to dissect the phenotypic and genomic basis of adaptation to sustained temperature increases. We evolved populations of eight Saccharomyces species under constant and progressively increasing temperature regimes, exceeding ancestral thermal limits in several species, to assess their potential for evolutionary rescue under future warming. Thermal performance curves varied significantly among species, revealing two main adaptive trajectories with contrasting rescue outcomes. Warm-tolerant species evolved higher optimal growth temperatures and increased thermal tolerance, whereas cold-tolerant species evolved broader thermal breadth and higher upper thermal limits but exhibited reduced maximum performance. To identify the molecular basis of these responses, we performed whole-genome sequencing of 256 evolved genotypes. Across species, independent lineages repeatedly accumulated de novo mutations in conserved signaling pathways, particularly TORC1, PKA, and MAPK, revealing strong functional convergence at the pathway level. In contrast, species-specific fitness outcomes were associated with divergent regulatory rewiring, distinct gene expression responses, and frequent mitochondrial DNA loss in some lineages. Functional assays confirmed that mitochondrial state and signaling pathway modulation jointly shape thermal tolerance trajectories. Together, our results demonstrate that evolutionary rescue from sustained warming is driven by conserved molecular targets but constrained by lineage-specific physiological and mitochondrial differences, defining the limits of thermal adaptation across species.

## Cooperative interactions between measles viruses facilitate rapid dissemination throughout the brain

**Presenter:** Alexander Robertson, University Of Washington, Seattle

**Abstract:**

Measles virus (MeV) usually infects respiratory and immune cells, but intra-host evolution can enable viral brain spread, leading to fatal encephalitis. This neuron-to-neuron transmission requires mutations that destabilize the viral trimeric fusion protein. While single mutations can allow the virus to spread independently, clinical isolates can harbor cooperative populations. These groups are made up of some genotypes that produce stable proteins and others with highly destabilizing truncation mutations–both non-viable alone but their subunits can pair to form functional hybrid trimers. Given the availability of mutations that enable spread self sufficiently, why do cooperative collectives emerge? To investigate this phenomenon, we developed a computational model of MeV neuronal replication and spread, and show that these cooperative mutations provide a transient "heterozygote advantage". Because de novo mutants initially arise in cells alongside stable wildtype genomes, truncations can immediately form functional hybrids that facilitate faster initial spread than a new self-sufficient mutation. However, this strategy is vulnerable to stochastic bottlenecks during cell-to-cell transmission that can separate the two interdependent genotypes. We show that MeV transmitting on a network structure parameterized with human neuronal connectivity data can nevertheless spread robustly as cooperating genomes because such networks contain redundant pathways that buffer the population against local drift. This work provides new insight into viral adaptation to a new host environment, and reveals how transmission dynamics can impact viral heritability in a case where viruses blur the line between collective and individual fitness.

## Evolution of genetic variance and genetic architecture in the context of species’ range dynamics

**Presenter:** Jitka Polechova, University of Vienna

**Abstract:**

When does the evolution of genetic variance under joint spatial and temporal change prevent extinction during a rapid temporal change? When environments vary through both time and space, their effects on fitness and genetic variation interact, influencing adaptation rates and the probability of evolutionary rescue. In spatially structured populations, a substantial proportion of genetic variance can be maintained by gene flow. I show that spatial structure can substantially extend the timescales over which recurrent selective sweeps, driven by a temporally changing optimum, elevate local genetic variance -- thereby supporting adaptation to faster temporal change. Furthermore, joint spatiotemporal change alters the expected effect sizes of polymorphic loci: whereas in the absence of temporal change we expect most polymorphic loci to be of small effect, temporal change leads to an excess of alleles of moderate effect.

## Occurrence and potential of inter- and intraspecific gene flow in rapidly adapting butterfly populations

**Presenter:** Jana Olivia Weigel, Uppsala University

**Abstract:**

Human-driven climate change and habitat fragmentation increasingly threaten biodiversity by reducing population sizes and eroding genetic variation required for adaptation. These effects are particularly severe for locally adapted taxa, where limited standing variation constrains adaptive responses. Under such conditions, gene flow hinders local adaptation or provides<br />genetic variation to fuel evolutionary rescue.<br />A compelling system to investigate these dynamics is the butterfly Euphydryas editha, the first species documented to shift northwards in response to climate change, and one including<br />multiple subspecies of conservation concern despite stable species-wide abundance (Parmesan 1996; Edwards et al. 2025). E. editha exhibits rapid, heritable local adaptation to diverse host<br />plants and strong geographic structure shaped by sedentary behaviour, ecological specialisation, and episodic recolonisation of extinct populations.<br />This raises questions about the extent to which intraspecific gene flow among ecotypes, and introgression with closely related species, contribute to adaptive potential and population<br />persistence under environmental change. <br />Using >300 whole genomes, we investigate the genetic basis of potential evolutionary rescue in E. editha by integrating landscape genomics with species ecology. We ask (i) whether incorporating ecological constraints such as phenology and host-plant use improves inference of realistic barriers to gene flow; (ii) how ecotype-specific ecology and recolonisation dynamics shape rescue via natural range shifts; and (iii) whether adaptive introgression contributes to persistence and expansion into novel environments. <br />By explicitly accounting for ecological barriers and opportunities for gene flow, this work highlights how the genetic architecture of evolutionary rescue is shaped by population structure, hybridisation, and landscape context.

## The effects of environmental stress on the genetic architecture of fitness

**Presenter:** Max Reuter, University College London

**Abstract:**

Adaptation to stressful environmental conditions relies on the availability of suitable genetic variation. Interestingly, studies measuring variance in traits and fitness suggest that genetic variation is itself dependent on the environment, where stressful conditions can result in the release of ‘cryptic’ variation that is invisible under benign conditions. Being able to predict the ease and speed with which populations can adapt to a changing environment therefore requires an understanding of whether and how heritable variation in fitness changes with stress. Here, we address this question in highly replicated laboratory experiments in the fission yeast, Schizosaccharomyces pombe. Measuring growth under a range of conditions across a collection of mutation accumulation lines we show that genetic variance in growth rates varies with the level of stress. Genetic variation for relative fitness is highest at intermediate stress levels and reduced under both benign and extremely stressful conditions. But stress also affects stochastic variation in growth within strains and the way in which residual variance relates to strain-specific average growth rate: in more benign conditions, growth variation correlates positively with a strain's growth rate, while at high stress this relationship vanishes. Our result show that adaptive responses to moderate stress can be helped by the increased genetic variation and reduced stochasticity of growth, making selection more effective in increasing population productivity. Furthermore, they suggest a reinterpretation of cryptic genetic variation. Fitness variation under benign conditions is not the result of purifying selection, but rather due to inherently reduced mutational effects and/or increased mutational robustness.

## Evolutionary rescue via short-lived modifiers of genetic variation

**Presenter:** Alexander Longcamp, Mississippi State University

**Abstract:**

Evolutionary rescue depends on the availability of genetic variation. Empirical studies have shown that environmental change can promote the evolution of mechanisms that generate such variation, including increased recombination or mutation rates. To identify conditions under which this feedback facilitates rescue, we analyze a three-locus model consisting of two fitness loci and an unlinked modifier locus that alters either recombination or mutation rates. Using analytical approximations validated by stochastic simulations, we show that a rare modifier mutation—whether promoting recombination or mutation—can substantially enhance the probability of rescue. Our main finding is that this enhancement often occurs despite the modifier quickly assorting onto the wild type background and thus disappearing before the rescue process is complete. We consider modifiers arising from both standing genetic variation and de novo mutation, and we identify conditions under which recombination modifiers are more likely to promote rescue than mutation modifiers. Overall, our results suggest that short-lived changes in the genetic architecture of populations can play a decisive role in enabling evolutionary rescue.
