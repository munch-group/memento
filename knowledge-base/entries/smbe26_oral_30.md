## Fatal entanglement: how a DNA satellite causes reproductive isolation in Drosophila

**Presenter:** Mia Levine, University Of Pennsylvania

**Abstract:**

A fundamental driver of biological diversification is the evolution of reproductive barriers between species. Instability and mis-regulation of repetitive DNA underlie numerous post-zygotic reproductive barriers, yet the molecular mechanisms are unknown. A long-studied genetic incompatibility between Drosophila melanogaster and D. simulans arises from mis-segregation of the D. melanogaster-specific 359bp DNA satellite in hybrid embryos. Here we report that the D. simulans version of the essential enzyme Topoisomerase II/Top2 causes this lethal incompatibility. Combining interspecies gene swaps with cell biology and genetics revealed that D. simulans-specific adaptive divergence of Top2 DNA-interacting domains prevents the resolution of 359bp-induced topological stress. Our findings demonstrate that species-specific DNA satellite topology requires species-specific molecular machinery and that even vital housekeeping genes can underlie reproductive isolation between closely related species.

## The tdk family: an evolutionarily persistent single-gene killer meiotic driver family employing a toxin–antidote strategy

**Presenter:** Yu Hua, Zhejiang University-University of Edinburgh Institute, Zhejiang University

**Abstract:**

Killer meiotic drivers (KMDs) are selfish genetic elements that bias their transmission by eliminating meiotic products lacking the driver element in driver+/driver− heterozygotes. Though predicted to arise recurrently, KMDs are often considered evolutionarily ephemeral—going extinct after fixation or host suppression. We recently identified tdk1 in Schizosaccharomyces pombe as a single-gene KMD. Its protein product forms a toxin–antidote pair via structural duality: a toxic conformation forming supramolecular assemblies that induce abnormal chromosome adhesion by interacting with the histone reader Bdf1, and an antidote conformation that neutralizes the toxin by dissolving the toxic supramolecular assemblies. The toxin forms in all meiotic products (spores), whereas the antidote is produced only in carriers upon spore germination, leading to chromosome segregation defects exclusively in noncarriers. We now report two functional homologs (tdk210 and tdk203) from Schizosaccharomyces cryophilus, a fission yeast species that diverged from S. pombe ~100 million years ago. Despite low sequence identity (<30% with Tdk1), all three KMDs disrupt chromosome segregation in noncarriers post-germination. Notably, they are mutually incompatible (showing no cross-resistance), and tdk210 and tdk203 act independently of Bdf1. This demonstrates that a single KMD family can achieve deep evolutionary persistence through repeated functional innovation—generating mutually incompatible variants and rewiring host dependencies—while retaining a conserved killing mode. Our findings challenge the conventional view of KMDs as evolutionarily transient elements and elucidate how KMDs sustain long-term evolutionary success through functional diversification.

## Dissecting the genetic basis of the Paris SR meiotic drive system in Drosophila simulans

**Presenter:** Cécile Courret, Cnrs

**Abstract:**

In Drosophila simulans, X-linked meiotic drivers disrupt Y-chromosome segregation during male meiosis, producing a strongly female-biased sex ratio. This segregation distortion is caused by two X-linked elements acting together. The first is a dysfunctional allele of HP1D2, a young and rapidly evolving member of the heterochromatin protein 1 family. HP1D2 originated from a duplication of HP1D/Rhino and accumulates on the heterochromatic Y chromosome in spermatogonia. The second driver maps within a tandem duplication (DPSR) encompassing six genes and a junction region. Preliminary evidence indicates that the driver embedded within DPSR corresponds to Trf2. Trf2 produces two main protein isoforms: a long and a short form. Within the DPSR, the distal copy (Trf2d) appears wild type, while the proximal copy (Trf2p) is 5′-truncated and may therefore encode a truncated version of the long isoform. We designed four shRNAs to target both Trf2 copies and their isoforms. Preliminary RNAi experiments show that knockdown of the truncated long isoform from Trf2p restores a balanced sex ratio, supporting the hypothesis that Trf2p is the DPSR-embedded driver. We propose that Trf2p produces a dominant-negative Trf2 isoform that competes with the functional Trf2d product. Notably, in D. melanogaster, Trf2 interacts with Rhino to regulate transposable elements through the transcription of heterochromatic small RNA source loci. This suggests that HP1D2SR, together with Trf2p, mis-regulate Y heterochromatin, thereby causing Y nondisjunction during meiosis II. The Paris SR system constitutes an ideal model to investigate the regulation of heterochromatin during spermatogenesis and how its mis-regulation leads to meiotic failure.

## To purge or not to purge? How mating systems shape transposable element dynamics in Mimulus (monkeyflowers)

**Presenter:** Eleanore Ritter, University Of Georgia

**Abstract:**

The shift to self-fertilization (selfing) is one of the most common evolutionary transitions in plants and results in dramatic genomic consequences. Owing to reduced effective population size, selfing species often have increased homozygosity, reduced genetic diversity, and more deleterious mutations. However, empirical evidence of the impact of selfing on transposable element (TE) evolution is mixed, and evolutionary theory is similarly conflicted. While selfing lineages are expected to accumulate more deleterious mutations, such as new TE insertions, due to the reduced efficacy of selection, they are also theorized to purge highly deleterious TEs. Here, we investigated how transitions to self-fertilization impact TE dynamics in the genus Mimulus (monkeyflowers) using comparative genomics and pangenome TE annotations for three pairs of closely related outcrossing and selfing species representing three independent transitions to selfing. We have found that the selfing species differ in the distribution of TEs within their genome and accumulate fewer young TEs compared to their outcrossing relatives. Characterizing intra- and interspecific TE presence/absence variation has further clarified the evolutionary processes driving these dynamics in selfing monkeyflowers. Ultimately, this work provides powerful insights into the fate of TEs in selfing lineages, helping to reconcile the conflicting patterns seen in previous empirical studies and predicted by evolutionary theory. It also illuminates the evolutionary mechanisms that may be shaping the fate of TEs in selfing lineages.

## An ancient allele directs epigenetic suppression of a selfish reproductive barrier in maize

**Presenter:** Elli Cryan, University Of California, Irvine

**Abstract:**

In maize and related plants, a locus called Gametophytic factor1 (Ga1) controls a reproductive barrier that can selfishly prevent ga1 pollen from fertilizing Ga1 zygotes. This selfish element could in theory sweep through plant populations to reach fixation. At fixation the reproductive barrier separating the population from related plants might be reinforced and contribute to speciation. However, the selfish Ga1 allele does not sweep. We find that in over thirty maize genome assemblies the most common allele at the Ga1 locus is actually a ga1 allele that is unable to make or overcome the reproductive barrier. This conserved ga1 allele (ga1-Off), is a specific haplotype containing three full-length gene models that are non-coding due to conserved stop codons. Each non-functional gene model within the ga1-Off allele is between 610 thousand and 1.5 million years old. We show that in developing pollen tissues the ga1-Off allele likely generates 24-nt siRNAs associated with RNA-directed DNA methylation, and these siRNAs map to functional Ga1 alleles. In crossing experiments, the ga1-Off allele was associated with reduced strength of the typically dominant functional Ga1 alleles encoding the reproductive barrier. An allelic interaction between the ga1-Off allele and the Ga1 allele seems to lead to epigenetic suppression of the Ga1 allele across multiple generations. Taken together, this system serves as an example of epigenetic repression of a selfish element that might otherwise contribute to speciation.<br />

## A large selfish mitochondrial deletion hints at an unexpected diversity of drive strategies

**Presenter:** Alexandre Schifano, Uppsala University

**Abstract:**

Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) molecules are found in multiple copies, replicate and divide independently from nuclear DNA and are transmitted (both between cells and individuals) through seemingly random bottlenecks. Such specificities allow competition to occur between mtDNA molecules within and between cells. This creates opportunities for intra-individual selection to favour mtDNA molecules with a replicative advantage, even at the expense of the individual, rendering certain mitochondrial variants selfish. In Caenorhabditis elegans, several deletions have been found to selfishly impair individual fitness while gaining a replicative or transmission advantage. Most of the effort in unravelling the dynamics of the proliferation of selfish mtDNA in C. elegans has focused on the uadf5 mitochondrial deletion. Here we focus our efforts on a 499 bp deletion in the ctb-1 gene (Δctb-1). The Δctb-1 mitotype was found to impose extensive fitness costs and persist within large populations of deletion-bearing individuals. Comparisons of the transcriptome of Δctb-1 bearing worms at two life stages and in relation to wild-type worms show differences in OXPHOS gene expression and mitochondrial housekeeping pathways as a consequence of the deletion. Additionally, we explore the effects of an RNAi treatment suppressing UPRmt expression across 15 generations at two different population sizes (N=1 and N>1000), allowing us to differentiate the roles of intra- and inter-individual selection on the progression of the variant as well as the interaction between natural selection and RNAi knockdown. Cumulatively, those results show how selfish mtDNA deletions can both vary in their consequences and in the source of their proliferation.

## Nondisjunction and fitness costs in a selfish X chromosome that causes meiotic drive in both sexes in Drosophila testacea

**Presenter:** Steve Perlman, University Of Victoria

**Abstract:**

Meiotic drivers are selfish genetic elements that manipulate the process of meiosis to increase their transmission. Because meiosis fundamentally differs between the sexes, meiotic drivers use different mechanisms and strategies in male and female meiosis. We recently discovered a selfish X chromosome that causes meiotic drive in both males and females in the woodland fly, Drosophila testacea. In this study, we examine segregation distortion through females. We used controlled crosses to ask whether one or two copies of this selfish X chromosome cause higher rates of nondisjunction, or chromosome segregation failure, in female flies. We found that females carrying a selfish X chromosome failed to transmit either of their X chromosomes at a significantly greater rate than wildtype females; this was even more pronounced in females with two selfish X chromosomes, resulting in a large number of sterile sons with an XO genotype. On the other hand, females carrying two selfish X chromosomes almost never transmitted both of their X chromosomes (which would produce XXY offspring). These results suggest that in D. testacea females that are homozygous for the selfish X chromosome, X chromosomes interfere with each other over segregation. Our results show that female meiotic drive is associated with nondisjunction, resulting in negative fitness consequences, and provides insights into the transmission of this unusual selfish chromosome.

## Natural variation in sex chromosome drive enables the discovery of a neo-X meiotic drive gene family

**Presenter:** Jeffrey Vedanayagam, University Of Texas At San Antonio

**Abstract:**

During gametogenesis, chromosomes segregate equally, ensuring that gametes retain only a single copy of each chromosome. However, selfish genetic elements such as meiotic drive genes frequently ‘cheat’ equal segregation to propagate their own transmission to the gametes. When meiotic drivers occur on sex chromosomes in males, they cause unequal transmission of X and Y chromosomes, resulting in a skewed sex ratio among offspring—a phenomenon known as sex chromosome drive. Only a few meiotic drivers have been identified at the gene level so far, and none have had their molecular mechanisms fully elucidated. Here, we study sex chromosome drive in hybrids between D. albomicans and D. nasuta—two relatively young Drosophila species in the immigrans group. Building on the prior quantitative trait loci (QTL) map of meiotic drive regions on the neo-X chromosome in D. albomicans, we integrated single-molecule sequencing and testis transcriptomics from multiple D. albomicans strains that exhibit near-complete sex chromosome drive to rescue of drive (50:50 male-to-female ratio) to uncover putative meiotic drive genes within the QTL windows. Our comparative transcriptomic analyses of testes from the drive and non-drive strains identified a recently expanded zinc-finger domain-containing gene family linked to the drive phenotype in D. albomicans. Currently, we are seeking to validate our candidate driver in additional drive and non-drive strains and to elucidate the mechanisms by which the D. albomicans sex-chromosome driver causes sex-ratio distortion and/or hybrid sterility phenotypes.
